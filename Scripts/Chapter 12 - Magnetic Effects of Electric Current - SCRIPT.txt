  - Electromagnet is temporary — it works only while current flows
  - Earthing and fuse are both safety devices — know the difference between them
  - Pronunciations in Punjabi help bilingual learners anchor the terms

    Electric Motor / ਬਿਜਲਈ ਮੋਟਰ (Electrical → mechanical)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Excellent. We have six more important terms on this slide.

  Magnetic Field — in Punjabi ਚੁੰਬਕੀ ਖੇਤਰ. It is the invisible region around a magnet or a current-carrying wire where the magnetic force can be felt. You cannot see it, but a compass needle will turn if you bring it into this region.

  Field Lines — in Punjabi ਬਲ ਰੇਖਾਵਾਂ. These are imaginary lines drawn to show the direction and strength of a magnetic field. Where lines are close together, the field is strong. Where they spread apart, the field is weak.

  Electric Generator — in Punjabi ਬਿਜਲਈ ਜਨਰੇਟਰ. This is the opposite of a motor. Mechanical energy goes IN, electrical energy comes OUT. Power stations are essentially giant generators.

  Live Wire — in Punjabi ਲਾਈਵ ਤਾਰ. This is the dangerous wire — red or brown in colour — that actually carries current to your appliances. Never touch this wire without switching off the main supply.

  Notice the beautiful symmetry: motor converts electrical to mechanical; generator converts mechanical to electrical. Keep this pair in mind — it will appear in your board exam.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Motor: Electrical → Mechanical; Generator: Mechanical → Electrical
  - Induction (ਪ੍ਰੇਰਣਾ) = current produced by a changing magnetic field
  - Live wire is red/brown and carries current — dangerous
  - Field lines (ਬਲ ਰੇਖਾਵਾਂ) show direction and strength of the magnetic field

  - This proved that electricity produces magnetism — the foundation of this chapter
  - Real-world applications: motors, generators, electromagnets all stem from this discovery
  - The effect is reversible — no current, no deflection

SLIDE 5: Magnetic Field & Field Lines
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition of magnetic field
  - Four properties of magnetic field lines:
    1. Closed, continuous curves
    2. Never intersect each other
    3. Crowded lines = stronger field
    4. Direction = way the north pole of a compass points

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us understand magnetic fields and field lines properly, because these properties appear in the board exam almost every year.

  A magnetic field — ਚੁੰਬਕੀ ਖੇਤਰ — is the region around a magnet where its force can be experienced. If you place a small compass anywhere inside this region, the needle will align itself along the field direction.

  To help us visualise this invisible field, scientists use imaginary lines called magnetic field lines or ਬਲ ਰੇਖਾਵਾਂ. There are four key properties you must know.

  Property one: Field lines are closed, continuous curves. They form complete loops — they never simply start and stop in mid-air.

  Property two: Field lines never intersect each other. This is extremely important. If two lines crossed, it would mean the magnetic force at that point has two different directions simultaneously — which is physically impossible.

  Property three: The closer the lines are to each other, the stronger the magnetic field at that point. At the poles of a magnet, the lines are very crowded — the field is strongest there. Far away from the magnet, the lines spread out — the field is weaker.

  Property four: The direction of a field line is defined as the direction in which the north pole of a small compass needle would point if placed at that location.

  Think of field lines like the contour lines on a map — they tell you not just where the field exists, but how strong it is and which way it points.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Field lines are closed and continuous — always form complete loops
  - Field lines NEVER cross each other
  - Closer lines = stronger field; widely spaced lines = weaker field
  - Direction is from North pole to South pole outside the magnet

BOARD TIP: "Field lines never intersect" is a very frequent 1-mark or reason-based question — always explain WHY (two directions at one point is impossible).

SLIDE 6: Field Lines of a Bar Magnet
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Diagram/description of field lines around a bar magnet
  - Outside: lines run from North pole to South pole
  - Inside: lines run from South to North (completing closed loops)
  - Lines most crowded at the poles → strongest field there

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us look at the actual pattern of magnetic field lines around a bar magnet — this is what appears in textbook diagrams and in exam questions.

  Outside the magnet, the field lines emerge from the North pole, arc through the surrounding space, and enter the South pole. So outside, the direction is always from North to South.

  Inside the magnet, the lines continue from the South pole back to the North pole — this is what makes the loops closed and continuous. So inside the magnet, the direction is from South to North.

  If you trace a single field line, it makes a complete oval loop: coming out of the North pole, curving around the outside, entering the South pole, travelling through the magnet from South to North, and emerging from the North pole again. It is a perfectly closed circuit — just like current flowing in a loop.

  Now look at the poles in your mind. At the North and South poles, many field lines crowd close together in a small area. That crowding tells you the field is very strong there. Move to the middle of the magnet — the lines are more spread out — the field is weaker there.

  This diagram is something you should be able to draw from memory. Practice drawing it: a rectangular bar, the word NORTH on the left end, SOUTH on the right end, and curved lines arcing from N to S outside, and a straight arrow inside going from S to N.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Outside bar magnet: field lines go North → South
  - Inside bar magnet: field lines go South → North (completing closed loops)
  - Poles have the strongest field (lines most crowded)
  - Be able to draw and label a bar magnet field line diagram

BOARD TIP: Drawing the field lines diagram is a common 3-mark question — label N, S poles and show at least 3–4 closed loops with correct arrows.

SLIDE 7: Oersted's Experiment
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Statement of Oersted's discovery: compass needle deflects near a current-carrying wire
  - Sub-heading "Properties of Field Lines (3-Mark Rule)" with three bullets:
    1. Field lines emerge from North, merge at South (outside)
    2. Inside magnet, direction is South to North (closed loops)
    3. Relative strength shown by closeness of lines

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide consolidates Oersted's experiment and the three field-line properties that are almost always asked together for a 3-mark question. Let me teach both clearly.

  Hans Christian Oersted's experiment was simple but revolutionary. He placed a compass needle near a straight conducting wire. When no current flowed, the needle pointed north as usual. The moment he switched on the current, the needle deflected — it turned sideways. When he reversed the direction of the current, the needle deflected the other way. When he switched the current off, the needle returned to north.

  The conclusion: a current-carrying conductor produces a magnetic field around it. The direction of this field depends on the direction of the current. This was the first experimental proof that electricity and magnetism are related.

  Now, the three field-line properties labelled "3-Mark Rule" — memorise all three together as a set:

  One — Field lines emerge from the North pole and merge at the South pole when you are looking outside the magnet. They leave North, they enter South.

  Two — Inside the magnet, the direction is reversed: from South to North. This keeps the lines as closed continuous loops.

  Three — The relative strength of the magnetic field is shown by how close the field lines are to each other. Close lines mean a strong field; widely spaced lines mean a weak field.

  If an exam question says "State three properties of magnetic field lines" — give exactly these three, clearly and in order.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Oersted: current in wire → deflection of compass → magnetic field around the wire
  - Reversing current reverses the deflection direction
  - 3-mark field line properties: North→South outside; South→North inside; closeness = strength
  - Oersted's experiment is the foundation of the entire chapter

BOARD TIP: "Describe Oersted's experiment" is a very frequent 3-mark question. Include: setup (compass near wire), observation (needle deflects), conclusion (current produces magnetic field), and effect of reversing current.

SLIDE 8: Spot-the-Error Inspector (ਗਲਤੀ ਲੱਭੋ) — Interactive Activity
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive activity: a diagram showing two magnetic field lines that intersect
  - Students must identify and tap the error
  - Corrected explanation shown after: "Magnetic field lines CANNOT intersect."

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is a quick activity to test whether you have truly understood the rule about field lines. I want you to look at the diagram on screen.

  You will see two field lines — let us call them Line A and Line B. Line B has been drawn so that it crosses Line A. This is the error you need to find.

  Why is this wrong? Here is the physics reasoning you must be able to state in an exam:

  A magnetic field line represents the direction of the magnetic force at every point along it. If two lines were to cross at a single point, it would mean that the magnetic force at that point has two different directions at the same time. That is physically impossible — a force can only point in one direction at any given point.

  Therefore, magnetic field lines can NEVER intersect, cross, or touch each other.

  - Field lines NEVER intersect — this is an absolute rule
  - Reason: at a crossing point, force would have two directions simultaneously — impossible
  - This is often tested as a "give reason" question in board exams

BOARD TIP: When asked "Why can't field lines intersect?", always include the phrase "…because at the point of intersection, the compass needle would have to point in two directions simultaneously, which is impossible."

SLIDE 9: Field Patterns & Hand Rules
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two sub-sections:
    Straight Wire: field forms concentric circles; Right-Hand Thumb Rule
    Circular Loop: field lines become straight and parallel at the centre

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we move to one of the most important practical sections: how current in different shapes of wire produces different magnetic field patterns, and how we determine the direction of those fields using hand rules.

  Let us start with a straight wire. When current flows through a straight conducting wire, the magnetic field lines form concentric circles — perfectly round rings that are centred on the wire. Imagine looking at the wire end-on, and seeing rings spreading outward like ripples in water from a stone dropped in a pond. The rings closest to the wire are small and crowded — strong field. The rings farther out are larger and spread apart — weaker field.

  To find which direction those circles go, we use the Right-Hand Thumb Rule. Here is how to do it: Take your right hand. Point your thumb in the direction of the conventional current — that is, the direction positive charge flows. Now curl your four fingers around the wire. The direction in which your fingers curl is the direction of the magnetic field around the wire. If the current flows upward, your fingers curl anticlockwise when viewed from above.

  Now for a circular loop of wire. When current flows in a circular loop, the field inside the loop is different from a straight wire. At the very centre of the loop, all the small circular field contributions from every part of the wire add up in the same direction. So the field lines at the centre become nearly straight and parallel — similar to the inside of a solenoid. The centre of the loop behaves like a tiny bar magnet.

  One face of the loop acts as a North pole and the other as a South pole — we will learn the Clock-Face Rule for this on the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Straight wire → concentric circular field lines
  - Right-Hand Thumb Rule: thumb = current direction, curled fingers = field direction
  - Circular loop → field lines straight and parallel at the centre
  - The centre of a current loop has a uniform, perpendicular field

BOARD TIP: Right-Hand Thumb Rule is a guaranteed 2-mark question — be able to state it AND draw the setup with arrows.

SLIDE 10: Field due to a Straight Conductor
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - A straight current-carrying conductor produces concentric circular field lines
  - Three factors:
    1. Field strength increases with more current
    2. Field strength decreases with distance from wire
    3. Direction found using Right-Hand Thumb Rule

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us go deeper into the magnetic field around a straight conductor. We already know the shape — concentric circles. Now let us understand what controls the strength and direction of this field.

  Factor one: the current. If you increase the current flowing through the wire — for example, by using a stronger battery — the magnetic field gets stronger. More current means more magnetic effect. This is a direct relationship.

  Factor two: the distance. As you move farther away from the wire, the field becomes weaker. The field circles spread out as you go farther from the wire, just like the ripples from a stone in water weaken as they travel outward. So the field strength is inversely related to the distance from the wire.

  Factor three: the direction. We use the Right-Hand Thumb Rule to find the direction. Let me describe it again very clearly for this context. Hold your right hand with the thumb pointing upward — this represents current flowing upward in the wire. Your four fingers, curled around the imaginary wire, will curl in the anticlockwise direction when viewed from the top. That anticlockwise curl is the direction of the field lines around the wire. If you reverse the current — thumb pointing downward — the fingers curl clockwise. The current direction controls the field direction.

  This is also why Oersted saw the compass needle deflect in opposite directions when he reversed the current — the magnetic field itself reversed direction.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Field strength ∝ current (more current = stronger field)
  - Field strength decreases as distance from wire increases
  - Right-Hand Thumb Rule gives the direction of the circular field
  - Reversing current reverses field direction

BOARD TIP: A common question: "What happens to the magnetic field if the current is doubled?" Answer: the field strength doubles. Strength is directly proportional to current.

SLIDE 11: Field due to a Circular Loop
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Every part of the loop contributes to the field at the centre in the same direction
  - Field lines at the centre: nearly straight and perpendicular to the plane of the loop
  - Clock-Face Rule: anticlockwise current = North pole; clockwise current = South pole

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  When a wire is bent into a circular loop and current flows through it, the magnetic field at the centre of that loop is very special.

  Think of it this way. Every small segment of the circular wire is itself a tiny straight wire, and each tiny straight wire produces a small curved magnetic field. Now, because the wire curves all the way around in a circle, every one of those tiny field contributions at the centre happens to point in the same direction — they all add up, rather than cancelling out. The result is that the field at the very centre of the loop is strong, and the field lines there are nearly straight lines, running perpendicular to the plane of the loop.

  This is important: the number of turns matters. If you have a coil with ten loops instead of one, ten times more field is produced at the centre. This is the key idea behind the solenoid.

  Now, the Clock-Face Rule. This tells you which face of the circular loop acts as the North pole and which as the South pole.

  Look at the face of the loop from the front. If the current flows anticlockwise — like the hands of a clock going backwards — that face acts as a North pole. Think of it as "N for anticlockwise" — or a mnemonic: Anticlockwise = North.

  If the current flows clockwise — like normal clock hands — that face acts as a South pole. Clockwise = South.

  This makes sense: the loop behaves exactly like a small bar magnet, with one face as North and the other as South.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - At the centre of a loop, field lines are straight and perpendicular to the loop's plane
  - More turns of wire = stronger field at the centre
  - Clock-Face Rule: Anticlockwise current = North pole; Clockwise current = South pole
  - A current loop behaves like a flat bar magnet

BOARD TIP: The Clock-Face Rule is tested frequently. Draw a circle, mark anticlockwise arrows, and label it "North face." Draw another with clockwise arrows labelled "South face."

SLIDE 12: Solenoids & Electromagnets
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition of solenoid: many circular turns of insulated copper wire
  - Inside solenoid: field lines are parallel straight lines → uniform field
  - Electromagnet: place a soft iron core inside the coil → powerful temporary magnet

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A solenoid — ਸੋਲੇਨੋਇਡ — is one of the most important devices in this chapter. Let me describe it carefully.

  A solenoid is made by winding insulated copper wire into many, many circular turns, one after another, along the same axis. The turns are tightly wound and close together. From the outside it looks like a coil, or a wire spring.

  Now here is what makes it special. When current flows through the solenoid, every individual turn of wire acts like a circular loop and produces its own small magnetic field. Because all the turns are wound in the same direction and carry current in the same direction, all their fields add up together. The result inside the solenoid is remarkable: the field lines are perfectly straight and parallel to the axis of the coil. This means the magnetic field inside a solenoid is uniform — same strength and same direction everywhere inside. This is the same property that a perfectly uniform bar magnet would have inside it.

  In fact, the solenoid behaves exactly like a bar magnet from the outside too. One end is the North pole, the other end is the South pole, and the field lines emerge from North, arc around the outside, and re-enter at South — forming closed loops.

  Now, if you want to make this field even stronger, you place a piece of soft iron inside the coil. The solenoid's field magnetises the soft iron, and the iron's own magnetic field adds to the solenoid's field. The result is a very powerful magnet — called an electromagnet (ਬਿਜਲਈ ਚੁੰਬਕ). The beauty is that the moment you switch off the current, the soft iron loses its magnetism. The electromagnet can be turned on and off at will. This makes it perfect for devices like electromagnetic cranes, electric bells, and MRI machines.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Solenoid = tightly wound coil of many turns of insulated copper wire
  - Inside solenoid: field is uniform (parallel straight lines)
  - Solenoid ends behave like N and S poles of a bar magnet
  - Soft iron core + solenoid = electromagnet (temporary, controllable, powerful)

BOARD TIP: "Why is the field inside a solenoid said to be uniform?" is a 2-mark question. Answer: the field lines are parallel and equidistant, meaning the field has the same strength and direction at every point inside.

SLIDE 13: Force on a Conductor & Fleming's Left-Hand Rule
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - A current-carrying wire in an external magnetic field experiences a physical force
  - This force is the basis of electric motors
  - Warning: force is maximum when wire ⊥ field (90°); force is zero when wire ∥ field
  - Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Forefinger = Field, Centre finger = Current, Thumb = Thrust/Force

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide introduces one of the most important board exam topics: the force on a current-carrying conductor in a magnetic field, and Fleming's Left-Hand Rule.

  When a wire carrying current is placed inside an external magnetic field, the wire experiences a physical mechanical force — it actually gets pushed. This pushing force is what makes electric motors spin. So the principle here is: magnetic field plus current equals force.

  Before we learn the rule, note this critical warning. The force is maximum when the wire is placed perpendicular to the magnetic field — at 90 degrees. If you tilt the wire and make it parallel to the field — at 0 degrees — the force becomes zero. There is no force at all on a wire running parallel to the field. This is a very common board exam trap. Remember: perpendicular = maximum force; parallel = zero force.

  Now, Fleming's Left-Hand Rule tells us the direction of this force.

  Here is how to use it: Take your LEFT hand. Stretch out your thumb, your forefinger, and your middle finger so that all three are at right angles to each other — each one pointing in a different direction, like the three edges of a corner of a box.

  Now assign: the Forefinger points in the direction of the magnetic Field. The middle Finger points in the direction of the Current. The Thumb points in the direction of the Thrust — the force or motion of the wire.

  A simple memory trick: FBI — Forefinger = Field, B = well, we use the middle finger for current (I), and Thumb = motion. Or remember: "Father, Child, Move" — Forefinger=Field, Centre=Current, Thumb=Motion.

  This rule is used whenever you need to find the direction of force on a conductor — in other words, whenever you are dealing with an electric motor.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Current in external magnetic field → force on the wire (basis of electric motors)
  - Force maximum at 90° (perpendicular); Force = zero at 0° (parallel)
  - Fleming's Left-Hand Rule: Forefinger = Field, Middle finger = Current, Thumb = Thrust
  - Left-Hand Rule is always for motors (finding force/motion)

BOARD TIP: This is a guaranteed 3-mark question: "State Fleming's Left-Hand Rule and draw a diagram." Practice pointing your LEFT hand with all three fingers extended mutually at right angles.

SLIDE 14: Electromagnet vs Permanent Magnet
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table:
    | Feature       | Electromagnet              | Permanent Magnet |
    | Magnetism     | Temporary (needs current)  | Permanent        |
    | Strength      | Strong & adjustable        | Fixed            |
    | Core used     | Soft iron                  | Steel            |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Students, this comparison table is a classic board exam question — typically asked for 3 marks. Let me walk through each row carefully so you understand not just what to write, but why.

  Row one: Magnetism. An electromagnet's magnetism is temporary. It exists only as long as current flows through the coil. Switch off the current and the magnetism vanishes. A permanent magnet, on the other hand, retains its magnetism without any power supply — it is always on.

  Row two: Strength. An electromagnet can be made very strong — much stronger than most permanent magnets — by increasing the current or adding more turns to the coil. Crucially, its strength can be adjusted. A permanent magnet has a fixed strength that you cannot change.

  Row three: The core. An electromagnet uses a soft iron core. Why soft iron? Because soft iron is easily magnetised and easily demagnetised. When you switch on the current, it quickly becomes a strong magnet. When you switch off the current, it quickly loses its magnetism. Steel, by contrast, is hard to magnetise but once magnetised, it stays magnetised — that is why steel is used for permanent magnets.

  So in summary: electromagnets are temporary, adjustable, and use soft iron. Permanent magnets are always on, fixed in strength, and made with steel.

  Real-life usage: Electromagnets are used in electric bells, cranes, speakers, and MRI machines where you need controllable magnetism. Permanent magnets are used in compasses, fridge magnets, and loudspeaker drivers where constant magnetism is needed without power.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Electromagnet: temporary, adjustable strength, soft iron core
  - Permanent magnet: always magnetised, fixed strength, steel core
  - Soft iron is easy to magnetise AND demagnetise (ideal for electromagnets)
  - Steel is hard to demagnetise (ideal for permanent magnets)

BOARD TIP: This comparison table is asked almost every year for 3 marks. Write all three rows with both columns clearly filled in — partial answers lose marks.

SLIDE 15: Electric Motor
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: converts electrical energy → mechanical (rotational) energy
  - Table of parts and functions:
    | Part                     | Function                                                      |
    | Armature coil            | Rotates inside the magnetic field                             |
    | Split-ring commutator    | Reverses current every half turn to keep rotation continuous  |
    | Brushes                  | Carry current between the coil and the battery               |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The electric motor is one of the most important applications in this chapter. Let us understand exactly how it works and what each part does.

  An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy — specifically rotational mechanical energy. The motor takes current from a power supply and uses it to make a coil rotate. That rotation can then drive a fan blade, pump water, or spin a machine.

  The key principle: a current-carrying coil placed in an external magnetic field experiences forces. As we learned from Fleming's Left-Hand Rule, the two sides of the coil feel forces in opposite directions, which creates a turning effect — a torque — that rotates the coil.

  Now the three main parts:

  The Armature Coil: This is the rectangular coil of wire that sits between the poles of a magnet. The current flows through this coil, and the magnetic force acts on it, causing it to rotate. This is the "rotor" — the part that actually moves.

  The Split-Ring Commutator: This is the clever part that solves a big problem. Without it, the coil would rotate half a turn and then stop — because the force direction would reverse and push it back. The split-ring commutator is a ring divided into two halves, connected to the two ends of the coil. Every half turn, the connection swaps — so the current direction in the coil reverses every half turn. This keeps the force always pushing in the same rotational direction, so the coil keeps spinning continuously in one direction.

  The Brushes: These are fixed pieces of conducting material — often carbon — that press against the split ring and maintain electrical contact between the rotating coil and the stationary external circuit (the battery). They allow current to flow into and out of the rotating coil without any wires getting tangled.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Electric motor: Electrical energy → Mechanical (rotational) energy
  - Armature coil rotates in the magnetic field
  - Split-ring commutator reverses current every half turn → keeps rotation continuous
  - Brushes maintain electrical contact between rotating coil and external circuit

BOARD TIP: "What is the function of the split-ring commutator?" is a very common 2-mark question. Answer: it reverses the direction of current in the armature coil every half rotation, ensuring the coil keeps rotating in the same direction continuously.

SLIDE 16: Electromagnetic Induction
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Faraday's discovery: changing magnetic field through a coil induces a current
  - Moving magnet into or out of a coil deflects a galvanometer
  - Faster movement → larger induced current
  - Fleming's Right-Hand Rule: Thumb = motion, Forefinger = field, Middle finger = induced current

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Electromagnetic induction is one of the greatest discoveries in the history of science. It was discovered by Michael Faraday in 1831, and it is the principle that makes electric generators — and therefore all the electricity in our homes — possible.

  The key idea is this: a changing magnetic field through a coil of wire induces — ਪ੍ਰੇਰਿਤ ਕਰਦਾ ਹੈ — that is, produces a current in the coil, even without any battery. You do not need to connect any external power source. The changing magnetism alone creates the current.

  Faraday demonstrated this with a simple experiment. He took a coil connected to a galvanometer — a sensitive current detector. When he pushed a magnet into the coil, the galvanometer needle deflected — current was flowing. When the magnet was still inside the coil and not moving, the needle returned to zero — no current. When he pulled the magnet out, the needle deflected again, but in the opposite direction. The current only flows when the magnetic field is changing.

  Two important results: If you move the magnet faster, the induced current is larger — the galvanometer deflects more. If you use a stronger magnet, the induced current is also larger. Both speed and field strength affect the magnitude of the induced current.

  Now, to find the direction of the induced current, we use Fleming's Right-Hand Rule. Take your RIGHT hand — note, this time it is the right hand, unlike the left hand for motors. Stretch the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger mutually at right angles. The Thumb points in the direction of motion of the conductor. The Forefinger points in the direction of the magnetic Field. The Middle finger points in the direction of the induced Current. This rule is used in generators.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Electromagnetic induction: changing magnetic field through a coil → induced current
  - Current flows only when the field is changing (moving magnet); stationary magnet = no current
  - Faster motion or stronger magnet → larger induced current
  - Fleming's Right-Hand Rule (for generators): Thumb = Motion, Forefinger = Field, Middle = induced Current

BOARD TIP: Be clear on who discovered what: Oersted discovered current produces magnetism; Faraday discovered changing magnetism produces current. Mixing these up is a common exam mistake.

SLIDE 17: Fleming's Left-Hand vs Right-Hand Rule
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table:
    | Feature     | Left-Hand Rule             | Right-Hand Rule                  |
    | Used in     | Electric motor             | Electric generator               |
    | Finds       | Direction of force/motion  | Direction of induced current     |
    | You are given | Current & field          | Motion & field                   |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Students, this comparison is one of the most commonly tested distinctions in this chapter. Many students mix up which hand to use when. Let me make this crystal clear.

  Both rules use the same physical setup: thumb, forefinger, and middle finger stretched mutually at right angles. The difference is which hand, what each finger represents, and when you apply it.

  Left-Hand Rule: This is for the electric motor. You use it when current is already flowing in a wire and you want to find the direction of the force or motion produced. So you are given: the direction of the current and the direction of the magnetic field. What you find: the direction of the mechanical force.

  Right-Hand Rule: This is for the electric generator. You use it when a conductor is moving in a magnetic field and you want to find the direction of the induced current. So you are given: the direction of motion and the direction of the magnetic field. What you find: the direction of the induced current.

  Here is a brilliant way to remember: L for Left, L for (motor that produces) Load movement. R for Right, R for (generator that produces) Result — the induced current.

  Another memory hook: Left = motor = Left hand = you use your muscles to work (motor does mechanical work). Right = generator = Right hand = you are generating something new (a current).

  Read the table on screen one more time and commit the three rows to memory. In the exam, if you state the wrong hand, you lose all the marks for that question.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Left-Hand Rule → Electric Motor → finds force/motion direction
  - Right-Hand Rule → Electric Generator → finds induced current direction
  - Left-Hand: given Current & Field; Right-Hand: given Motion & Field
  - Never mix up the hands — it is a very common and costly mistake

BOARD TIP: Memory trick: "Motor = Left hand = work is done ON the conductor; Generator = Right hand = work is done BY the conductor to generate current."

SLIDE 18: Electric Generator (Dynamo)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: converts mechanical energy → electrical energy via electromagnetic induction
  - Comparison table:
    | Feature     | AC Generator        | DC Generator              |
    | Rings used  | Two slip rings      | Split-ring commutator     |
    | Output      | Alternating current | Direct current            |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The electric generator is the opposite of the motor: where the motor converts electrical energy to mechanical energy, the generator converts mechanical energy to electrical energy. It is the device that produces the electricity used in our homes.

  The working principle is electromagnetic induction. A coil of wire is rotated inside a magnetic field. As the coil rotates, the magnetic flux through it keeps changing — this changing flux induces a current in the coil. The mechanical work of rotating the coil is being converted into electrical energy.

  Now, there are two types of generators, and the difference lies in how the electrical output is taken from the rotating coil.

  An AC Generator uses two slip rings — one connected to each end of the coil. As the coil rotates, each brush always contacts the same slip ring, so the output current direction follows the rotation of the coil — it alternates, flowing in one direction during the first half turn and the opposite direction during the second half turn. This gives Alternating Current — AC.

  A DC Generator uses a split-ring commutator — the same device used in the DC motor. As the coil rotates, the commutator swaps the connections every half turn, ensuring the current always flows in the same direction in the external circuit. This gives Direct Current — DC.

  Most power stations use AC generators. The electricity that comes to your home through the mains supply is AC at 220 volts and 50 Hz frequency.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Generator: Mechanical energy → Electrical energy (opposite of motor)
  - Principle: electromagnetic induction (rotating coil in magnetic field)
  - AC generator: two slip rings → alternating current output
  - DC generator: split-ring commutator → direct current output

BOARD TIP: The difference between slip rings and split-ring commutator is a guaranteed question. Slip rings → AC; Split-ring commutator → DC. This appears in both motors and generators.

SLIDE 20: AC vs DC — The Difference
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table:
    | Feature    | AC                                    | DC                       |
    | Direction  | Reverses periodically                 | One direction only       |
    | In India   | Changes 50 times/second (50 Hz)       | Constant                 |
    | Source     | Generators, mains supply              | Cells, batteries         |
  - Explanation: AC is used for long-distance transmission because voltage can be changed using transformers

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide explains the fundamental difference between two types of electric current: Alternating Current (AC) and Direct Current (DC).

  Direct Current — DC — flows in one direction only and stays constant. The current from a battery or a cell is DC. The electrons flow continuously from the negative terminal to the positive terminal around the circuit. The graph of DC against time is a flat horizontal line.

  Alternating Current — AC — reverses its direction periodically. The current flows in one direction, then reverses and flows in the other direction, then reverses again, and so on. In India, the mains supply AC reverses direction 50 times per second — that is what we mean by 50 Hz (50 hertz). The graph of AC against time is a wave — a sine wave that goes up, comes down, crosses zero, goes negative, and comes back up, repeatedly.

  Sources: AC is produced by generators and is what comes out of the wall sockets in your home. DC is produced by cells and batteries.

  Now, a very important question: why do we use AC for the domestic supply rather than DC? The answer is transmission efficiency. AC voltage can be very easily increased (stepped up) or decreased (stepped down) using a device called a transformer. By stepping up the voltage to very high levels, the current becomes very small, which means very little energy is lost as heat in the transmission wires over long distances. This makes AC much more economical than DC for sending power from a power station to your home.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - DC: constant, one direction — from batteries/cells
  - AC: periodically reverses direction — from generators/mains
  - Indian mains supply: AC at 220 V, 50 Hz
  - AC is preferred for long-distance power transmission because transformers can change its voltage

BOARD TIP: "Why is AC preferred over DC for long-distance transmission?" is a frequent 2-mark question. Answer must mention: transformers can change AC voltage; higher voltage means lower current; lower current means less energy loss (heating) in wires.

SLIDE 21: Domestic Safety Hazards (ਘਰੇਲੂ ਸੁਰੱਖਿਆ ਖ਼ਤਰੇ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Home receives AC at 220 V
  - Comparison table of two hazards:
    | Short Circuiting (ਸ਼ਾਰਟ ਸਰਕਟ)                               | Overloading (ਓਵਰਲੋਡਿੰਗ)                                         |
    | Live wire touches Neutral wire directly (insulation melted)  | Too many high-power appliances on one socket                     |
    | Resistance ≈ 0; massive immediate current spike              | Total current exceeds wire capacity; wires overheat slowly       |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we come to domestic electrical safety — a very practical and important topic that is also well-tested in board exams.

  Your home's mains supply is Alternating Current at 220 volts. This is powerful enough to cause serious injury or death if something goes wrong. There are two main electrical hazards you must understand: Short Circuiting and Overloading.

  Short Circuiting — ਸ਼ਾਰਟ ਸਰਕਟ. This happens when the Live wire and the Neutral wire come into direct contact with each other, without any resistance in between. How does this happen? The plastic insulation around a wire can melt due to heat — perhaps from a previous overload — or it can be damaged mechanically. When the insulation is gone, the bare live wire can touch the bare neutral wire. The result is catastrophic: the resistance of the circuit drops almost to zero. From Ohm's Law, if resistance approaches zero, the current becomes enormous — a massive spike in a fraction of a second. This extreme current can instantly start a fire, melt wires, and damage appliances.

  Overloading — ਓਵਰਲੋਡਿੰਗ. This is a more gradual problem. It happens when you connect too many high-power appliances — like a heater, air conditioner, microwave, and iron — all to the same circuit or socket. Each appliance draws current. When too many appliances are on simultaneously, the total current drawn exceeds the safe capacity of the household wiring. The wires cannot carry so much current and begin to heat up — not as explosively as a short circuit, but slowly and dangerously. Over time, this overheating can melt insulation and eventually cause a fire.

  Understanding these two hazards helps you understand why we need fuses and earthing, which we cover on the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Home supply: AC at 220 V
  - Short circuit: live and neutral wires touch → resistance ≈ 0 → massive current spike → fire hazard
  - Overloading: too many appliances → total current exceeds wire capacity → wires overheat gradually
  - Short circuit is sudden; overloading is gradual — both are dangerous

BOARD TIP: Students often confuse short circuit and overloading. Remember: Short circuit = low resistance problem; Overloading = high total current problem. A 3-mark question may ask you to compare both — include cause, effect, and how it differs from the other.

SLIDE 22: Domestic Electric Circuit
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Power reaches homes through three wires:
    | Wire    | Insulation colour  | Role                          |
    | Live    | Red / Brown        | Carries current (high potential)|
    | Neutral | Black / Blue       | Return path for current       |
    | Earth   | Green              | Safety — connects to ground   |

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Every house is connected to the electricity supply through three distinct wires, each with a specific colour and a specific purpose. You must be able to state all three from memory — this is a standard board exam question.

  The Live Wire — ਲਾਈਵ ਤਾਰ — is coloured Red or Brown. This is the dangerous wire. It is at a high electrical potential and it carries the current from the power supply to your appliances. Under no circumstances should you touch this wire without switching off the main circuit breaker. In the circuit, the fuse is always connected in series in the live wire — this is critical and we will revisit it on the next slide.

  The Neutral Wire — is coloured Black or Blue. This wire is at zero potential, or close to it. It provides the return path for the current — the current flows out of the supply through the live wire, through the appliance, and returns to the supply through the neutral wire. By itself, the neutral wire is relatively safe, but it should still not be handled carelessly.

  The Earth Wire — is coloured Green. This is the safety wire. It connects the metal bodies or casings of electrical appliances to the actual earth — the ground outside. Under normal operation, no current flows through the earth wire. It only carries current if something goes wrong — if there is a fault and the metal casing becomes live. The earth wire provides a low-resistance path for fault current to flow safely into the ground, preventing you from getting an electric shock when you touch the appliance.

  Memorise: Red/Brown = Live (dangerous). Black/Blue = Neutral (return path). Green = Earth (safety).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Live wire: Red/Brown — carries current at high potential — dangerous
  - Neutral wire: Black/Blue — return path at zero potential
  - Earth wire: Green — safety wire, directs fault current to ground
  - These three wire colours must be memorised for the board exam

BOARD TIP: "State the colour and function of each wire in domestic wiring" is a guaranteed 3-mark question — one mark per wire; include both colour AND function for each.

SLIDE 23: Earthing & the Fuse
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Earthing: metal body of appliance connected to earth wire → leaked current flows to ground → prevents electric shocks
  - Fuse: thin wire of low melting point in the live wire → melts when current exceeds safe value → breaks circuit

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide focuses on the two most important safety devices in your home's electrical system: earthing and the fuse.

  Let us begin with Earthing — ਅਰਥਿੰਗ. Imagine you have a washing machine. Its outer casing is metal. Now suppose there is a fault inside the machine — perhaps the live wire has come loose and is touching the metal casing. The casing is now live at 220 volts. If you reach out and touch the machine, current will flow from the casing, through your body, to the ground. This is an electric shock — potentially fatal.

  Earthing prevents this. The metal casing is connected by the green earth wire directly to the ground (to a buried metal plate or rod in the earth outside your house). If the casing becomes live due to a fault, the fault current now has two paths: through you, or through the earth wire. The earth wire has very low resistance — much lower than your body — so virtually all the fault current takes the earth wire path instead of going through you. The current safely drains into the ground and you are protected.

  Now the Fuse — ਫਿਊਜ਼. A fuse is a short piece of thin wire made from a material with a low melting point — typically an alloy of tin and lead. It is placed in series in the live wire. Under normal operation, the current through the fuse is within the safe limit, and the fuse wire stays intact.

  But if a short circuit occurs, the current suddenly shoots up to a dangerously high value. This massive current heats the fuse wire very rapidly. Because the fuse wire has a low melting point, it melts and physically breaks the circuit. The circuit is now open — no current can flow — and the appliance, the wiring, and anyone nearby is protected from the dangerous excess current.

  Remember: the fuse must always be placed in the live wire, not in the neutral wire. If placed in the neutral wire, the circuit would still be connected to the live potential even after the fuse blows — dangerous.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Earthing: connects appliance casing to ground → fault current flows to earth, not through you
  - Fuse: low-melting-point wire in the LIVE wire → melts on excess current → breaks circuit
  - Fuse is always in the live wire (never in neutral or earth)
  - Both earthing and fuse work together — earthing protects you from shock; fuse protects wiring from fire

BOARD TIP: "Why is the fuse always connected in the live wire?" is a 2-mark reason question — answer: to completely isolate the appliance from the high-potential supply when the fuse blows; placing it in the neutral wire would leave the appliance still connected to the live (dangerous) wire.

SLIDE 25: How an Electric Motor Rotates — Step-by-Step (Activity)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four-step animated/reveal sequence:
    Step 1: Current flows through the armature coil in a magnetic field
    Step 2: Two sides of coil feel opposite forces (Fleming's LHR) → coil rotates
    Step 3: After half a turn, the split-ring commutator reverses the current
    Step 4: Force direction reverses → coil keeps spinning continuously in same direction

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us trace through the operation of an electric motor step by step. This step-by-step breakdown is exactly what examiners want when they ask "Explain the working of an electric motor."

  Step one: Current from the battery flows into the armature coil through the brushes and the split-ring commutator. The coil is placed between the North and South poles of a permanent magnet, so it is in an external magnetic field.

  Step two: Now apply Fleming's Left-Hand Rule to each side of the coil. The two sides of the coil carry current in opposite directions — current flows one way along the left side, and the opposite way along the right side. Applying the Left-Hand Rule to each side separately shows that the force on the left side is upward, and the force on the right side is downward — or vice versa, depending on the current direction. These two forces act in opposite directions on opposite sides of the coil, creating a turning effect — a torque. The coil begins to rotate.

  Step three: After the coil has rotated exactly half a turn, the two halves of the split-ring commutator swap their contact with the two brushes. This swapping automatically reverses the direction of the current flowing through the coil.

  Step four: Because the current in the coil reversed, the force on each side of the coil also reverses — but at the same moment the coil has flipped to the opposite position. The net result is that the torque always acts in the same rotational direction. The coil continues to spin in the same direction, round and round, for as long as current flows.

  This is how the beautiful continuous rotation of an electric motor is achieved. The split-ring commutator is the key clever component that makes continuous one-direction rotation possible.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Four steps: current flows → opposite forces on coil sides → coil rotates → commutator reverses current → keeps rotating
  - The split-ring commutator reverses current every half rotation to maintain continuous rotation
  - Without the commutator, the motor would only turn half a revolution and stop
  - All four steps together explain the "working of a DC motor"

BOARD TIP: "Explain the working of a DC motor" is a 5-mark question. Cover all four steps in order; draw a labelled diagram showing coil, magnets, commutator, and brushes for full marks.

      Thumb = Motion, Forefinger = Field, Middle finger = induced Current. Used in generators.
  - Footer: "Left-Hand → Motor (find the Force) · Right-Hand → Generator (find the induced Current)"

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Here are three flip cards — one for each hand rule. I want you to do something physical right now as we go through each one.

  Card one: Right-Hand Thumb Rule. In Punjabi: ਸੱਜੇ ਹੱਥ ਅੰਗੂਠਾ. Take your right hand. Extend your thumb straight upward — this represents the direction of current in a straight wire. Now curl your four fingers. The direction your fingers curl in is the direction of the magnetic field lines that circle around the wire. This rule is used only for a straight current-carrying conductor — to find the direction of the circular field around it.

  Card two: Fleming's Left-Hand Rule. In Punjabi: ਖੱਬੇ ਹੱਥ ਦਾ ਨਿਯਮ. Take your LEFT hand. Extend the thumb, forefinger, and middle finger so each points in a different direction — three mutually perpendicular directions. Forefinger = magnetic Field. Middle finger = Current. Thumb = the Thrust or Force on the conductor. This is the motor rule.

  Card three: Fleming's Right-Hand Rule. In Punjabi: ਸੱਜੇ ਹੱਥ ਦਾ ਨਿਯਮ. Take your RIGHT hand. Same finger configuration. Thumb = Motion of the conductor. Forefinger = magnetic Field. Middle finger = induced Current. This is the generator rule.

  Now do all three rules with your actual hands right now. Hold up your right hand for the Thumb Rule. Switch to your left hand for the motor. Switch to your right hand for the generator. Do this physical action — muscle memory will help you recall it in the exam room.

  Footer summary: Left Hand → Motor (find the Force). Right Hand → Generator (find the induced Current).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Right-Hand Thumb Rule: for circular field around straight wire
  - Fleming's Left-Hand: for motor force (thumb=force, fore=field, middle=current)
  - Fleming's Right-Hand: for generator's induced current (thumb=motion, fore=field, middle=current)
  - Physical practice with your hands is the best way to remember these rules

BOARD TIP: In the exam, if asked to demonstrate a rule, explicitly state which hand, then describe each finger and what it represents. Diagrams with labelled arrows earn full marks.

SLIDE 29: Numerical Practice — Force on a Conductor (ਸੰਖਿਆਤਮਕ ਅਭਿਆਸ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four numerical problems with formulas:
    Q1: Wire 0.5 m, current 4 A, field 0.2 T (perpendicular). Find force. F = BIL
    Q2: Same wire, current doubled to 8 A. Find new force. F ∝ I
    Q3: 2 m conductor, B = 0.1 T, F = 0.6 N. Find current I. I = F/(BL)
    Q4: Fuse, max 5 A on 220 V line. Find max power. P = V × I

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us practise numerical problems. These formula-based questions appear in the board exam for 2–3 marks. The good news is that there are only a few formulas to know, and the calculations are straightforward.

  The key formula for force on a conductor: F = B × I × L. That is: Force equals magnetic field strength times current times the length of the conductor in the field. This applies when the conductor is perpendicular to the field. Keep your notebook ready and solve each question before looking at the answer.

  Question 1: A wire of length 0.5 m carries a current of 4 A in a uniform magnetic field of 0.2 T, placed perpendicular to the field. Find the force on it.
  Solution: F = B × I × L = 0.2 × 4 × 0.5 = 0.4 N. Answer: 0.4 newtons.

  Question 2: In the same wire, the current is doubled to 8 A (B and L unchanged). Find the new force.
  Since F = BIL and B and L are constant, doubling I doubles F. New F = 0.2 × 8 × 0.5 = 0.8 N. Or simply: 0.4 N × 2 = 0.8 N. The force doubles. Answer: 0.8 N.

  Question 3: A 2 m conductor in a 0.1 T field experiences a force of 0.6 N. Find the current I.
  Rearranging F = BIL → I = F ÷ (B × L) = 0.6 ÷ (0.1 × 2) = 0.6 ÷ 0.2 = 3 A. Answer: 3 amperes.

  Question 4: An appliance fuse can carry a maximum of 5 A on a 220 V line. Find the maximum power it can handle.
  Formula: P = V × I = 220 × 5 = 1100 W = 1.1 kW. Answer: 1100 watts or 1.1 kilowatts.

  Formulas to memorise: F = BIL for force on a conductor; P = VI for electrical power.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - F = B × I × L (force on conductor perpendicular to field)
  - Doubling current doubles force (F ∝ I); doubling field doubles force (F ∝ B)
  - Rearrange F = BIL to find I = F/(BL) or B = F/(IL)
  - P = V × I for maximum power through a fuse

BOARD TIP: Always write the formula first, then substitute values with units, then calculate. Showing the formula earns 1 mark even if the arithmetic has an error.

SLIDE 30: True or False? (ਸਹੀ ਜਾਂ ਗਲਤ?) — Practice Activity
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Eight True/False questions (with Punjabi translations):
    Q1: Magnetic force is maximized when conductor ⊥ field → TRUE
    Q2: Clock Face rule: clockwise current = South pole → TRUE
    Q3: Inside a solenoid, magnetic field is uniform (parallel lines) → TRUE
    Q4: Overloading occurs when live and neutral wires touch directly → FALSE
    Q5: Magnetic field lines emerge from the South pole → FALSE
    Q6: Electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy → TRUE
    Q7: A DC generator uses two slip rings → FALSE
    Q8: Earthing protects a user from electric shocks → TRUE

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Eight True or False questions — a classic quick-review format. Let me go through each one and explain why, not just whether.

  Q1: "Magnetic force is maximized when conductor is perpendicular to the field." TRUE. At 90 degrees, F = BIL (maximum). At 0 degrees (parallel), F = 0.

  Q2: "Clock Face rule: clockwise current acts as a South pole." TRUE. Remember: Clockwise = South; Anticlockwise = North.

  Q3: "Inside a solenoid, the magnetic field is uniform." TRUE. The parallel straight field lines inside the solenoid indicate a uniform field — same strength and direction everywhere inside.

  Q4: "Overloading occurs when live and neutral wires touch directly." FALSE. This is the definition of a short circuit, not overloading. Overloading is when too many high-power appliances draw more total current than the circuit can safely handle.

  Q5: "Magnetic field lines emerge from the South pole." FALSE. Field lines emerge from the North pole and enter the South pole outside the magnet. Inside, they travel from South to North.

  Q6: "An electric motor converts electrical energy into mechanical energy." TRUE. This is the fundamental definition of an electric motor.

  Q7: "A DC generator uses two slip rings." FALSE. A DC generator uses a split-ring commutator. It is the AC generator that uses two slip rings.

  Q8: "Earthing protects a user from electric shocks." TRUE. The earth wire diverts fault current safely to the ground, preventing it from passing through a person.

  Tally your score. Any question you got wrong — go back to the corresponding slide immediately and re-read it.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Perpendicular = max force; parallel = zero force
  - Clockwise current face = South pole; Anticlockwise = North pole
  - Overloading ≠ short circuit (a very common confusion)
  - DC generator = split-ring commutator (NOT slip rings)
  - Field lines emerge from North pole (outside magnet)

BOARD TIP: Q4 and Q7 are the two classic "trap" questions in this chapter. Short circuit vs overloading, and slip rings vs split-ring commutator — know these distinctions cold.
