SLIDE 2: Key Vocabulary (ਮੁੱਖ ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Vocabulary table with 6 terms: Acid, Base, Salt, Indicator, Neutralization, Dilution
  - English term | Punjabi name | Brief meaning/definition for each

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we dive into the chemistry, let us make sure everyone knows the key words of this chapter.
  Think of this as your chapter dictionary.

  First — Acid (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ). Acids taste sour, like lemon or vinegar, and they give H⁺ ions when
  dissolved in water. This H⁺ ion is the reason they are acidic.

  Second — Base (ਖਾਰ). Bases taste bitter and feel soapy to touch. They give OH⁻ ions in water.
  Baking soda and lime water are common examples.

  Third — Salt (ਲੂਣ). A salt is the product formed when an acid and a base react with each other.
  Common table salt, sodium chloride, is the most familiar example.

  Fourth — Indicator (ਸੂਚਕ). An indicator is a substance that tells us whether a solution is acidic
  or basic by changing its color or smell. Litmus paper is the most well-known indicator.

  Fifth — Neutralization (ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ). This is the reaction where an acid and a base cancel each
  other out to form salt and water. The word ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ literally means "making neutral."

  Sixth — Dilution (ਪਤਲਾ ਕਰਨਾ). Dilution means adding water to an acid or base to reduce its
  concentration. We will see later that this must be done carefully — especially with acids!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Acids give H⁺ ions; bases give OH⁻ ions in water.
  - Salts are products of the neutralization reaction.
  - An indicator (ਸੂਚਕ) detects whether a solution is acid or base.
  - Neutralization = Acid + Base → Salt + Water.

BOARD TIP: The equation "Acid + Base → Salt + Water" is a frequent 1-mark fill-in-the-blank. Memorize this definition of neutralization.

SLIDE 3: Vocabulary Continued (ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ ਜਾਰੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Continuation vocabulary table: Alkali, Hydronium, Brine, Acid Rain, Chlor-Alkali
  - Special callout box: "The Alkali Rule — All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis!"

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us continue building our vocabulary with five more important terms.

  Alkali (ਐਲਕਲੀ) — An alkali is a special type of base. The key rule is: all alkalis are bases, but
  NOT all bases are alkalis. An alkali is a base that completely dissolves in water. For example,
  sodium hydroxide (NaOH) dissolves fully in water, so it is an alkali. But some bases, like copper
  oxide (CuO), do not dissolve in water — so they are bases but NOT alkalis. This distinction is very
  important for exams.

  Hydronium (H₃O⁺) — When an H⁺ ion is released by an acid in water, it does not float freely. It
  immediately attaches to a water molecule to form H₃O⁺, called the hydronium ion. This is the actual
  ion responsible for acidic behavior.

  Brine (ਬ੍ਰਾਈਨ) — This is simply a concentrated aqueous solution of sodium chloride — salt water.
  We will see brine again when we study the chlor-alkali process.

  Acid Rain (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬੀ ਵਰਖਾ) — Normal rain has a pH of about 5.6 because CO₂ in the air dissolves in
  rainwater. When industrial gases like SO₂ and NO₂ dissolve in rain, the pH drops well below 5.6,
  making it acid rain — highly damaging to plants, buildings, and aquatic life.

  Chlor-Alkali (ਕਲੋਰ-ਐਲਕਲੀ ਪ੍ਰਕਿਰਿਆ) — This is an industrial process where electricity is passed
  through brine to produce three useful products: sodium hydroxide (NaOH), chlorine gas (Cl₂), and
  hydrogen gas (H₂). We will study this in detail in a later slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis — only water-soluble bases are alkalis.
  - H⁺ ion in water forms H₃O⁺ (hydronium ion).
  - Acid rain has pH below 5.6 due to SO₂/NO₂ from burning fossil fuels.
  - Brine = aqueous solution of NaCl.

BOARD TIP: "All alkalis are bases but not all bases are alkalis" — this exact statement appears as a 1-mark or 2-mark question in PSEB exams. Memorize it.

SLIDE 4: Acids vs. Bases (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਅਤੇ ਖਾਰ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table: Acids vs. Bases (taste, litmus test, ion produced)
  - Special callout: "Dry HCl Exception" — Dry HCl gas does NOT change dry blue litmus paper

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us compare acids and bases side by side. This is the most foundational comparison in the
  whole chapter.

  Taste — Acids taste sour (ਖੱਟੇ). Think of lemon juice, tamarind, or vinegar — all sour, all acidic.
  Bases taste bitter (ਕੌੜੇ). Baking soda tastes bitter. Bases also feel soapy to the touch, because
  soap itself is a base.

  Litmus test — Acids turn BLUE litmus paper RED. Bases turn RED litmus paper BLUE. A simple way to
  remember: Acids are aggressive — they attack blue and turn it red. Bases are cool — they calm red
  down to blue.

  Ions — Acids give H⁺ ions in water. Bases give OH⁻ ions in water. These ions are the chemical
  explanation for all the physical properties we just discussed.

  Now, here is a very important exception that appears regularly in PSEB exams: the Dry HCl Exception.
  Hydrochloric acid is an acid, right? So you might expect dry HCl gas to turn dry blue litmus paper
  red. But it does NOT! Why? Because acids only release H⁺ ions when they are dissolved in water.
  Dry HCl gas has no water, so it cannot produce H⁺ ions, and therefore it cannot show acidic behavior.
  The moment you add water, HCl dissolves and produces H⁺ ions — and THEN it turns litmus red. This is
  a classic trick question in board exams!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Acids: sour taste, turn blue litmus red, give H⁺ in water.
  - Bases: bitter taste, soapy feel, turn red litmus blue, give OH⁻ in water.
  - Acidic/basic properties are only shown in the PRESENCE of water.
  - Dry HCl does NOT turn dry blue litmus red — no water = no H⁺ ions.

BOARD TIP: "Dry HCl gas does not turn dry blue litmus red" — this is a very frequent 2-mark "Give Reason" question in PSEB. Never miss it.

SLIDE 5: Strong vs. Weak (ਸ਼ਕਤੀਸ਼ਾਲੀ ਅਤੇ ਕਮਜ਼ੋਰ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Introductory statement: strength depends on degree of ionization in water
  - Comparison table: Strong Acid vs. Weak Acid with definition and examples
  - Examples: Strong — HCl, H₂SO₄; Weak — Acetic Acid (CH₃COOH)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Not all acids are equally powerful. The strength of an acid or base depends on how completely it
  ionizes — that is, how fully it breaks apart into ions — when dissolved in water.

  A Strong Acid (ਸ਼ਕਤੀਸ਼ਾਲੀ ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ) completely ionizes in water. This means every single molecule
  breaks apart and releases H⁺ ions. Because there are many H⁺ ions, the solution is highly acidic.
  Examples include hydrochloric acid (HCl) and sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄). These are the acids we use
  in school labs and they require extreme caution.

  A Weak Acid (ਕਮਜ਼ੋਰ ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ) only partially ionizes in water. Most molecules stay intact without
  breaking apart. So there are fewer H⁺ ions, and the solution is mildly acidic. Acetic acid
  (CH₃COOH), the acid in vinegar, is the perfect example. It is acidic — you can taste it — but it
  is weak enough to use in food safely.

  An important clarification: strength is NOT the same as concentration. A concentrated weak acid
  and a dilute strong acid can have very different properties. Strength refers only to the degree of
  ionization.

  The same logic applies to bases. A strong base like NaOH fully ionizes to give many OH⁻ ions.
  A weak base like ammonia solution (NH₄OH) only partially ionizes.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Strong acid = completely ionizes in water → many H⁺ ions. Examples: HCl, H₂SO₄.
  - Weak acid = partially ionizes in water → fewer H⁺ ions. Example: CH₃COOH (acetic acid).
  - Strength ≠ Concentration. These are two different concepts.
  - Same logic applies to strong and weak bases.

BOARD TIP: Students often confuse "strong acid" with "concentrated acid." Strength refers to degree of ionization, not how much is dissolved.

========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story-mode reading passage titled "The Magic of Litmus"
  - Audio play/stop controls (interactive element)
  - Narrative about the history of litmus extracted from lichens

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide is a reading comprehension passage. Have your students read — or play the audio — and
  follow along carefully.

  Let me walk you through the story. Long before modern laboratory equipment existed, scientists
  needed a way to identify whether a liquid was acidic or basic just by looking at it. They found the
  answer in nature. Certain natural dyes change color depending on the chemical environment around them.

  The most famous of these natural dyes is litmus — a purple pigment extracted from lichens, which
  are slow-growing, plant-like organisms found growing on rocks and trees. When litmus is placed in

  simple biological extract gave early chemists the power to "see" the invisible chemical nature of a
  liquid just by watching the color shift.

  This passage teaches us something important: science is built on careful observation of the natural
  world. Litmus is not something invented in a lab — it was discovered by watching nature. Use this
  story to remember that litmus is a NATURAL indicator, extracted from a living organism, and that its
  color change is a reliable, time-tested method for identifying acids and bases. After reading, ask
  students: "Why was litmus so useful before modern instruments existed?" and "What color does litmus
  show in a neutral solution?" — the answer is purple, its original color.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Litmus is a natural dye extracted from lichens.
  - Red in acid, Blue in base, Purple when neutral.
  - Natural indicators precede modern pH meters.
  - The color change is caused by the H⁺ or OH⁻ ions in solution.

SLIDE 7: Indicators & pH (ਸੂਚਕ ਅਤੇ pH)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Bullet list of three indicator types: Litmus, Phenolphthalein, Olfactory Indicators
  - pH Scale summary: Acidic < 7, Neutral = 7, Basic > 7

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us look at the tools we use to identify acids and bases — these are called indicators (ਸੂਚਕ).

  The first and most common is Litmus. It is available as litmus paper or litmus solution. In an
  acidic solution, litmus turns red. In a basic solution, it turns blue. Simple, reliable, and
  universally used in school labs.

  The second is Phenolphthalein. This is a colorless liquid indicator. In an acidic or neutral
  solution, it remains colorless — you cannot see any change. But in a basic solution, it turns a
  striking pink color. It is especially useful in titration experiments.

  The third type is Olfactory Indicators (ਸੁੰਘਣ ਵਾਲੇ ਸੂਚਕ). These are indicators that work through
  smell, not color. For example, onion and clove oil have strong, characteristic smells. In a base,
  these smells disappear or change significantly. So if you add an olfactory indicator to a solution
  and the smell vanishes, the solution is basic. These are useful when color-based indicators cannot
  be used.

  Now, the pH Scale. pH stands for "potential of Hydrogen." It is a numerical scale from 0 to 14
  that tells us exactly how acidic or basic a solution is.
  — pH less than 7: the solution is acidic. The lower the pH, the stronger the acid.
  — pH equal to 7: the solution is neutral. Pure water is the classic example.
  — pH greater than 7: the solution is basic. The higher the pH, the stronger the base.

  This scale is universal — scientists across the world use it to communicate the exact strength of
  an acid or base without any ambiguity.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Litmus: Red in acid, Blue in base.
  - Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid/neutral, Pink in base.
  - Olfactory indicators (ਸੁੰਘਣ ਵਾਲੇ ਸੂਚਕ): lose smell/change odor in bases.
  - pH < 7 = acidic; pH = 7 = neutral; pH > 7 = basic.

BOARD TIP: Olfactory indicators — "onion and clove" — are a frequent 1-mark question. Remember: their odor disappears in bases.

SLIDE 8: pH in Digestion & Soil (ਪਾਚਨ ਅਤੇ ਮਿੱਟੀ ਵਿੱਚ pH)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two real-life applications of pH
  - Digestive System (ਪਾਚਨ ਪ੍ਰਣਾਲੀ): stomach produces HCl at pH ~1.2; antacids neutralize excess
  - Soil pH (ਮਿੱਟੀ ਦਾ pH): plants need neutral soil; acidic soil treated with quicklime (CaO)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The pH scale is not just a textbook concept — it has direct, life-or-death consequences in your own
  body and in agriculture. Let us look at two very important real-life applications.

  First — the Digestive System (ਪਾਚਨ ਪ੍ਰਣਾਲੀ). Your stomach produces hydrochloric acid (HCl) to help
  digest food. The stomach naturally maintains a pH of about 1.2 — that is extremely acidic! The inner
  lining of the stomach is specially designed to withstand this acid. But sometimes the stomach produces
  too much acid — perhaps due to spicy food, stress, or other reasons. This excess acid causes the
  burning sensation we call acidity or heartburn. The treatment? Antacids. Antacids contain mild bases
  like magnesium hydroxide (milk of magnesia) or sodium bicarbonate. When you take an antacid, it
  neutralizes the excess acid, reducing the pain. This is neutralization chemistry happening inside
  your body!

  Second — Soil pH (ਮਿੱਟੀ ਦਾ pH). Plants grow best in soil with a pH close to 7 — neutral or
  slightly acidic. When farmers overuse chemical fertilizers, the soil gradually becomes too acidic.
  Acidic soil reduces plant growth and crop yield. The solution? Farmers add Quicklime (CaO), also
  called calcium oxide, to the soil. Quicklime is a base, and it neutralizes the excess acid, restoring
  the soil pH to a healthy level. This is an everyday agricultural application of acid-base chemistry.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Stomach produces HCl at pH ~1.2 for digestion.
  - Excess acid → acidity/heartburn → treated with antacids (mild bases).
  - Plants grow best in neutral soil (pH ~7).
  - Acidic soil → add Quicklime (CaO) to neutralize it.

BOARD TIP: "Why do farmers add quicklime to soil?" is a classic 2-mark application question. Answer: to neutralize excess acidity in the soil.

SLIDE 11: Reading Comprehension Story — The Acid Rain Crisis
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story-mode reading passage titled "The Acid Rain Crisis"
  - Audio play/stop controls (interactive element)
  - Narrative covering formation of acid rain and its environmental impact

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is another reading comprehension story — have students read it independently or play the audio.
  Let me walk you through the key science in this passage so you can guide the discussion.

  When coal and petroleum are burned in factories and vehicles, they release sulfur dioxide (SO₂) and
  nitrogen oxides (NOₓ) into the atmosphere. These gases rise into the sky and dissolve in atmospheric
  water vapour, forming dilute sulfuric acid (H₂SO₄) and nitric acid (HNO₃). When this acidic moisture
  falls as rain, it is called acid rain (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬੀ ਵਰਖਾ).

  Normal rain has a pH of about 5.6 because even clean air has CO₂, which forms a mild carbonic acid
  (H₂CO₃) in rainwater. Acid rain drops significantly below 5.6 — sometimes as low as pH 3 or 4.
  This is highly damaging.

  The consequences are severe: marble and limestone buildings and monuments are dissolved by acid rain
  — this is called acid attack on marble (CaCO₃). Lakes and rivers become too acidic for fish and
  frogs to survive. Forests are damaged. This is an environmental chemistry problem directly linked
  to human industrial activity.

  After the reading, ask students: "What gases cause acid rain?" (SO₂ and NOₓ). "What is the pH of
  normal rain versus acid rain?" (5.6 vs. below 5.6). "What equation connects acid rain with marble
  damage?" — CaCO₃ + H₂SO₄ → CaSO₄ + H₂O + CO₂.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Burning fossil fuels releases SO₂ and NOₓ → form H₂SO₄ and HNO₃ in atmosphere.
  - Acid rain has pH < 5.6; normal rain ~5.6.
  - Acid rain damages aquatic life, buildings (marble/limestone), and forests.
  - This is a real-world application of acids and pH.

BOARD TIP: "What causes acid rain?" and "What is the pH of normal vs. acid rain?" are frequent short-answer questions.

SLIDE 12: Acid Reactions (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਦੀਆਂ ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Three key acid reaction types with Punjabi labels:
    1. Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas
    2. Acid + Base → Salt + Water (Neutralization / ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ)
    3. Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Acids are chemically reactive — they react with metals, bases, and carbonates. Let us study each
  of these three reaction types carefully, because they are all exam-relevant.

  Reaction Type 1 — Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas.
  When a metal like zinc or iron is placed in an acid like hydrochloric acid, a salt is produced along
  with hydrogen gas. For example:
  Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑
  The hydrogen gas produced makes a characteristic "pop" sound when a burning matchstick is brought
  near — this is the test for hydrogen gas. Not all metals react with all acids, and some metals like
  gold and platinum do not react at all.

  Reaction Type 2 — Acid + Base → Salt + Water. This is the neutralization reaction
  (ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ). The acid and base "neutralize" each other. Neither acidic nor basic properties
  remain — the result is a salt dissolved in water. For example:
  HCl + NaOH → NaCl + H₂O
  The word "neutralization" means both the acid and base lose their individual character. This is
  the chemistry behind antacids curing acidity!

  Reaction Type 3 — Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂.
  When an acid reacts with a metal carbonate or hydrogencarbonate, it produces salt, water, AND
  carbon dioxide gas. The CO₂ gas makes the solution bubble and fizz. This is why vinegar (acid)
  and baking soda (carbonate) fizz together. We will see the equations for this on the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Acid + Metal → Salt + H₂ gas (hydrogen tested by pop sound).
  - Acid + Base → Salt + Water (Neutralization / ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ).
  - Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂ (tested by limewater turning milky).
  - All three reaction types are exam-tested individually.

BOARD TIP: These three reaction types with one example each is a very common 3-mark question. Practice writing balanced equations for each.

SLIDE 13: Reading Comprehension Story — The Baking Soda Volcano
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story-mode reading passage titled "The Baking Soda Volcano"
  - Audio play/stop controls
  - Narrative connecting science-fair volcano to acid-carbonate reactions and baking chemistry

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This reading comprehension passage connects the acid-carbonate reaction we just studied to two very
  familiar real-life situations: the classic science-fair volcano and baking a cake.

  Let me walk you through the science. The "volcano" experiment works because vinegar (which contains
  ethanoic acid / acetic acid) reacts with baking soda (sodium hydrogencarbonate, NaHCO₃). This is a
  perfect acid-carbonate reaction:
  CH₃COOH + NaHCO₃ → CH₃COONa + H₂O + CO₂↑
  The CO₂ gas is released rapidly, causing the dramatic bubbling — the "eruption."

  Now, bakers use this exact same chemistry! When baking soda is mixed with a mild edible acid
  (like yogurt or lemon juice) in cake batter, CO₂ gas is produced. This gas gets trapped in the wet
  dough as tiny bubbles. When the cake is put in a hot oven, the gas expands due to heat, and the
  bubbles push the dough upward, making the cake rise and become light and fluffy. Without CO₂
  production, you would get a flat, dense cake.

  After reading, ask students: "What type of reaction is happening in both the volcano and the cake?"
  Answer: Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂. Ask them to identify the acid and the carbonate in
  each case. This reinforces Slide 12's reaction type 3 with real examples.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Vinegar (acetic acid) + baking soda (NaHCO₃) → CO₂ gas is produced.
  - This is an acid-carbonate reaction: acid + carbonate → salt + water + CO₂.
  - Same reaction makes cakes rise — CO₂ trapped in dough expands when heated.
  - Connecting chemistry to daily life strengthens conceptual understanding.

SLIDE 14: Acids with Carbonates (ਕਾਰਬੋਨੇਟਾਂ ਨਾਲ ਕਿਰਿਆ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Explanation: acid + metal carbonate or hydrogencarbonate → salt + water + CO₂
  - Two balanced equations:
    Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
    NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We saw the general rule on Slide 12. Now let us look at two specific, balanced equations for acid
  reacting with carbonates — these are the equations you must be able to write in the exam.

  Equation 1 — Sodium carbonate with hydrochloric acid:
  Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
  Sodium carbonate is a solid. It reacts with two molecules of hydrochloric acid. The products are
  sodium chloride dissolved in water, water, and carbon dioxide gas. Notice the state symbols —
  (s) for solid, (aq) for aqueous (dissolved in water), (l) for liquid, (g) for gas. The CO₂ gas
  bubbles out of the solution — this is what you see as fizzing.

  Equation 2 — Sodium hydrogencarbonate with hydrochloric acid:
  NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
  This is a simpler, 1:1 ratio reaction. Sodium hydrogencarbonate (baking soda) reacts with one
  molecule of HCl to produce the same three products: sodium chloride, water, and CO₂.

  How do we prove CO₂ is produced? Pass the gas through limewater (calcium hydroxide solution,
  Ca(OH)₂). The CO₂ reacts with limewater to form calcium carbonate (CaCO₃), which is white and
  insoluble — this turns the limewater milky/cloudy. This is called the limewater test for CO₂.
  Interesting fact: if you keep passing excess CO₂, the milky limewater turns clear again! This is
  because the excess CO₂ reacts with CaCO₃ to form calcium hydrogencarbonate (Ca(HCO₃)₂), which
  is soluble.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Na₂CO₃ + 2HCl → 2NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ (learn state symbols).
  - NaHCO₃ + HCl → NaCl + H₂O + CO₂.
  - CO₂ is confirmed by turning limewater milky; excess CO₂ clears limewater again.
  - Memorize both balanced equations for the exam.

BOARD TIP: Writing these balanced equations with correct state symbols is a 3-mark question. Pay attention to the "2" coefficient in the first equation.

SLIDE 15: Metallic vs. Non-Metallic Oxides
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table: Metallic Oxides vs. Non-Metallic Oxides
  - Metallic: react with acids → basic in nature; examples CuO, MgO
  - Non-Metallic: react with bases → acidic in nature; examples CO₂, SO₂

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide is a very important concept that connects acids and bases to the world of metal and
  non-metal chemistry.

  Let us start with Metallic Oxides. These are compounds formed when a metal combines with oxygen.
  Examples include copper oxide (CuO) and magnesium oxide (MgO). The key property: metallic oxides
  react with acids to produce salt and water. Because they react with acids — that is, they can
  neutralize acids — they are basic in nature. They are also called basic oxides.

  For example:
  CuO + H₂SO₄ → CuSO₄ + H₂O
  MgO + 2HCl → MgCl₂ + H₂O

  Now, Non-Metallic Oxides. These are compounds formed when a non-metal combines with oxygen.
  Examples include carbon dioxide (CO₂) and sulfur dioxide (SO₂). These react with bases to produce
  salt and water. Because they react with bases — neutralizing them — they are acidic in nature.
  They are called acidic oxides.

  For example:
  CO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂CO₃ + H₂O
  SO₂ + 2NaOH → Na₂SO₃ + H₂O

  The pattern to remember: METALS form BASIC oxides. NON-METALS form ACIDIC oxides. This connects
  beautifully to why SO₂ from industry causes acid rain — it is an acidic non-metallic oxide that
  reacts with water in the atmosphere.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Metallic oxides (CuO, MgO) react with acids → they are BASIC in nature.
  - Non-metallic oxides (CO₂, SO₂) react with bases → they are ACIDIC in nature.
  - Metals → basic oxides; Non-metals → acidic oxides. (Easy pattern to remember.)
  - CO₂ dissolving in rainwater is why even normal rain is slightly acidic.

BOARD TIP: "Why are metallic oxides basic in nature?" — Answer: because they react with acids to form salt and water. Frequent 2-mark question.

SLIDE 16: Lab Guide — Identifying Gases (ਗੈਸਾਂ ਦੀ ਪਛਾਣ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Context: acids release gases during reactions; how do we identify which gas?
  - Test 1 (Pop Test): burning matchstick near test tube — "pop" sound confirms Hydrogen (H₂)
  - Test 2 (Milky Test): pass gas through limewater — milky/cloudy confirms CO₂; excess CO₂ clears it again

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  When acids react with metals or carbonates, gases are released. In the school lab, we need to
  identify which gas has been produced. There are two key gas tests you must know for the exam.

  Test 1 — The Pop Test for Hydrogen Gas (H₂).
  After the acid reacts with a metal and the gas collects in the test tube, bring a burning matchstick
  close to the mouth of the test tube. If the gas is hydrogen, it will ignite and burn with a sharp
  "pop" or crackling sound. This "pop" sound is the standard confirmatory test for hydrogen gas.
  Why does it pop? Because hydrogen is highly flammable and burns rapidly when it contacts a flame.

  Test 2 — The Limewater Test for Carbon Dioxide (CO₂).
  After the acid reacts with a carbonate and CO₂ gas is produced, pass the gas through limewater —
  that is, a solution of calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)₂). The CO₂ reacts with Ca(OH)₂ to form calcium
  carbonate (CaCO₃), which is a white precipitate. This turns the limewater milky or cloudy.
  The reaction: CO₂ + Ca(OH)₂ → CaCO₃↓ + H₂O

  Very important extra point: if you continue passing EXCESS CO₂ into the milky limewater, it turns
  clear again! This is because the excess CO₂ reacts with CaCO₃ to form calcium hydrogencarbonate
  (Ca(HCO₃)₂), which IS soluble in water.
  Ca(CO)₃ + CO₂ + H₂O → Ca(HCO₃)₂

  Both of these gas tests appear in practical-based exam questions every year.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Hydrogen: burning matchstick → POP sound.
  - CO₂: passed into limewater → limewater turns MILKY/CLOUDY.
  - Excess CO₂ into milky limewater → turns CLEAR again (forms Ca(HCO₃)₂).
  - Learn both tests — they appear in lab practical questions.

BOARD TIP: "How do you test for hydrogen gas in the lab?" — Pop test with burning matchstick. Very commonly asked in practical-based 2-mark questions.

SLIDE 17: Safety — The Dilution Process (ਪਤਲਾ ਕਰਨਾ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Safety warning heading: "Add Acid to Water! NEVER add water to acid."
  - Reason: highly exothermic — can splash and cause severe burns
  - Scientific explanation: dilution decreases ion concentration (H₃O⁺ / OH⁻) per unit volume

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide covers one of the most important safety rules in chemistry — and it is also an exam topic.
  It is about how to safely dilute a concentrated acid.

  The Golden Rule: ALWAYS add acid to water — NEVER add water to acid.
  In Punjabi: ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਨੂੰ ਪਾਣੀ ਵਿੱਚ ਪਾਉ, ਪਾਣੀ ਨੂੰ ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਵਿੱਚ ਨਹੀਂ।

  Why? Because mixing a concentrated acid with water is a highly exothermic reaction — it releases
  a very large amount of heat. If you add water to concentrated acid (instead of the other way around),
  this heat is generated so rapidly and in such a small volume of liquid that it can cause the mixture
  to boil and splatter violently. That splatter is concentrated acid — it can cause severe chemical
  burns to your skin, face, and eyes. It can also crack or shatter the glass container.

  When you add acid to a large volume of water instead, the heat released is distributed and absorbed
  by the water safely. The large mass of water absorbs the heat without a dangerous temperature spike.

  What happens scientifically during dilution? When we dilute an acid or base by adding water, the
  number of H₃O⁺ ions (in the case of an acid) or OH⁻ ions (in the case of a base) per unit volume
  decreases. There are the same number of ions overall, but they are now spread across a larger volume
  of water. So the concentration of ions goes down, and the acid or base becomes weaker.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - ALWAYS add acid to water; NEVER add water to acid.
  - Mixing is highly exothermic — adding water to acid causes violent splattering.
  - Dilution reduces H₃O⁺ / OH⁻ ion concentration per unit volume.
  - This is both a safety rule AND a chemistry concept.

BOARD TIP: "Why should acid always be added to water and not water to acid?" is a classic 2-mark "Give Reason" question. The answer: the reaction is highly exothermic and adding water to acid can cause the mixture to splash.

SLIDE 18: Top "Give Reason" Questions — PSEB 2017, 2019, 2023
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Three frequently asked "Give Reason" exam questions with answers:
    1. Why should curd not be kept in brass/copper vessels?
    2. Why do acids not show acidic behavior without water?
    3. Why does an aqueous solution of acid conduct electricity?
  - PSEB year tags: 2017, 2019, 2023

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide is pure exam preparation — three "Give Reason" type questions that have appeared in
  actual PSEB board exams in 2017, 2019, and 2023. These are worth 2 marks each. Let us go through
  all three carefully.

  Question 1: Why should curd not be kept in brass or copper vessels?
  Answer: Curd contains lactic acid. When curd is stored in a brass or copper vessel, the lactic acid
  reacts with the metal (brass is an alloy of copper and zinc) to form metallic salts that are
  poisonous in nature. Consuming these salts can cause food poisoning. Therefore, curd should be stored
  in glass, ceramic, or food-grade plastic containers, not metallic ones.

  Question 2: Why do acids not show acidic behavior without water?
  Answer: Acids only release H⁺ ions when they are dissolved in water. In the absence of water,
  there is no ionization — the acid molecules remain intact and do not produce H⁺ ions. Since the
  acidic properties (like turning litmus red or conducting electricity) depend on the presence of H⁺
  ions, acids cannot show acidic behavior in the absence of water. The classic example: dry HCl gas
  does not turn dry blue litmus paper red.

  Question 3: Why does an aqueous solution of an acid conduct electricity?
  Answer: Electricity is conducted through a solution by the movement of charged particles called
  ions. When an acid dissolves in water, it ionizes to produce H⁺ ions (and corresponding negative
  ions). These free-moving ions carry electric charge through the solution, allowing it to conduct
  electricity. A solution that conducts electricity this way is called an electrolyte.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Curd in brass/copper → lactic acid reacts with metal → poisonous salts formed.
  - Acids need water to ionize → no water = no H⁺ = no acidic behavior.
  - Aqueous acids conduct electricity because they produce free-moving H⁺ ions (electrolytes).
  - All three answers are "Give Reason" format — practice writing them in 2-3 sentences.

BOARD TIP: These three questions have appeared in PSEB 2017, 2019, and 2023. Write complete reason-based answers — not one-liners — to get full 2-mark credit.

SLIDE 19: The Chlor-Alkali Process — PSEB 2020, 2022
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: electrolysis of brine (NaCl aqueous solution)
  - Balanced equation: 2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)
  - Three products and uses:
    - Chlorine (Anode): water treatment, swimming pools
    - Hydrogen (Cathode): fuel, making margarine
    - Sodium Hydroxide (near Cathode): degreasing metals, making soaps

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The Chlor-Alkali Process is one of the most important industrial applications in this chapter,
  and it has appeared in PSEB board exams in 2020 and 2022. Pay close attention.

  The process begins with brine — a concentrated aqueous solution of sodium chloride (NaCl). When
  electricity is passed through brine, the process of electrolysis occurs — electricity breaks the
  chemical bonds in brine. The balanced equation is:

  2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)

  Three useful products are formed:

  Product 1 — Chlorine gas (Cl₂) is produced at the anode (the positive electrode). Chlorine is a
  disinfectant — it kills harmful bacteria. It is used for treating drinking water and in swimming
  pools to keep the water safe and clean. Chlorine is also used to make bleaching powder and PVC plastic.

  Product 2 — Hydrogen gas (H₂) is produced at the cathode (the negative electrode). Hydrogen is
  used as a fuel (it burns cleanly) and in the food industry for making margarine — by hydrogenation
  of vegetable oils.

  Product 3 — Sodium Hydroxide (NaOH) remains in solution near the cathode. NaOH is a strong base
  with many industrial uses: it is used for degreasing metals (removing oil and grease from steel),
  making soaps, making paper, and in the textile industry.

  The name "Chlor-Alkali" (ਕਲੋਰ-ਐਲਕਲੀ ਪ੍ਰਕਿਰਿਆ) comes from the two main products: Chlorine and
  the Alkali (NaOH).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Chlor-Alkali process = electrolysis of brine (NaCl aqueous solution).
  - Equation: 2NaCl + 2H₂O → 2NaOH + Cl₂ + H₂.
  - Chlorine → water treatment / swimming pools (at anode).
  - Hydrogen → fuel / margarine (at cathode).
  - NaOH → soap making / degreasing metals (near cathode).

BOARD TIP: Know the full equation, electrode positions (Cl₂ at anode, H₂ at cathode), and at least two uses of each product. This 5-mark question appears almost every exam.

SLIDE 20: Manufacturing Salts — Bleaching Powder & Baking Soda

ON SCREEN:

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we study two very important manufactured salts: bleaching powder and baking soda. Both are
  produced through specific chemical reactions and have important uses in daily life and industry.

  Salt 1 — Bleaching Powder (CaOCl₂).
  Bleaching powder is produced by the action of chlorine gas on dry slaked lime. Slaked lime is
  calcium hydroxide — Ca(OH)₂. The equation is:
  Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O
  Note: dry slaked lime must be used — not wet. If water is present, the reaction produces different

  Uses of Bleaching Powder: it is used as a disinfectant to purify drinking water — it kills harmful

  Salt 2 — Baking Soda (NaHCO₃), also called Sodium Hydrogencarbonate.
  Baking soda is produced using a reaction involving sodium chloride (brine), water, carbon dioxide,

  NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NH₄Cl + NaHCO₃

  Uses of Baking Soda: it is used in making baking powder (a mixture of baking soda and a mild acid
  like tartaric acid). When baking powder is heated in an oven, CO₂ is released, which makes the
  cake rise and become fluffy. Baking soda is also used as an antacid — it is mildly basic and
  neutralizes excess stomach acid.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Bleaching Powder: Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O. Used for water disinfection and bleaching cotton.
  - Baking Soda: NaHCO₃. Produced by Solvay Process. Used in baking and as antacid.
  - NaHCO₃ releases CO₂ when heated — this makes cakes rise.
  - Chemical formula of bleaching powder: CaOCl₂.

BOARD TIP: "Baking soda is prepared by the Solvay process" — TRUE. The formula CaOCl₂ for bleaching powder and NaHCO₃ for baking soda are 1-mark questions.

SLIDE 21: Manufacturing Salts — Washing Soda & Plaster of Paris
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O): recrystallization of sodium carbonate
    Equation: Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O
    Uses: glass/soap/paper industries, removing permanent hardness of water
  - Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O): heating Gypsum at exactly 373 K
    Equation: CaSO₄·2H₂O + Heat(373K) → CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O
    Uses: supporting fractured bones

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we study two more important manufactured salts: washing soda and plaster of Paris. These also
  involve a key concept called "water of crystallization" — meaning water molecules that are
  chemically incorporated into the crystal structure of these salts.

  Salt 3 — Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O).
  Washing soda is sodium carbonate decahydrate — meaning each formula unit contains 10 molecules of
  water. It is produced by a process called recrystallization: when anhydrous sodium carbonate (Na₂CO₃)
  is dissolved in water and then allowed to crystallize slowly, it picks up 10 molecules of water to
  form washing soda crystals.
  Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O

  Uses of Washing Soda: it is used in the glass, soap, and paper industries. It is also used to
  remove the permanent hardness of water — hard water contains dissolved calcium and magnesium salts
  that prevent soap from lathering properly. Washing soda reacts with these salts and removes them.

  Salt 4 — Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O).
  Plaster of Paris is calcium sulfate hemihydrate — each formula unit contains only HALF a molecule
  of water. It is made by heating gypsum (CaSO₄·2H₂O) at exactly 373 K (100°C).
  CaSO₄·2H₂O + Heat(373K) → CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O

  Why exactly 373 K? If you heat gypsum above this temperature, you get "dead burnt plaster," which
  cannot set — it has lost too much water and cannot re-absorb it to harden. The exact temperature
  is critical.

  Use of Plaster of Paris: doctors use it to make casts for fractured bones. When Plaster of Paris
  is mixed with water, it re-absorbs water and sets into a hard solid — this immobilizes the broken
  bone while it heals. The setting process is: CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O → CaSO₄·2H₂O (back to gypsum!).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Washing Soda: Na₂CO₃·10H₂O — contains 10 molecules of water of crystallization.
  - Plaster of Paris: CaSO₄·½H₂O — contains ½ molecule of water of crystallization.
  - Plaster of Paris made by heating gypsum at EXACTLY 373 K.
  - When POP + water → hardens (sets) = becomes gypsum again.

BOARD TIP: "At what temperature is gypsum heated to form Plaster of Paris?" — 373 K. "How many water molecules does washing soda contain?" — 10. Both are frequent 1-mark questions.

  Point 2: Indicators tell us whether a solution is acidic or basic by changing color (litmus,
  phenolphthalein, methyl orange) or odor (olfactory indicators like onion and clove).

  Point 3: The pH scale runs from 0 (strongest acid) to 14 (strongest base), with 7 being perfectly
  neutral. Pure water is the universal neutral reference at pH 7.

  Point 4: Important manufactured salts include Baking Soda (NaHCO₃) — used in baking and as an
  antacid; Washing Soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O) — used in cleaning and industry; Bleaching Powder (CaOCl₂)
  — used for disinfection; and Plaster of Paris (CaSO₄·½H₂O) — used in medicine.

  Point 5: Water of crystallization (ਕ੍ਰਿਸਟਲੀਕਰਨ ਦਾ ਪਾਣੀ) is the fixed number of water molecules
  chemically bonded within a salt crystal. Washing soda has 10 water molecules; Plaster of Paris has
  ½; Gypsum has 2. This water gives the crystals their specific shape, color, and properties.

  Take a moment to make a mental list — how many key equations can you write from memory right now?
  The practice questions coming up will test all of this.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Acids: H⁺, sour; Bases: OH⁻, bitter; Neutralization: acid + base → salt + water.
  - pH 0–14: below 7 acidic, 7 neutral, above 7 basic.
  - Water of crystallization is part of the crystal structure, not free water.
  - Know all four manufactured salts, their formulas, and their uses.

BOARD TIP: "What is water of crystallization?" is a common 2-mark definition question. Answer: the fixed number of water molecules chemically incorporated in one formula unit of a crystalline salt.

SLIDE 27: True or False? — 13 Questions (ਸਹੀ ਜਾਂ ਗਲਤ?)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive True/False activity with 13 questions (bilingual — English + Punjabi)
  - Topics cover litmus, baking soda, pH, acids/bases, olfactory indicators, dry HCl,
    washing soda, Solvay process, ionization, acid-carbonate reactions

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is a 13-question True or False revision activity. Run this as a class engagement exercise.
  Before revealing each answer, ask students to vote — thumbs up for True, thumbs down for False.
  Let us go through each question and its correct answer with explanation.

  Q1: Acids turn red litmus paper blue. → FALSE. Acids turn BLUE litmus RED. It is bases that turn
  red litmus blue.

  Q2: Baking soda is basic in nature. → TRUE. NaHCO₃ is mildly basic — it is used as an antacid.

  Q3: The pH of pure water is 0. → FALSE. Pure water has a pH of 7 (neutral). pH 0 is an extremely
  strong acid.

  Q4: Acids taste bitter and feel soapy to touch. → FALSE. These are properties of BASES, not acids.
  Acids taste sour and can feel corrosive.

  Q5: Water neutralizes a base to form salt and hydrogen gas. → FALSE. It is an ACID that neutralizes
  a base, producing salt and WATER — not hydrogen gas.

  Q6: Olfactory indicators change their odor in acidic or basic solutions. → TRUE. Onion and clove
  are olfactory indicators that change or lose their smell in basic solutions.

  Q7: A solution with pH 12 is a strong acid. → FALSE. pH 12 is strongly BASIC (alkaline). Strong
  acids have very low pH (close to 0).

  Q8: Baking soda is prepared using the Solvay process. → TRUE. The Solvay process uses NaCl, H₂O,
  CO₂, and NH₃ to produce NaHCO₃.

  Q9: A strong acid completely ionizes in water. → TRUE. By definition, a strong acid fully
  dissociates into ions in water.

  Q10: Dry HCl gas turns dry blue litmus paper red. → FALSE. Dry HCl has no water, so it cannot
  produce H⁺ ions, and therefore CANNOT change dry blue litmus paper.

  Q11: Washing soda contains 10 molecules of water of crystallization. → TRUE. The formula is
  Na₂CO₃·10H₂O — ten water molecules per formula unit.

  Q12: Tamarind contains Tartaric acid. → TRUE. Tamarind is sour because it contains tartaric acid.

  Q13: When an acid reacts with a metal carbonate, oxygen gas is released. → FALSE. The gas released
  is CARBON DIOXIDE (CO₂), not oxygen.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - False: Acids turn blue litmus red (not red litmus blue).
  - False: pH 12 is strongly basic, NOT acidic.
  - False: Dry HCl does NOT change dry litmus.
  - True: Washing soda has 10 water molecules; baking soda prepared by Solvay process.

BOARD TIP: Questions 7 (pH 12 = base, not acid) and 10 (dry HCl exception) are the trickiest — these are the ones students most commonly get wrong.

SLIDE 29: Quick Check — Chapter 2 (ਤੁਰੰਤ ਜਾਂਚ) — 12 MCQs
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - 12 multiple-choice questions (bilingual), topics across the full chapter
  - Final slide — ends with "Continue to Chapter 3 →"

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is the final activity of Chapter 2 — a 12-question multiple choice Quick Check. This format
  mirrors the type of questions students see in the actual PSEB exam. Let us go through every
  question with the correct answer and a brief explanation.

  Q1: What is the pH of a neutral solution?
  Correct: B — 7. Pure water is the neutral standard. pH below 7 = acidic; above 7 = basic.

  Q2: Which gas is released when an acid reacts with a metal?
  Correct: C — Hydrogen. Acid + Metal → Salt + H₂ gas. Tested by pop sound with burning matchstick.

  Q3: Plaster of Paris is made by heating:
  Correct: B — Gypsum. CaSO₄·2H₂O heated at 373 K gives CaSO₄·½H₂O (Plaster of Paris).

  Q4: Which of the following is a strong acid?
  Correct: B — Hydrochloric acid (HCl). It completely ionizes in water. Acetic acid and citric acid
  are weak acids.

  Q5: Baking soda is basic in nature. Which is its formula?
  Correct: B — NaHCO₃. Na₂CO₃ is washing soda; NaOH is caustic soda.

  Q6: Which acid is present in vinegar?
  Correct: C — Acetic acid (CH₃COOH), also called ethanoic acid.

  Q7: Tooth decay starts when the pH of the mouth is lower than:
  Correct: B — 5.5. Below pH 5.5, tooth enamel starts dissolving due to bacterial acids.

  Q8: An aqueous solution turns red litmus blue. Excess addition of which solution would reverse
  the change?
  Correct: C — Hydrochloric acid (HCl). The solution is basic (turned red litmus blue). To reverse
  it, we need to add an acid — HCl will neutralize the base and turn the solution acidic, making
  litmus turn back to red. Baking powder and ammonium hydroxide are both basic — they would not
  reverse the change.

  Q9: Brine is an aqueous solution of:
  Correct: B — Sodium chloride (NaCl). This is the starting material for the chlor-alkali process.

  Q10: Which base is present in milk of magnesia?
  Correct: B — Magnesium hydroxide (Mg(OH)₂). It is used as an antacid.

  Q11: At what temperature is gypsum heated to form Plaster of Paris?
  Correct: B — 373 K. This is the critical and exact temperature. Overheating gives "dead burnt
  plaster" which cannot set.

  Q12: Which of the following salts does NOT contain water of crystallization?
  Correct: B — Baking soda (NaHCO₃). Blue vitriol (CuSO₄·5H₂O) has 5 water molecules; washing
  soda (Na₂CO₃·10H₂O) has 10. Baking soda (NaHCO₃) has NO water of crystallization.

  Excellent work completing Chapter 2! You now know the chemistry of acids, bases, and salts from
  the fundamentals all the way to industrial applications. Continue to Chapter 3 when ready.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - pH 7 = neutral; HCl = strong acid; NaHCO₃ = baking soda formula.
  - Tooth enamel dissolves below pH 5.5.
  - Baking soda has NO water of crystallization; Washing soda has 10.
  - Plaster of Paris made at exactly 373 K from gypsum.

BOARD TIP: Q8 (reversing basic litmus by adding acid) and Q12 (no water of crystallization in baking soda) are the two most commonly missed questions in practice tests. Review both carefully.
