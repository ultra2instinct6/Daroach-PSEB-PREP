  Good morning, everyone! Welcome to Chapter 1 — Chemical Reactions and Equations,
  or in Punjabi: ਰਸਾਇਣਕ ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ ਅਤੇ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ.

SLIDE 2: Key Vocabulary (ਮੁੱਖ ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table with three columns: English | Punjabi | Meaning
  - Six terms: Reactant, Product, Equation, Exothermic, Endothermic, Precipitate
  - Punjabi equivalents: ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ, ਉਤਪਾਦ, ਸਮੀਕਰਣ, ਤਾਪ ਨਿਕਾਸੀ, ਤਾਪ ਸੋਖੀ, ਅਵਖੇਪ

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we dive into the chemistry, let us build our vocabulary. These six words will
  appear again and again throughout the chapter, so you must know them cold.

  First: Reactant — ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ. A reactant is the starting substance. It is what you
  put INTO the reaction. Think of it as the ingredient.

  Second: Product — ਉਤਪਾਦ. The product is the new substance formed AFTER the reaction.
  It is what you get OUT of the reaction.

  Third: Equation — ਸਮੀਕਰਣ. A chemical equation is the mathematical representation
  of a reaction. It uses symbols and formulas to show what reacts and what is formed.

  Fourth: Exothermic — ਤਾਪ ਨਿਕਾਸੀ. Exothermic reactions RELEASE heat. The reaction
  gives out energy to the surroundings. Burning wood is exothermic — you feel the heat.

  Fifth: Endothermic — ਤਾਪ ਸੋਖੀ. Endothermic reactions ABSORB heat. They take energy
  in from the surroundings. When you cool something down using a reaction, that is
  endothermic.

  Sixth: Precipitate — ਅਵਖੇਪ. A precipitate is an insoluble solid that forms inside
  a liquid during a reaction. It appears as a cloudiness or a solid settling at the
  bottom.

  Keep these six words in your mind — they are the language of this chapter.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Reactant (ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ) = what goes IN; Product (ਉਤਪਾਦ) = what comes OUT.
  - Exothermic (ਤਾਪ ਨਿਕਾਸੀ) releases heat; Endothermic (ਤਾਪ ਸੋਖੀ) absorbs heat.
  - Precipitate (ਅਵਖੇਪ) is a solid formed inside a liquid reaction mixture.

BOARD TIP: "Exothermic vs Endothermic" is a very common 1-mark definition question — memorise both.

SLIDE 3: Vocabulary Continued (ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ ਜਾਰੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table: Catalyst (ਉਤਪ੍ਰੇਰਕ), Aqueous (ਜਲਮਈ), Redox (ਰੈਡੌਕਸ),
    Oxidation (ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ), Reduction (ਲਘੂਕਰਨ)
  - Etymology box: Exo (Exit/ਬਾਹਰ) + Thermic (Heat/ਤਾਪ); Endo (Enter/ਅੰਦਰ) + Thermic

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us continue with five more important vocabulary words.

  Catalyst — ਉਤਪ੍ਰੇਰਕ. A catalyst is a substance that speeds up a chemical reaction
  without itself being consumed or permanently changed. It is like a helpful friend
  who arranges a meeting between two people — the meeting happens faster because of
  them, but they themselves are unchanged. MnO₂ is a classic example used in the
  decomposition of hydrogen peroxide.

  Aqueous — ਜਲਮਈ. This simply means "dissolved in water." When you see (aq) written
  next to a substance in an equation, it means that substance is dissolved in water.

  Redox — ਰੈਡੌਕਸ. This is short for "Reduction-Oxidation." Oxidation and reduction
  always happen together in the same reaction. You cannot have one without the other.
  We will study this in detail later in the chapter.

  Oxidation — ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ. Oxidation means gaining oxygen OR losing hydrogen.

  Reduction — ਲਘੂਕਰਨ. Reduction means losing oxygen OR gaining hydrogen. Reduction
  is the opposite of oxidation.

  Now, look at this clever trick on screen. The word "Exothermic" comes from "Exo"
  meaning exit, and "Thermic" meaning heat — so heat EXITS the reaction. "Endothermic"
  comes from "Endo" meaning enter — heat ENTERS the reaction. If you remember the
  etymology, you will never confuse these two words again!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Catalyst (ਉਤਪ੍ਰੇਰਕ) speeds up a reaction but is NOT used up.
  - Oxidation = gain O or lose H; Reduction = lose O or gain H.
  - Redox = both happening simultaneously in one reaction.
  - Exo = exit (heat out); Endo = enter (heat in). Use this to remember forever.

BOARD TIP: Mnemonic for Redox — "OIL RIG": Oxidation Is Loss (of electrons/hydrogen), Reduction Is Gain.

SLIDE 4: Chemical Reactions (ਰਸਾਇਣਕ ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: "A chemical reaction means new substances are formed."
  - Table: Reactants (ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ) vs. Products (ਉਤਪਾਦ)
  - Example: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  So, what exactly is a chemical reaction? Very simply: a chemical reaction is a
  process in which NEW substances are formed — ਨਵੇਂ ਪਦਾਰਥ ਬਣਦੇ ਹਨ. The original
  substances are called Reactants (ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ) — they are the starting materials, the
  things that react together. The new substances produced are called Products (ਉਤਪਾਦ).

  Look at the example on screen: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide.
  Magnesium and Oxygen are the reactants — they exist before the reaction. Magnesium
  oxide is the product — it is the brand new substance formed after the reaction.
  Notice that Magnesium oxide is completely different from both Magnesium and Oxygen.
  It is a new substance with new properties. That is the hallmark of a chemical reaction.

  How do we know a chemical reaction has occurred? We look for signs: a change in
  colour, a change in smell, the release of a gas (bubbles), the formation of a
  precipitate, or a change in temperature. When any of these happen, a chemical
  reaction is likely taking place.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - In a chemical reaction, NEW substances (products) are always formed.
  - Reactants (ਅਭਿਕਾਰਕ) go in; Products (ਉਤਪਾਦ) come out.
  - Signs of a chemical reaction: colour change, gas evolved, temperature change, precipitate formed.
  - Example: Mg + O₂ → MgO (Magnesium combines with oxygen to form magnesium oxide).

BOARD TIP: "List four observations that indicate a chemical reaction has occurred" — a very common 2-mark question.

SLIDE 5: Word Equation vs. Skeletal Equation
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two types of chemical equations contrasted
  - Word Equation (ਸ਼ਬਦ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ): Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium oxide
  - Skeletal Equation (ਢਾਂਚਾ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ): Mg + O₂ → MgO
  - Note that the skeletal equation is NOT yet balanced

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A chemical reaction can be written in two different ways, and you must know both.

  The first way is a Word Equation — ਸ਼ਬਦ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ. In a word equation, we write
  the full names of all the chemicals. For example: Magnesium + Oxygen → Magnesium
  oxide. This is easy to read and understand, but it is not very scientific or precise.

  The second way is a Skeletal Equation — ਢਾਂਚਾ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ. Here, we replace the names
  with chemical formulas. So our example becomes: Mg + O₂ → MgO. This is more
  compact and scientific. However — and this is very important — a skeletal equation
  is NOT balanced yet. It just gives us the "skeleton" or framework of the reaction.
  It tells us WHICH substances react and WHICH substances are formed, but the number
  of atoms on the left and right sides may not be equal yet.

  The arrow (→) in the equation means "reacts to form" or "yields." Reactants are
  always written on the LEFT side of the arrow, and products are always on the RIGHT.

  After writing the skeletal equation, our next job is to BALANCE it — which is what
  we will learn on the very next slides.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Word equation uses full names; Skeletal equation uses chemical symbols/formulas.
  - Reactants are on the left of →; Products are on the right.
  - A Skeletal equation (ਢਾਂਚਾ ਸਮੀਕਰਣ) is NOT balanced — it is just the first step.
  - Balancing is the process of making atom counts equal on both sides.

BOARD TIP: Always label which type of equation you are writing in exam answers. "This is a skeletal equation" or "This is a balanced equation."

SLIDE 6: Reading Comprehension — The Philosophy of Balancing
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story passage about Antoine Lavoisier and the Law of Conservation of Mass
  - Audio controls: ▶️ Read to Me / ⏹️ Stop
  - Key idea: matter is neither created nor destroyed; atoms are merely rearranged

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we have a beautiful reading comprehension passage, and I want you to really
  think about this story — it explains WHY we balance equations in the first place.

  In the late 18th century, a French chemist named Antoine Lavoisier conducted a
  famous experiment. He sealed a container and carefully weighed everything before
  and after a violent chemical reaction — including burning. What he found was
  extraordinary: the total mass of the sealed container never changed. No matter how
  dramatic the reaction, mass was conserved.

  This discovery became what we now call the Law of Conservation of Mass: "Matter
  can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction." In Punjabi terms,
  ਪਦਾਰਥ ਨਾ ਤਾਂ ਬਣਾਇਆ ਜਾ ਸਕਦਾ ਹੈ ਅਤੇ ਨਾ ਹੀ ਨਸ਼ਟ ਕੀਤਾ ਜਾ ਸਕਦਾ ਹੈ.

  Think about what this means for chemical equations. If you start with 10 atoms of
  iron, you must end with exactly 10 atoms of iron. The atoms do not disappear — they
  are simply rearranged into new molecules. This is EXACTLY why we balance equations.
  An unbalanced equation violates Lavoisier's law — it is scientifically wrong. Every
  balanced equation is a tribute to this 250-year-old discovery.

  Read through this passage carefully. Understanding the "why" behind balancing makes
  the process feel logical rather than mechanical.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Antoine Lavoisier discovered the Law of Conservation of Mass.
  - Matter cannot be created or destroyed — only rearranged.
  - We MUST balance equations to obey this fundamental law.
  - An unbalanced equation is scientifically incorrect.

BOARD TIP: "State the Law of Conservation of Mass" is a direct 1-mark question — memorise the exact wording.

SLIDE 7: Balancing Equations — Pro Tips (ਸਮੀਕਰਣ ਸੰਤੁਲਿਤ ਕਰਨ ਦੇ ਨੁਕਤੇ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four step-by-step tips for balancing equations
  - Law of Conservation of Mass statement
  - Key rule: never change subscripts inside formulas, only add coefficients

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Balancing equations is a GUARANTEED question on the board exam. Let me give you
  the exact method that works every time.

  Step 1: Draw a box around each chemical formula. This is a mental trick — it reminds
  you that you can NEVER change anything inside the box. You cannot change H₂O to H₃O,
  for example. The only thing you can change is the big number written IN FRONT of the
  formula. That big number is called the coefficient.

  Step 2: Start with the atom that appears the most. Often this is a metal, but
  sometimes it is oxygen or hydrogen if they appear in multiple places.

  Step 3: Follow this order — balance metals first, then non-metals, then oxygen,
  and hydrogen last. Hydrogen and oxygen are usually balanced at the end because
  they appear in many compounds.

  Step 4: Double check. Count every atom on the left-hand side (LHS) and every atom
  on the right-hand side (RHS). They must match completely for each element.

  Remember the golden rule: we balance to obey the Law of Conservation of Mass —
  "Matter can neither be created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction." Every atom
  that enters the reaction as a reactant must appear somewhere in the products.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - NEVER change subscripts (numbers inside formulas) — only change coefficients (numbers in front).
  - Balancing order: Metals → Non-metals → Oxygen → Hydrogen.
  - Always verify by counting atoms on both sides after balancing.
  - Balancing is mandatory — an unbalanced equation is a wrong equation.

BOARD TIP: The most common mistake students make is changing the subscript (e.g., writing H₃ instead of H₂) — this changes the substance entirely and is always wrong.

SLIDE 8: Interactive Simulator — Balancing Equations (ਸਮੀਕਰਣ ਸੰਤੁਲਿਤ ਕਰੋ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive equation: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂
  - Coefficient sliders for each substance
  - Real-time atom counts for Fe, H, O on both LHS and RHS
  - Current status: UNBALANCED ❌

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we have an interactive balancing simulator. This is a hands-on activity, so
  let us work through it together as a class.

  The unbalanced equation on screen is: Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂. Right now, the
  atom counts are unequal on both sides, which is why it shows UNBALANCED ❌.

  Your job is to adjust the coefficients — the big numbers placed in front of each
  formula — until every element has equal atoms on both sides.

  Let us think through this together. The product Fe₃O₄ tells us we need 3 iron
  atoms on the left, so we put a coefficient of 3 in front of Fe. Fe₃O₄ has 4 oxygen
  atoms, so we need 4 H₂O molecules on the left to supply 4 oxygen atoms. That gives
  us 4 × 2 = 8 hydrogen atoms on the left, so we need a coefficient of 4 in front of
  H₂ on the right. The balanced equation is: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂.

  Let each student try the simulator independently. Adjust the coefficients using the
  controls and watch the atom counts update in real time. Aim for the BALANCED ✅ message.

  Pause here — give students 3–5 minutes to work through the simulator themselves.
  Encourage them to use the step-by-step tips from the previous slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Balanced equation for this reaction: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂.
  - Only coefficients change — never the subscripts inside formulas.
  - Verify: Fe: 3=3 ✅, H: 8=8 ✅, O: 4=4 ✅.
  - Practice balancing on paper after completing the simulator.

BOARD TIP: This exact reaction (Fe + H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + H₂) has appeared in PSEB exams. Memorise the balanced form: 3Fe + 4H₂O → Fe₃O₄ + 4H₂.

SLIDE 9: Concept Map — Reaction Architecture (ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ ਦਾ ਨਕਸ਼ਾ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Central node: "Chemical Reactions"
  - Four branches: Combination (ਸੰਜੋਜਨ) A+B→AB, Decomposition (ਅਪਘਟਨ) AB→A+B,
    Displacement (ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ) A+BC→AC+B, Double Displacement (ਦੋਹਰਾ ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ) AB+CD→AD+CB

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we come to the four main types of chemical reactions. This concept map gives
  you a beautiful bird's-eye view. Study the map carefully — this is the architecture
  of all chemical reactions.

  Type 1: Combination — ਸੰਜੋਜਨ. The general form is A + B → AB. Two or more
  substances combine to form a SINGLE new substance. Think of it as two things joining
  together. Example: Magnesium burns in oxygen to form Magnesium oxide — 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO.

  Type 2: Decomposition — ਅਪਘਟਨ. The general form is AB → A + B. A SINGLE reactant
  breaks down into two or more products. It is the exact opposite of combination.
  Example: Limestone heated to form quicklime and CO₂.

  Type 3: Displacement — ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ. The general form is A + BC → AC + B. A more
  reactive element kicks out a less reactive element from its compound and takes its
  place. Example: Iron displaces copper from copper sulphate solution.

  Type 4: Double Displacement — ਦੋਹਰਾ ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ. The general form is AB + CD → AD + CB.
  Two compounds exchange their ions. It is like two couples swapping partners. Example:
  Barium chloride + sodium sulphate → barium sulphate + sodium chloride.

  Keep this map in your mind. When you see any reaction in the exam, your first
  question should be: which of these four types is it?

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Four types: Combination, Decomposition, Displacement, Double Displacement.
  - Combination: two things join → one product; Decomposition: one thing breaks → multiple products.
  - Displacement involves a MORE reactive element replacing a LESS reactive one.
  - Double Displacement: two compounds exchange ions — often produces a precipitate or gas.

BOARD TIP: "Classify the given reaction and give its general form" is a 2-mark question. Always write both the name AND the general formula (e.g., A + B → AB).

SLIDE 10: Physical States in Equations (ਭੌਤਿਕ ਅਵਸਥਾਵਾਂ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - State symbols: (s) Solid, (l) Liquid, (g) Gas, (aq) Aqueous
  - Punjabi translations for each state
  - Example equation: Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s)↓ + H₂O(l)
  - Note about ↓ (precipitate) and ↑ (gas) arrows

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A chemical equation becomes much more informative when we include the physical
  states of every substance. Scientists use four symbols for this.

  (s) stands for Solid — ਠੋਸ. The substance is in solid form.
  (l) stands for Liquid — ਤਰਲ. The substance is in liquid form.
  (g) stands for Gas — ਗੈਸ. The substance is a gas.
  (aq) stands for Aqueous — ਜਲਮਈ ਘੋਲ. The substance is dissolved in water.

  Now look at the example: Ca(OH)₂(aq) + CO₂(g) → CaCO₃(s)↓ + H₂O(l).
  Read it like a story: Calcium hydroxide, which is dissolved in water, reacts with
  carbon dioxide gas to produce calcium carbonate as a solid and water as a liquid.

  Notice the downward arrow ↓ after CaCO₃(s). This arrow indicates a PRECIPITATE —
  a solid that has formed inside a liquid reaction mixture. It sinks to the bottom.
  If you see an upward arrow ↑ after a formula, it means a GAS has been produced and
  is escaping from the reaction mixture.

  Including state symbols makes your equation complete and scientifically accurate.
  In board exams, writing state symbols often earns you extra marks in long-answer questions.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Four state symbols: (s) solid, (l) liquid, (g) gas, (aq) aqueous.
  - ↓ after a formula = precipitate formed; ↑ after a formula = gas evolved.
  - Always include state symbols in balanced equations for full marks.
  - (aq) means dissolved in water — not the same as (l) liquid.

BOARD TIP: Forgetting (aq) vs (l) is a very common mistake. NaCl(aq) is dissolved salt in water; H₂O(l) is liquid water. They are different!

SLIDE 11: Reaction Prediction Flowchart (ਕਿਰਿਆ ਦੀ ਭਵਿੱਖਬਾਣੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four decision questions leading to reaction type identification
  - "Are two elements combining into one?" → Combination
  - "Is there only ONE reactant being heated?" → Decomposition
  - "Is a single element reacting with a compound?" → Displacement
  - "Are two compounds exchanging ions?" → Double Displacement

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Instead of just memorising reaction types, let me give you a thinking tool — a
  flowchart you can use in the exam when you need to classify any reaction.

  Ask yourself these four questions in order:

  Question 1: Are two or more elements or compounds combining to form ONE single
  product? If yes — it is a Combination reaction (ਸੰਜੋਜਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ).

  Question 2: Is there only ONE reactant, and is it being broken down, especially
  by heat, electricity, or light? If yes — it is a Decomposition reaction
  (ਅਪਘਟਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ).

  Question 3: Is a single uncombined element reacting with a compound? Does the
  element substitute into the compound and push out another element? If yes — it is
  a Displacement reaction (ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ).

  Question 4: Are there two compounds as reactants, and do they exchange partners to
  form two new compounds? If yes — it is a Double Displacement reaction
  (ਦੋਹਰਾ ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ).

  This flowchart approach means you do not need to memorise every single example.
  You can REASON your way to the correct answer. Practice applying these four
  questions to the equations we have seen so far, and try the interactive activity
  on the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Use these four questions to classify ANY unfamiliar reaction in the exam.
  - One product → Combination; One reactant → Decomposition.
  - Single element + compound → Displacement; Two compounds swapping → Double Displacement.
  - Always justify your classification with a reason, not just the name.

BOARD TIP: In 3-mark questions, state the reaction type, write the general formula, and give the example equation with state symbols for full marks.

SLIDE 13: Decomposition Specifics (3 Marks) — PSEB 2018, 2021, 2023
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Three sub-types of decomposition: Thermal, Electrolytic, Photolytic
  - Thermal: CaCO₃(s) + Heat → CaO(s) + CO₂(g)
  - Electrolytic: 2H₂O(l) + Electricity → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g)
  - Photolytic: 2AgCl(s) + Sunlight → 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g)
  - Board Exam Pitfall warning box

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Pay very close attention to this slide. This topic has appeared in the PSEB board
  exam in 2018, 2021, and 2023 — it is one of the most frequently tested areas in
  this entire chapter.

  Decomposition reactions are endothermic — they always ABSORB energy to break chemical
  bonds. But the energy can come from three different sources, and each gives a
  different TYPE of decomposition.

  Type 1: Thermal Decomposition (using Heat). When limestone — calcium carbonate —
  is heated strongly, it breaks down into quicklime and carbon dioxide gas.
  CaCO₃(s) + Heat → CaO(s) + CO₂(g).
  This reaction is the basis of the cement and lime industry.

  Type 2: Electrolytic Decomposition (using Electricity). When electricity is passed
  through water, it splits into hydrogen gas and oxygen gas. This process is called
  electrolysis.
  2H₂O(l) + Electricity → 2H₂(g) + O₂(g).

  Type 3: Photolytic Decomposition (using Light). When silver chloride is exposed to
  sunlight, it decomposes into silver metal and chlorine gas. The silver turns grey.
  2AgCl(s) + Sunlight → 2Ag(s) + Cl₂(g).
  This reaction was historically used in black-and-white photography — the film was
  coated with silver chloride, and wherever light hit it, silver metal formed,
  creating the dark areas of the photograph.

  Now, here is the critical board exam pitfall shown in the red box: If the exam
  shows you the equation 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ and asks you to identify the reaction,
  do NOT write just "Decomposition." You MUST write "Photolytic Decomposition" to
  get full marks. Specifying the energy source is required!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Three types: Thermal (heat), Electrolytic (electricity), Photolytic (light).
  - All decomposition reactions are ENDOTHERMIC.
  - CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂ (thermal); 2H₂O → 2H₂ + O₂ (electrolytic); 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂ (photolytic).
  - ALWAYS name the specific type — "Photolytic Decomposition," not just "Decomposition."

BOARD TIP: This is a guaranteed 3-mark question. Write: reaction name + energy source + balanced equation with state symbols. That's your full-mark formula.

SLIDE 14: Oxidation & Reduction (ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ ਅਤੇ ਲਘੂਕਰਨ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table: Oxidation vs. Reduction
  - Oxidation: Gains Oxygen (+O) OR Loses Hydrogen (-H)
  - Reduction: Loses Oxygen (-O) OR Gains Hydrogen (+H)
  - Agent trick: the oxidized substance = Reducing Agent; the reduced substance = Oxidizing Agent

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we come to one of the most conceptually rich topics in this chapter: Oxidation
  and Reduction — ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ ਅਤੇ ਲਘੂਕਰਨ.

  These two processes always occur together in the same reaction — that is why we
  call them Redox reactions (ਰੈਡੌਕਸ ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ).

  Oxidation — ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ — is defined in two ways:
  Either a substance GAINS oxygen, or it LOSES hydrogen. If you see a substance
  pick up an oxygen atom during the reaction, that substance is being oxidised.

  Reduction — ਲਘੂਕਰਨ — is the exact opposite:
  Either a substance LOSES oxygen, or it GAINS hydrogen.

  These definitions are perfectly symmetrical and opposite to each other. Learn them
  as a pair.

  Now, here is a very tricky concept that students often get wrong — the agents.
  The substance that gets OXIDISED in a reaction is called the REDUCING agent.
  The substance that gets REDUCED is called the OXIDISING agent.
  Why? Because the reducing agent is doing the work of REDUCING the other substance
  — it donates electrons or oxygen to the other substance. Similarly, the oxidising
  agent causes oxidation in the other substance.

  In short: they are opposites. The thing being oxidised helps reduce something else
  — so it is the reducing agent. Remember this relationship!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Oxidation = gain O or lose H; Reduction = lose O or gain H.
  - Oxidation and reduction always happen simultaneously — they are always paired.
  - The substance that is OXIDISED = the Reducing Agent (ਅਪਚਾਇਕ ਕਰਕ).
  - The substance that is REDUCED = the Oxidising Agent (ਆਕਸੀਕਰਕ ਕਰਕ). They are opposites!

BOARD TIP: "Identify the oxidising agent and reducing agent in the given reaction" is a frequent 2-mark question. Always identify what gains and what loses oxygen first.

SLIDE 15: Oxidation Tracking — Example
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Reaction: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O
  - Analysis: CuO loses oxygen → it is Reduced → CuO is the Oxidising Agent
  - Analysis: H₂ gains oxygen → it is Oxidised → H₂ is the Reducing Agent

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us apply the rules of oxidation and reduction to a specific example.
  This type of question — identify the oxidised/reduced substance and the agents —
  appears almost every year in the board exam.

  The reaction is: CuO + H₂ → Cu + H₂O.

  Step 1: Look at CuO. Before the reaction, copper has oxygen attached to it (CuO).
  After the reaction, copper is just Cu — it has LOST its oxygen. When a substance
  loses oxygen, it is REDUCED. So CuO is reduced.

  Step 2: Because CuO is reduced, it is the OXIDISING agent. It caused something
  else to be oxidised.

  Step 3: Look at H₂. Before the reaction, hydrogen has no oxygen. After the
  reaction, it becomes H₂O — it has GAINED oxygen. When a substance gains oxygen,
  it is OXIDISED. So H₂ is oxidised.

  Step 4: Because H₂ is oxidised, it is the REDUCING agent. It caused CuO to be
  reduced.

  This is the complete, four-step analysis you should write in the exam. Notice
  how neatly the two processes happen at the same time — CuO gives its oxygen to H₂.
  One loses, the other gains. That is the essence of a Redox reaction.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - CuO loses O → it is REDUCED → CuO is the Oxidising Agent.
  - H₂ gains O → it is OXIDISED → H₂ is the Reducing Agent.
  - Always state BOTH the process (oxidised/reduced) AND the role (agent) in exam answers.
  - In this reaction, CuO provides oxygen to H₂ — it is the oxygen donor = oxidising agent.

BOARD TIP: Write your analysis in a table format in the exam: Substance | Change | Process | Role as Agent. This earns maximum marks for 3–4 mark questions.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):

SLIDE 20: Chemical Formula Drill (ਰਸਾਇਣਕ ਸੂਤਰ ਲਿਖਣਾ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Three-step interactive drill for writing Aluminum Oxide formula
  - Step 1: Find valency of Aluminum (Al)
  - Step 2: Find valency of Oxygen (O)
  - Step 3: Cross the valencies to get the final formula Al₂O₃
  - Check Answer / Next Step buttons

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we have an interactive formula-writing drill. Let us work through it step
  by step together.

  The goal is to write the correct chemical formula for Aluminum Oxide.

  Step 1: What is the valency of Aluminum? Aluminum (Al) has a valency of 3. That
  means each aluminum atom forms 3 bonds.

  Step 2: What is the valency of Oxygen? Oxygen (O) has a valency of 2. Each
  oxygen atom forms 2 bonds.

  Step 3: Now we use the "cross-multiplication" or "criss-cross" method. Take the
  valency of aluminum (3) and make it the SUBSCRIPT of oxygen. Take the valency of
  oxygen (2) and make it the SUBSCRIPT of aluminum. So aluminum gets subscript 2
  and oxygen gets subscript 3. The final formula is Al₂O₃ — Aluminum Oxide.

  Let students try each step in the interactive drill and check their answers.
  Pause at each step and confirm the answer before moving on.

  After finishing, ask students to try the same method for other common compounds:
  Magnesium Oxide (Mg = valency 2, O = valency 2 → MgO), or Calcium Chloride
  (Ca = valency 2, Cl = valency 1 → CaCl₂). The cross-valency method works for all
  ionic compounds.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Valency of Aluminum (Al) = 3; Valency of Oxygen (O) = 2.
  - Criss-cross method: swap valencies as subscripts → Al₂O₃.
  - Reduce the ratio if possible (e.g., Ca₂O₂ reduces to CaO).
  - Final answer: Aluminum Oxide = Al₂O₃.

BOARD TIP: Formula writing questions appear in the 1-mark section. Master the criss-cross method for all common elements using their valencies.

SLIDE 24: Chapter 1 Quick Recap
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Six summary bullet points covering all major topics of Chapter 1
  - Reaction signs, equation balancing, four reaction types, redox

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we move to the revision activities, let us do a complete rapid recap of
  everything we have covered in Chapter 1.

  Point 1: Chemical reactions involve observable changes — change in state, colour,
  smell, temperature, or the formation of gas or precipitate. If you see any of
  these, a chemical reaction has occurred.

  Point 2: Chemical equations MUST be balanced to obey the Law of Conservation of
  Mass. Equal atoms of each element on both sides. Never change subscripts — only
  add coefficients.

  Point 3: Combination reaction: A + B → AB. Two or more substances join to form
  one product. Example: 2Mg + O₂ → 2MgO.

  Point 4: Decomposition reaction: AB → A + B. One substance breaks down into
  multiple products. Requires energy (heat, electricity, or light). Always endothermic.

  Point 5: Displacement reaction: A + BC → AC + B. A more reactive element replaces
  a less reactive one from its compound. Example: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu.

  Point 6: Redox reactions involve simultaneous oxidation AND reduction. Oxidation =
  gain O or lose H. Reduction = lose O or gain H. The oxidised substance is the
  reducing agent, and the reduced substance is the oxidising agent.

  If you understand and can explain all six of these points, you are ready for the
  board exam on this chapter.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - All four reaction types with their general formulas must be memorised.
  - Balanced equations obey Conservation of Mass — never change subscripts.
  - Decomposition always needs energy input (endothermic).
  - Redox: the oxidised substance = reducing agent (they are OPPOSITES).

BOARD TIP: This recap slide is your revision checklist. If you cannot explain any of these six points from memory, go back and revise that section before the exam.

  and prevent oxidation of the fats and oils.

  Q5: What does Oxidation mean?
  Answer: Oxidation (ਆਕਸੀਕਰਨ) means gaining oxygen OR losing hydrogen during a
  chemical reaction.

  Q6: A substance that gives oxygen in a reaction is called an...?
  Answer: Oxidising Agent. It gives oxygen to another substance, causing that
  substance to be oxidised.

  Q7: AgCl turning grey in sunlight is used in what type of photography?
  Answer: Black and white photography. Silver chloride (AgCl) decomposes in light
  to form silver metal (Ag), which appears grey/dark on photographic film.

  Q8: What color is the Barium Sulphate (BaSO₄) precipitate?
  Answer: White. BaSO₄ is an insoluble white precipitate formed in double
  displacement reactions.

  Q9: What type of reaction involves the exchange of ions between reactants?
  Answer: Double Displacement reaction (ਦੋਹਰਾ ਵਿਸਥਾਪਨ). Two compounds exchange
  their ions to form two new compounds.

  Q10: What is the slow eating away of metals by air or moisture called?
  Answer: Corrosion (ਖੋਰਨ). For example, rusting of iron is corrosion.

  Q11: The chemical formula of marble is...?
  Answer: CaCO₃ — Calcium Carbonate. Marble is a form of calcium carbonate. This
  is also the formula of limestone and chalk.

  Q12: Photosynthesis is an example of what type of energy reaction?
  Answer: Endothermic (ਤਾਪ ਸੋਖੀ). Plants absorb light energy to produce glucose.

  Q13: What does the symbol (aq) mean in a chemical equation?
  Answer: Aqueous (ਜਲਮਈ ਘੋਲ) — the substance is dissolved in water.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - BaSO₄ precipitate is WHITE — a commonly asked colour.
  - Marble formula = CaCO₃ (same as limestone and chalk).
  - Nitrogen prevents rancidity by displacing oxygen in packaging.
  - (aq) = dissolved in water — not to be confused with (l) = liquid.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We end Chapter 1 with our final Quick Check — 12 multiple choice questions that
  are modelled on the style of actual PSEB board exam questions. Let me walk through

  Q1: Which gas prevents rancidity?
  A. Oxygen  B. Nitrogen  C. Carbon Dioxide
  Answer: B — Nitrogen. It is inert and displaces oxygen from food packaging.

  Q2: A chemical equation must satisfy the law of:
  A. Conservation of Mass  B. Conservation of Energy  C. Constant Proportions
  Answer: A — Conservation of Mass. We balance equations to ensure no atoms are
  created or destroyed.

  Q3: Fe + CuSO₄ → FeSO₄ + Cu is an example of:
  A. Combination  B. Decomposition  C. Displacement
  Answer: C — Displacement. Iron (Fe) is more reactive than copper and displaces
  copper from copper sulphate solution.

  Q4: The decomposition of limestone produces quicklime and which gas?
  A. Oxygen  B. Hydrogen  C. Carbon Dioxide
  Answer: C — Carbon Dioxide. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. CaO is quicklime; CO₂ is the gas.

  Q5: Which of the following is a decomposition reaction?
  A. CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂  B. 2H₂ + O₂ → 2H₂O  C. C + O₂ → CO₂
  Answer: A — CaCO₃ → CaO + CO₂. One reactant breaks into multiple products.
  Options B and C are combination reactions.

  Q6: Respiration is an example of which type of reaction?
  A. Endothermic  B. Exothermic  C. Displacement
  Answer: B — Exothermic. Respiration releases energy — that is why your body stays
  warm and your muscles work.

  Q7: When AgCl is exposed to sunlight, it turns grey due to the formation of:
  A. Chlorine gas  B. Silver metal  C. Silver Oxide
  Answer: B — Silver metal. 2AgCl → 2Ag + Cl₂. The grey colour comes from
  fine particles of silver metal (Ag) forming on the surface.

  Q8: Which gas is evolved when dilute HCl is added to zinc granules?
  A. Oxygen  B. Hydrogen  C. Chlorine
  Answer: B — Hydrogen. Zn + 2HCl → ZnCl₂ + H₂↑. Zinc displaces hydrogen from
  hydrochloric acid. This is a displacement reaction.

  Q9: The chemical formula of rust is:
  A. Fe₂O₃  B. FeCO₃  C. Fe₂O₃·xH₂O
  Answer: C — Fe₂O₃·xH₂O. Rust is hydrated iron oxide. Option A (Fe₂O₃) is
  anhydrous iron oxide — not rust. The ·xH₂O part (water of crystallisation) is
  essential.

  Q10: In the reaction PbO + C → Pb + CO, which substance is oxidised?
  A. PbO  B. C  C. Pb
  Answer: B — Carbon (C). Carbon gains oxygen (from PbO) to form CO — it is
  oxidised. PbO loses oxygen to become Pb — PbO is reduced. Carbon is the
  reducing agent here.

  Q11: What is the color of the precipitate formed when Barium Chloride reacts
  with Sodium Sulphate?
  A. Yellow  B. White  C. Green
  Answer: B — White. BaCl₂ + Na₂SO₄ → BaSO₄↓ + 2NaCl. Barium sulphate (BaSO₄)
  is a white precipitate.

  Q12: A substance that gains oxygen during a reaction is said to be:
  A. Reduced  B. Oxidised  C. Precipitated
  Answer: B — Oxidised. Gaining oxygen = oxidation. This is the basic definition.

  - BaSO₄ precipitate is WHITE (Q11).
