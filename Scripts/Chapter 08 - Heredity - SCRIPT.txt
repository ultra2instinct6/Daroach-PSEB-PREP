SLIDE 2: Core Vocabulary (ਮੁੱਖ ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Eight key terms with Punjabi translations and pronunciation guides:
    Trait (ਲੱਛਣ), Gene (ਜੀਨ), Dominant (ਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ), Recessive (ਅਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ),
    Genotype (ਜੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ), Phenotype (ਫੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ),
    Homozygous (ਹੋਮੋਜ਼ਾਈਗਸ), Heterozygous (ਹੈਟਰੋਜ਼ਾਈਗਸ)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we dive into Mendel's experiments, let's lock in our vocabulary. These
  eight words are the building blocks of genetics. If you don't know these terms,
  the rest of the chapter won't make sense — so pay close attention.

  A "Trait" — in Punjabi ਲੱਛਣ (lacchan) — is simply any characteristic of a living
  thing. Height, eye color, ear shape — all of these are traits.

  A "Gene" — ਜੀਨ — is a specific section of DNA that carries the instruction for
  one particular trait. Think of it as one chapter in the instruction manual for
  building a body.

  "Dominant" — ਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ (prabhavi) — describes the stronger trait that shows up
  whenever it is present, even if only one copy exists.

  "Recessive" — ਅਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ (aprabhavi) — is the weaker trait. It only shows up when
  two copies of it are present. If a dominant gene is alongside it, the recessive
  trait is completely hidden.

  "Genotype" — ਜੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ — is the actual genetic code — the letters you write, like
  TT, Tt, or tt.

  "Phenotype" — ਫੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ — is what you can actually see — tall or short, round or
  wrinkled. It is the visible result of the genotype.

  "Homozygous" — ਹੋਮੋਜ਼ਾਈਗਸ — means both alleles for a trait are the same, either
  TT or tt.

  "Heterozygous" — ਹੈਟਰੋਜ਼ਾਈਗਸ — means the two alleles are different, like Tt.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Dominant (ਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ) = shown even with one copy; Recessive (ਅਪ੍ਰਭਾਵੀ) = only shown with two copies.
  - Genotype = letters (TT, Tt, tt); Phenotype = what you see (Tall or Short).
  - Homozygous = same alleles (TT or tt); Heterozygous = different alleles (Tt).

BOARD TIP:  Students frequently mix up Genotype and Phenotype — use the memory hook: "Geno = Genes (letters); Pheno = Physical (what you see)."

SLIDE 3: More Genetics Terms (ਹੋਰ ਸ਼ਬਦ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Seven more terms: Heredity (ਵੰਸ਼ਾਗਤੀ), Variation (ਭਿੰਨਤਾ), Allele (ਏਲੀਲ),
    Chromosome (ਕ੍ਰੋਮੋਸੋਮ), F1 Generation (ਪਹਿਲੀ ਪੀੜ੍ਹੀ),
    F2 Generation (ਦੂਜੀ ਪੀੜ੍ਹੀ), Punnett Square (ਪਨੇਟ ਵਰਗ)
  - Each term has a brief Punjabi definition and pronunciation guide.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We have seven more important terms on this slide. Let's go through them one by one.

  "Heredity" — ਵੰਸ਼ਾਗਤੀ (vanshagti) — is the process by which traits are transmitted
  from parents to their offspring. Simply put: how characteristics move from one
  generation to the next.

  "Variation" — ਭਿੰਨਤਾ (bhinnta) — refers to the differences that appear among
  individuals of the same species, even within the same family. Siblings may share
  traits but they are not identical — those differences are variation.

  "Allele" — ਏਲੀਲ (a-leel) — is a specific version of a gene. For the height gene,
  the tall version (T) and the short version (t) are two alleles of the same gene.

  "Chromosome" — ਕ੍ਰੋਮੋਸੋਮ — is a thread-like structure made of DNA that is found
  inside the nucleus of every cell. Genes are located on chromosomes.

  "F1 Generation" — ਪਹਿਲੀ ਪੀੜ੍ਹੀ (pahili piri) — stands for "First Filial
  Generation." It is the first set of offspring produced when two parent plants are
  crossed.

  "F2 Generation" — ਦੂਜੀ ਪੀੜ੍ਹੀ — is the second generation, produced when F1 plants
  are allowed to self-pollinate or are crossed with each other.

  Finally, the "Punnett Square" — ਪਨੇਟ ਵਰਗ — is a grid-shaped tool used to predict
  the possible genotypes and phenotypes of offspring from a cross. We will use it
  a lot in this chapter!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - F1 = first generation offspring from the original (P) cross.
  - F2 = second generation from the self-cross of F1 plants.
  - Punnett Square is the prediction tool — know how to fill it in.
  - Allele = a form of a gene (T and t are two alleles of the height gene).

BOARD TIP:  "F1" and "F2" stand for "Filial" (from the Latin word for child/offspring) — mentioning this in a board answer shows understanding.

SLIDE 4: Reading — The Monk and the Peas
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story passage about Gregor Mendel's experiments in a monastery garden (1860s).
  - Read-aloud passage describing how Mendel crossed tall and short pea plants and
    discovered dominant and recessive "hidden factors" (genes).
  - Play/stop button for audio narration.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let's take a moment to read about the man who started it all — Gregor Mendel. In
  the 1860s, in the quiet garden of a monastery in Austria, a monk named Gregor
  Mendel spent seven long years growing and counting thousands of pea plants. At
  the time, people thought it was strange work for a monk to do. But Mendel was
  quietly uncovering one of the deepest secrets of life.

  Here is what he observed: when he crossed a tall pea plant with a short pea plant,
  every single offspring in the first generation came out tall. The shortness seemed
  to have completely vanished! But Mendel was not satisfied. He took those tall F1
  plants and bred them with each other. In the very next generation — the F2 — the
  short trait reappeared! And not randomly — it appeared in exactly about one out
  of every four plants.

  Mendel reasoned that traits must be carried by invisible "factors" — what we today
  call genes. He concluded that some factors are dominant (they show up whenever
  present) and some are recessive (they hide when a dominant factor is present, but
  they are never destroyed — they just wait). When two recessive factors come
  together, the hidden trait reappears.

  This careful observation and counting became the foundation of the entire science
  of genetics and heredity.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Mendel worked in a monastery garden in the 1860s with pea plants.
  - When he crossed Tall × Short, ALL F1 offspring were Tall (short was hidden).
  - In F2, the short trait reappeared in a 3:1 ratio.
  - He concluded traits are carried by "hidden factors" = genes; some dominant, some recessive.

BOARD TIP:  Mendel is called the "Father of Genetics." His work was ignored for decades and only recognized after his death.

SLIDE 5: Heredity & Variation — Definitions
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definitions of Heredity and Variation.
  - A two-column comparison table:
    Heredity (ਵੰਸ਼ਾਗਤੀ) vs. Variation (ਭਿੰਨਤਾ)
    — traits passed to next generation vs. differences between individuals
    — makes offspring resemble parents vs. helps species survive changing conditions

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us define our two most fundamental concepts for this chapter and understand
  how they differ.

  Heredity — ਵੰਸ਼ਾਗਤੀ — is the transmission of characters, or traits, from parents
  to their offspring. Because of heredity, children look like their parents. A child
  inherits the same blood group, similar facial features, or the same hair type as
  their parents. Heredity is what makes offspring resemble their parents.

  Variation — ਭਿੰਨਤਾ — refers to the differences that appear among offspring and
  between offspring and their parents. Even though siblings come from the same
  parents, they are not identical. One child may be taller, another may have a
  different eye color. These differences are variations.

  Now, why does variation matter? Variation is the basis of evolution. When conditions
  in the environment change, organisms with certain variations are better suited to
  survive. Over many generations, these useful variations become more common in the
  population. So variation is not just interesting — it is the engine that drives
  evolution and the survival of species.

  Remember: Heredity creates similarity; Variation creates difference. Both happen
  simultaneously in every generation.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Heredity = transmission of traits from parent to offspring (similarity).
  - Variation = differences among individuals (diversity).
  - Variation is the basis of evolution — it helps species adapt and survive.
  - These two processes work together in every generation.

BOARD TIP:  Frequent 2-mark question: "Distinguish between heredity and variation" — always include the evolution connection for variation.

SLIDE 6: Inherited vs. Acquired Traits
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Introductory sentence: "Not all characteristics can be passed to the next generation."
  - Two-column comparison table:
    Inherited Traits (ਜੱਦੀ ਲੱਛਣ) vs. Acquired Traits (ਗ੍ਰਹਿਣ ਕੀਤੇ ਲੱਛਣ)
    — passed through DNA vs. developed during lifetime due to environment
    — causes changes in germ cells vs. only affects somatic cells, DNA not changed
    — examples: eye color, blood group vs. learning to swim, a scar, cutting a mouse's tail

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is a very important distinction that often comes up in board exams. Not every
  characteristic you have can be passed on to your children. Let's understand why.

  Inherited traits — ਜੱਦੀ ਲੱਛਣ — are characteristics that are encoded in your DNA
  and passed from parents to offspring through the germ cells — that is, the sperm
  and egg cells. Examples include eye color and blood group. These traits are present
  from birth because the instructions for them were already written in your DNA when
  you were conceived.

  Acquired traits — ਗ੍ਰਹਿਣ ਕੀਤੇ ਲੱਛਣ — are characteristics that develop during a
  person's lifetime because of their experiences or environment. If you learn to
  swim, your muscles develop — but that muscular development is not written into
  your DNA. If you get a scar, your children will not be born with that scar.

  Here is the key biological reason: Acquired traits only affect somatic cells —
  the body cells. They do NOT change the DNA in the germ cells (sperm and eggs).
  Since heredity only passes what is in the germ cells' DNA, acquired traits cannot
  be inherited.

  A classic exam example: Weismann, a scientist, cut off the tails of mice for many
  generations. But the offspring were always born with full tails. Why? Because
  cutting the tail is an acquired change — it changes somatic cells, not germ cells.
  The DNA in the germ cells remained unchanged.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Inherited traits → encoded in DNA of germ cells → passed to offspring.
  - Acquired traits → changes only in somatic (body) cells → NOT passed to offspring.
  - Key rule: Only changes in germ cell DNA can be inherited.
  - Examples: Eye color = inherited; A scar = acquired.

BOARD TIP:  Frequent 3-mark question: "Why are acquired traits not inherited?" Always mention germ cells vs. somatic cells and DNA in your answer.

SLIDE 7: Genotype vs. Phenotype
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two flip-card style boxes:
    Genotype (ਜੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ) — "The Genetic Code" — actual genetic makeup, e.g., Tt or TT
    Phenotype (ਫੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ) — "The Physical Look" — visible expression, e.g., Tall or Short

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Of all the terms in this chapter, Genotype and Phenotype are the two that students
  most commonly confuse. Let's fix that right now with a very clear explanation.

  Genotype — ਜੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ — is the actual genetic code of an organism. It is what is
  written in the DNA. We represent genotype using letters. For example, TT, Tt, and
  tt are all genotypes for the height trait. You cannot see the genotype just by
  looking at a plant — you need to do genetic testing to know it.

  Phenotype — ਫੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ — is the physical, visible expression of those genes. It is
  what you can observe with your eyes. For example, "tall" or "short" are phenotypes.
  A plant that is TT (genotype) LOOKS tall (phenotype). A plant that is Tt (genotype)
  ALSO looks tall (phenotype) — because T is dominant.

  Here is the important connection: Two plants can have DIFFERENT genotypes but the
  SAME phenotype. TT and Tt both look tall — they have the same phenotype (tall) but
  different genotypes (TT vs. Tt). This is why a test cross is needed to figure out
  the hidden genotype.

  Memory trick: "Geno" sounds like "Genes" — it's the genetic code (letters).
  "Pheno" sounds like "Physical" — it's the physical appearance.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Genotype = letters (TT, Tt, tt) = the genetic code inside the organism.
  - Phenotype = what you see (Tall, Short) = the visible expression.
  - TT and Tt have the SAME phenotype (tall) but DIFFERENT genotypes.
  - You can observe phenotype; you cannot observe genotype directly.

BOARD TIP:  Most common mistake: writing "phenotype = Tt" — no! Tt is a genotype. Phenotype = "Tall." Never write letters as a phenotype answer.

SLIDE 8: Why Mendel Chose Pea Plants
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Gregor Mendel — "Father of Genetics"
  - Pea plant: Pisum sativum
  - Three reasons: clear contrasting traits, short life cycle and many seeds,
    can self-pollinate and be cross-pollinated by hand.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Gregor Mendel is known as the "Father of Genetics." He could have chosen any
  plant for his experiments, but he chose the garden pea — Pisum sativum. This was
  a brilliant choice, and here's why.

  First, pea plants have very clear, contrasting traits. Height is either tall OR
  short — not medium. Seeds are either round OR wrinkled. Flowers are either purple
  OR white. These sharp, either/or differences made it easy to count and categorize
  the offspring without any ambiguity.

  Second, pea plants have a short life cycle and produce a large number of seeds.
  Mendel was able to grow many generations quickly and analyze thousands of
  offspring, which gave his data statistical reliability. You can't trust a ratio
  based on just five plants — Mendel counted tens of thousands.

  Third, and most importantly, pea plants naturally self-pollinate — pollen from
  the same plant fertilizes its own eggs. This allowed Mendel to create "pure
  breeding" lines — plants that always produce the same trait generation after
  generation. But he could also manually cross two different plants by transferring
  pollen with a brush, giving him full control over which plants mated.

  These three features made pea plants the perfect experimental subject for
  discovering the laws of heredity.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Mendel chose Pisum sativum (garden pea) for his experiments.
  - Reasons: clear contrasting traits, short life cycle + many seeds, can self- and cross-pollinate.
  - Self-pollination allowed creation of pure-breeding lines.
  - Manual cross-pollination gave Mendel precise experimental control.

BOARD TIP:  Frequent short-answer: "Why did Mendel use pea plants?" — list all three reasons for full marks.

SLIDE 9: Mendel's Three Laws
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: "Mendel's Three Laws — Very Important"
  - Law 1: Law of Dominance
  - Law 2: Law of Segregation
  - Law 3: Law of Independent Assortment (with 9:3:3:1 reference)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Mendel's experiments led him to formulate three fundamental laws of inheritance.
  These laws are the heart of this chapter — know them, understand them, and be
  ready to explain each one in an exam.

  Law 1 — The Law of Dominance: When two organisms with contrasting traits are
  crossed, only the dominant trait appears in the F1 offspring. The recessive trait
  is completely hidden. For example, when a tall plant (TT) is crossed with a short
  plant (tt), all F1 plants are tall (Tt). The 't' allele for shortness is present,
  but it is dominated by 'T'. This law explains why some traits seem to disappear in
  the first generation — they don't disappear, they just go into hiding.

  Law 2 — The Law of Segregation: During gamete formation (when sperm or egg cells
  are made), the two alleles of a gene separate from each other. Each gamete receives
  only ONE allele. So a plant with genotype Tt produces some gametes carrying T and
  some carrying t — never both together. When fertilization happens, the pairs come
  together again in the offspring. This law explains why recessive traits can
  reappear in the F2 generation.

  Law 3 — The Law of Independent Assortment: When two or more traits are inherited
  together, each pair of alleles separates independently of every other pair during
  gamete formation. This is demonstrated by the dihybrid cross, which gives the
  famous 9:3:3:1 ratio in the F2 generation. This law means that the inheritance
  of one trait does not affect the inheritance of another trait.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Law of Dominance: Only dominant trait visible in F1 hybrid (Tt → Tall).
  - Law of Segregation: Alleles separate during gamete formation; each gamete gets only one allele.
  - Law of Independent Assortment: Different traits are inherited independently → 9:3:3:1 ratio.
  - All three laws together explain the results of Mendel's crosses.

BOARD TIP:  Very important for 5-mark questions — state each law by name, then explain it. The Law of Segregation is sometimes called "Law of Purity of Gametes."

SLIDE 10: Alleles & Genetic Symbols
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: An allele is one form of a gene. Height has two alleles: T (tall) and t (short).
  - Table of symbols and meanings:
    T = Dominant allele (Tall)
    t = Recessive allele (short)
    TT = Homozygous dominant — pure tall
    Tt = Heterozygous — hybrid tall
    tt = Homozygous recessive — pure short

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let's understand the symbolic language of genetics — because in this chapter,
  we write everything using letters, and you must know exactly what each letter
  combination means.

  An allele is one specific form of a gene. For the height gene in pea plants, there
  are two alleles: T (the tall allele) and t (the short allele). Capital T represents
  the dominant allele and lowercase t represents the recessive allele. This is a
  standard convention: capital letter for dominant, lowercase for recessive.

  Now, every organism has two copies of each gene — one from each parent. So for
  height, each pea plant has two alleles. Let's look at what the combinations mean:

  TT: This plant has two dominant alleles. It is homozygous dominant — a "pure tall"
  plant. All its gametes will carry T. No matter what it is crossed with, it can
  only pass a T to offspring.

  Tt: This plant has one dominant and one recessive allele. It is heterozygous. It
  looks tall because T is dominant, but it secretly carries a t. It is sometimes
  called a "hybrid tall" plant.

  tt: This plant has two recessive alleles. It is homozygous recessive — it will
  always appear short. There is no dominant T to mask the recessive t.

  So the phenotype "tall" can come from TWO different genotypes: TT or Tt. But the
  phenotype "short" can only come from one genotype: tt.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Capital letter = dominant allele; lowercase = recessive allele.
  - TT = pure tall (homozygous dominant); tt = pure short (homozygous recessive); Tt = hybrid tall (heterozygous).
  - "Tall" phenotype corresponds to two possible genotypes: TT or Tt.
  - "Short" phenotype corresponds to only one genotype: tt.

BOARD TIP:  Always use capital for dominant and lowercase for recessive when writing genetic symbols. Mixing them up costs marks.

SLIDE 11: Mendel's Monohybrid Cross (One Trait)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Monohybrid Cross — crossing one trait at a time.
  - P generation: Pure Tall (TT) × Pure Short (tt).
  - F1 generation: All Tt → 100% Tall.
  - F2 generation: Tt × Tt → 3 Tall : 1 Short (phenotypic ratio).
  - Key ratio: Monohybrid Cross F2 Ratio = 3:1

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A monohybrid cross is a cross between two organisms that differ in just ONE trait.
  Mendel's most famous experiment was crossing tall and short pea plants. Let's trace
  it step by step.

  Parent Generation — P: Mendel took a pure tall plant (TT) and crossed it with a
  pure short plant (tt). Remember: a pure tall plant always produces tall offspring
  no matter what, so its genotype must be TT. Similarly, a pure short plant is tt.

  F1 Generation: Every offspring receives one allele from each parent. From TT, every
  gamete carries T. From tt, every gamete carries t. So every F1 offspring is Tt.
  Now, T is dominant over t, so what do all F1 plants look like? They are ALL TALL.
  100% of F1 offspring are tall. The shortness has apparently vanished — but it is
  still hiding inside those t alleles.

  F2 Generation: Mendel then self-pollinated the F1 plants. He crossed Tt × Tt.
  Now something remarkable happens. The hidden t alleles can finally come together.
  Some offspring will be TT (pure tall), some Tt (hybrid tall), and some tt (short).

  When you count them out, you get: 3 tall plants for every 1 short plant.
  This is the famous 3:1 phenotypic ratio.

  The short trait did not disappear — it was just recessive and hidden in the F1
  generation. Mendel's genius was in seeing this and understanding what it meant.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Monohybrid cross = cross involving ONE trait at a time.
  - P cross: TT × tt → F1: All Tt → All Tall (100%).
  - F1 self-cross: Tt × Tt → F2 phenotype ratio = 3 Tall : 1 Short = 3:1.
  - F2 genotype ratio = 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt = 1:2:1.
  - The recessive trait disappears in F1 but reappears in F2.

BOARD TIP:  The 3:1 phenotypic ratio and 1:2:1 genotypic ratio are must-memorize for board exams — write both ratios clearly when answering questions about monohybrid crosses.

SLIDE 12: Punnett Square — P Cross (TT × tt)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Punnett square for the parental cross TT × tt:
    Columns: t, t (from tt parent)
    Rows: T, T (from TT parent)
    All four cells: Tt
  - Result: All offspring are Tt → 100% Tall (the short trait is hidden).

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let's now use the Punnett square — ਪਨੇਟ ਵਰਗ — to visualize the parental cross.
  The Punnett square is a simple grid that shows all the possible combinations of
  alleles that can appear in the offspring of a genetic cross.

  Here we are crossing a pure tall plant (TT) with a pure short plant (tt).

  To draw the Punnett square: Place the gametes of one parent along the top, and the
  gametes of the other parent down the side. The TT parent produces gametes that all
  carry T, so we write T and T across the top. The tt parent produces gametes that
  all carry t, so we write t and t down the side.

  Now we fill in the four boxes by combining the letters from the top and side:
  - Top T + Side t = Tt
  - Top T + Side t = Tt
  - Top T + Side t = Tt
  - Top T + Side t = Tt

  All four offspring are Tt. Since T is dominant, all four plants will be TALL. The
  short trait is hidden — not gone, just hidden inside the t allele of each plant.

  This confirms what Mendel observed: crossing a pure tall and pure short plant gives
  100% tall offspring in the F1 generation.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Punnett square for TT × tt → all four cells give Tt.
  - F1 genotype: 100% Tt (heterozygous).
  - F1 phenotype: 100% Tall (because T is dominant).
  - The recessive 't' allele is present but hidden.

BOARD TIP:  Always draw the Punnett square neatly in board answers — label the parents, place gametes on top and side, fill all four cells. This earns full marks.

SLIDE 13: Punnett Square — F1 Self-Cross (Tt × Tt)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Punnett square for Tt × Tt:
    Columns: T, t (top)
    Rows: T, t (side)
    Cells: TT, Tt, Tt, tt
  - Genotypic ratio: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt = 1:2:1
  - Phenotypic ratio: 3 Tall : 1 Short = 3:1
  - Interactive element: tap/fill in blanks.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let's look at the most important Punnett square in this chapter — the F1
  self-cross. We take the F1 plants (all Tt) and cross them with each other: Tt × Tt.

  To draw this Punnett square: The Tt plant produces two types of gametes — T and t.
  Write T and t across the top, and T and t down the side.

  Now fill in the four boxes:
  - Top T + Side T = TT
  - Top t + Side T = Tt
  - Top T + Side t = Tt
  - Top t + Side t = tt

  So we get: 1 TT, 2 Tt, and 1 tt.

  The genotypic ratio is 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt, written as 1:2:1. You have one pure
  tall, two hybrid tall, and one pure short.

  Now for the phenotypic ratio: TT is tall, Tt is tall (T is dominant), and tt is
  short. So out of 4 offspring: 3 are tall, 1 is short.
  The phenotypic ratio is 3 Tall : 1 Short = 3:1.

  This is exactly what Mendel observed in his experiment! The short trait that was
  hidden in F1 has reappeared in the F2 generation, exactly as Mendel predicted.

  Practise filling in this Punnett square on your own until you can do it in 30
  seconds — it's that important.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Tt × Tt → Punnett square gives: 1 TT : 2 Tt : 1 tt.
  - Genotypic ratio = 1:2:1.
  - Phenotypic ratio = 3 Tall : 1 Short = 3:1.
  - Both ratios must be memorized and written correctly in the exam.

BOARD TIP:  Always write BOTH the genotypic ratio (1:2:1) AND the phenotypic ratio (3:1) when answering monohybrid cross questions. Forgetting one costs marks.

SLIDE 15: Mendel's Dihybrid Cross (Two Traits)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Dihybrid Cross — two traits simultaneously.
  - Parents (P): Round/Yellow seeds (RRYY) × Wrinkled/Green seeds (rryy).
  - F1: All seeds Round and Yellow (RrYy).
  - F2: New combinations appear — 9:3:3:1 ratio.
  - Board trap warning: 9:3:3:1 proves Law of Independent Assortment.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  After mastering the monohybrid cross, Mendel went further and asked: what happens
  when two traits are studied at the same time? This is called a dihybrid cross.

  Mendel chose two traits: seed shape (Round R vs. Wrinkled r) and seed color (Yellow
  Y vs. Green y). Round is dominant over wrinkled, and Yellow is dominant over green.

  P Generation: He crossed a pure Round-Yellow plant (RRYY) with a pure
  Wrinkled-Green plant (rryy).

  F1 Generation: Every F1 offspring receives R from one parent and r from the other,
  and Y from one parent and y from the other. So all F1 offspring are RrYy. Since R
  and Y are both dominant, all F1 seeds are Round and Yellow. No wrinkled or green
  seeds appear in F1.

  F2 Generation: When F1 plants (RrYy) are allowed to self-pollinate, something
  remarkable happens. Traits from the original parents mix and match to create NEW
  combinations that never existed before — like Round-Green or Wrinkled-Yellow!

  After careful counting of thousands of seeds, Mendel found the F2 ratio was always:
  9 Round-Yellow : 3 Round-Green : 3 Wrinkled-Yellow : 1 Wrinkled-Green = 9:3:3:1.

  This ratio is the proof of the Law of Independent Assortment. It shows that the
  inheritance of seed shape has NO influence on the inheritance of seed color — they
  behave independently. A seed's shape does not dictate its color.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Dihybrid cross = two traits studied simultaneously.
  - Parents: RRYY × rryy → F1: All RrYy → All Round and Yellow.
  - F2 phenotypic ratio: 9 Round-Yellow : 3 Round-Green : 3 Wrinkled-Yellow : 1 Wrinkled-Green = 9:3:3:1.
  - 9:3:3:1 proves the Law of Independent Assortment.

BOARD TIP:  The 9:3:3:1 ratio is the definitive answer for dihybrid cross F2. Always mention it proves the Law of Independent Assortment. Very common 5-mark question.

SLIDE 16: Sex Determination in Humans
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Sex Determination in Humans — 3-Mark Sequence.
  - 5-step interactive sequence:
    1. Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes; 23rd pair = sex chromosomes; XX = Female, XY = Male.
    2. Mother produces eggs — ALL carry X chromosome.
    3. Father produces sperm — 50% carry X, 50% carry Y.
    4. X-sperm + egg → XX → Girl; Y-sperm + egg → XY → Boy.
    5. Conclusion: Sex of child determined entirely by father's contribution.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  How is it that almost exactly half of all humans born are male and half are female?
  Is it the mother's doing, or the father's? Let's trace the biology step by step.

  Step 1: Humans have 23 pairs of chromosomes in every cell — that is 46 chromosomes
  in total. The first 22 pairs are called autosomes, and they are the same in both
  males and females. But the 23rd pair — the sex chromosomes — is different. In
  females, the 23rd pair is XX. In males, the 23rd pair is XY.

  Step 2: When the mother forms egg cells, the 23rd pair (XX) separates. So every
  egg carries exactly ONE X chromosome. The mother can ONLY give an X chromosome
  to the child — 100% of the time.

  Step 3: When the father forms sperm cells, the 23rd pair (XY) separates. Some
  sperm carry the X chromosome and some carry the Y chromosome — in approximately
  equal numbers. So 50% of sperm carry X and 50% carry Y.

  Step 4: At fertilization, it is random chance which sperm meets the egg.
  - If an X-carrying sperm fertilizes the egg: the baby is XX → a Girl.
  - If a Y-carrying sperm fertilizes the egg: the baby is XY → a Boy.

  Step 5: Conclusion — The mother always contributes an X. The sex of the child is
  determined entirely by what the FATHER contributes! If the father contributes X,
  the child is a girl. If the father contributes Y, the child is a boy. This means
  it is biologically incorrect to blame the mother for the sex of a child.

  This is also why the probability of a boy or girl is always exactly 50% — because
  exactly half of the father's sperm carry X and half carry Y.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Human females are XX; human males are XY (23rd chromosome pair).
  - Mother always gives X → 100% of eggs carry X.
  - Father gives X or Y (50/50) → determines the sex of the child.
  - Boy = XY (from Y-sperm); Girl = XX (from X-sperm).
  - Sex of child is determined by the FATHER, not the mother.

BOARD TIP:  Classic 3-mark board question: "How is the sex of a human child determined?" — draw the diagram showing mother (XX) → only X eggs, father (XY) → X or Y sperm, and both outcomes. Always state that the father determines the sex.

SLIDE 17: How Are Traits Expressed?
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Mechanism: Gene (DNA) → Protein/Enzyme → Trait
  - Explanation: A gene is a piece of DNA that carries instructions to make a specific
    protein (enzyme). These proteins build the body and control its functions, producing
    the visible trait.
  - Example: Working gene → enough growth hormone → tall plant; faulty gene → less
    growth hormone → short plant.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We've been talking about genes controlling traits — but HOW exactly does a gene
  produce a physical trait like height? Let's understand the mechanism.

  A gene is a specific segment of DNA. It carries instructions — like a recipe — for
  making a specific protein or enzyme. These proteins are the actual workers inside
  the cell. They catalyze chemical reactions, build structures, and regulate the
  body's processes.

  The chain is: Gene (DNA) → Protein/Enzyme → Trait.

  Let's apply this to height in pea plants. The T allele carries instructions for
  producing an enzyme that is involved in making growth hormones in the plant. If
  the T allele is present and working correctly, enough growth hormone is produced,
  and the plant grows tall — tall phenotype.

  The t allele, however, is a "faulty" version of the same gene. It produces an
  enzyme that doesn't work as well, so less growth hormone is made, and the plant
  stays short — short phenotype.

  When a plant is Tt (heterozygous), the one working T allele produces enough enzyme
  and growth hormone for the plant to grow tall. That's why T is dominant — even
  one copy is sufficient to produce the full trait.

  When a plant is tt, there is no working T allele. No functional enzyme, insufficient
  growth hormone, and the plant remains short.

  This gene → protein → trait pathway is fundamental to all of biology. Every
  inherited trait ultimately comes down to whether a gene produces a functional
  protein or not.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Mechanism: Gene (DNA) → Protein/Enzyme → Trait.
  - Dominant allele (T) = makes functional enzyme → trait expressed.
  - Recessive allele (t) = makes non-functional or insufficient enzyme → trait not fully expressed.
  - Even one copy of a dominant allele (Tt) is enough to produce the trait.

BOARD TIP:  This is the biological explanation for dominance — even one working copy of T produces enough enzyme. This often appears as a "Why is T dominant over t?" question.

SLIDE 19: Test Cross
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Concept: A tall plant could be TT or Tt — they look identical.
  - Test Cross: Cross the unknown plant with a homozygous recessive (tt).
  - Result table:
    All offspring Tall → Unknown plant was TT
    Half Tall, half Short (1:1) → Unknown plant was Tt

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Here is a practical genetics problem: You are looking at a tall pea plant. It is
  definitely tall — but is its genotype TT or Tt? Both genotypes produce a tall
  phenotype, so you cannot tell just by looking. How do you find out?

  The answer is a test cross — also called a back cross. You cross the unknown plant
  with a plant that is definitely homozygous recessive: tt. A tt plant can only
  give a t allele to every offspring. So whatever alleles appear in the offspring
  must have come from the unknown parent.

  Case 1: If the unknown plant is TT, it can only give T. Every offspring will be
  Tt (TT parent contributes T, tt parent contributes t). All offspring are Tt — all
  tall. Result: 100% Tall → Unknown plant was TT.

  Case 2: If the unknown plant is Tt, it can give either T or t. Half the offspring
  will be Tt (tall) and half will be tt (short). Result: 50% Tall, 50% Short, that
  is a 1:1 ratio → Unknown plant was Tt.

  So: If all offspring are tall → TT. If half are tall and half are short (1:1 ratio)
  → Tt. The tt plant acts like a "revealer" — it exposes whatever hidden alleles the
  unknown plant was carrying.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Test cross = crossing unknown genotype with homozygous recessive (tt).
  - All offspring tall → unknown was TT (homozygous dominant).
  - Half tall, half short (1:1 ratio) → unknown was Tt (heterozygous).
  - Purpose: To determine the hidden genotype of a plant with dominant phenotype.

BOARD TIP:  Common 3-mark application question: "How would you determine if a tall plant is TT or Tt?" — describe the test cross and give both possible outcomes.

SLIDE 21: Spot the Error — Fix It
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Error to find: "Genotype of a pure short pea plant = Tt"
  - Correction revealed: A pure (homozygous) short plant is tt.
    "Tt" is a heterozygous TALL plant because T is dominant.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Error-spotting questions test whether you truly understand genetics or are just
  memorizing without understanding. Let's tackle this one.

  The statement on the screen says: "Genotype of a pure short pea plant = Tt."

  Can you spot the mistake? Think for a moment before I reveal the answer...

  The error is in writing "Tt" as the genotype for a pure short plant.

  Here's why it's wrong: A "pure" plant means it is homozygous — both alleles are
  the same. A pure short plant would be tt, not Tt. If you write Tt, you are
  describing a plant that is HETEROZYGOUS — one dominant T and one recessive t.
  And since T is dominant, a Tt plant would be TALL, not short!

  The correct statement should be: "Genotype of a pure short pea plant = tt."

  Let's also clarify what Tt actually means: Tt is a heterozygous plant that has
  one dominant allele (T) and one recessive allele (t). Its phenotype is TALL,
  because T is dominant and hides the effect of t.

  So: Pure tall plant = TT. Hybrid tall plant = Tt. Pure short plant = tt.
  Never confuse these.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Pure short plant = tt (homozygous recessive), NOT Tt.
  - Tt = heterozygous = TALL (because T is dominant).
  - "Pure" always means homozygous (both alleles the same).
  - The common error is writing Tt when the question says "pure short" — avoid it.

BOARD TIP:  This is the single most common mistake in this chapter. If you see the word "pure" in any genetics question, immediately write a homozygous genotype (TT or tt).

SLIDE 23: Dihybrid Cross — Building 9:3:3:1 (5-Mark Derivation)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Dihybrid Cross — Building 9:3:3:1 — 5-Mark Derivation.
  - 6-step interactive sequence:
    Step 1: Parents (P): RRYY × rryy.
    Step 2: F1: All RrYy → 100% Round and Yellow.
    Step 3: Gametes from F1 (RrYy): 4 types — RY, Ry, rY, ry.
    Step 4: F2 Punnett grid: 4 × 4 = 16 cells.
    Step 5: Phenotype count: 9 Round-Yellow : 3 Round-Green : 3 Wrinkled-Yellow : 1 Wrinkled-Green.
    Step 6: Conclusion: 9:3:3:1 proves independent assortment.
  - Key facts: 4 gamete types = RY, Ry, rY, ry.

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is the most detailed derivation in the chapter — and it is frequently asked
  as a 5-mark question. Let's trace the dihybrid cross step by step. In Punjabi,
  let's remember: 9:3:3:1 ਅਨੁਪਾਤ ਕਿਵੇਂ ਬਣਦਾ ਹੈ — how does the 9:3:3:1 ratio arise?

  Step 1 — Parents: We cross a plant that is pure Round-Yellow (RRYY) with one that
  is pure Wrinkled-Green (rryy). R = round (dominant), r = wrinkled (recessive),
  Y = yellow (dominant), y = green (recessive).

  Step 2 — F1 Generation: Every F1 offspring gets one R from the RRYY parent and one
  r from the rryy parent, giving Rr. Similarly, one Y and one y giving Yy. So all
  F1 are RrYy. Since R and Y are both dominant, all F1 seeds look Round and Yellow.

  Step 3 — Gametes from F1: An RrYy plant produces gametes, and during gamete
  formation, the R/r pair and the Y/y pair separate independently (Law of Independent
  Assortment). This creates 4 types of gametes: RY, Ry, rY, and ry. This step is
  critical — memorize these four gamete types.

  Step 4 — F2 Punnett Grid: Since F1 makes 4 gamete types, the F2 Punnett square is
  a 4×4 grid with 16 cells. Place RY, Ry, rY, ry across the top and down the side.
  Fill in all 16 combinations.

  Step 5 — Count the Phenotypes: After filling the 16-cell grid, count each type:
  - Round Yellow (R_Y_): 9 out of 16
  - Round Green (R_yy): 3 out of 16
  - Wrinkled Yellow (rrY_): 3 out of 16
  - Wrinkled Green (rryy): 1 out of 16

  Ratio: 9 : 3 : 3 : 1.

  Step 6 — Conclusion: This ratio appears ONLY because the two traits (shape and
  color) were inherited independently. If shape and color were linked, we would NOT
  see Round-Green or Wrinkled-Yellow (the new combinations). The appearance of these
  new combinations in a 9:3:3:1 ratio is direct proof of the Law of Independent
  Assortment.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Parents RRYY × rryy → F1: All RrYy (Round-Yellow).
  - F1 produces 4 gamete types: RY, Ry, rY, ry.
  - F2 is a 4×4 Punnett grid (16 cells).
  - F2 phenotype ratio: 9 Round-Yellow : 3 Round-Green : 3 Wrinkled-Yellow : 1 Wrinkled-Green = 9:3:3:1.
  - 9:3:3:1 proves the Law of Independent Assortment.

BOARD TIP:  5-mark board question: Write the full derivation in 6 steps, draw the 4×4 Punnett grid, label all 16 cells, and state the ratio with the law it proves. This derivation alone can fetch full 5 marks.

  Question 1: What plant did Mendel use for his experiments?
  Answer: The garden pea — Pisum sativum.

  Question 2: Traits that only affect somatic cells and aren't passed down are called?
  Answer: Acquired traits — ਗ੍ਰਹਿਣ ਕੀਤੇ ਲੱਛਣ.

  Question 3: The genetic makeup written as letters (e.g., Tt) is called?
  Answer: Genotype — ਜੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ.

  Question 4: What is the F2 phenotype ratio in a dihybrid cross?
  Answer: 9:3:3:1.

  Question 5: What letters represent a human male's sex chromosomes?
  Answer: XY.

  Question 6: The physical, visible expression of a gene (e.g., Tall) is called?
  Answer: Phenotype — ਫੀਨੋਟਾਈਪ.

  Question 7: Different forms of the same gene are called?
  Answer: Alleles — ਏਲੀਲ.

  Question 8: The grid used to predict offspring genotypes is the ___ square?
  Answer: Punnett square — ਪਨੇਟ ਵਰਗ.

  If any student hesitated on any of these, go back to that slide and review. These
  eight answers represent the minimum knowledge needed to pass the genetics section
  of any PSEB board exam. Practice these until every answer is instant.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Mendel used Pisum sativum (garden pea).
  - Acquired traits → somatic cells; Inherited traits → germ cells.
  - Genotype = letters; Phenotype = visible trait.
  - Dihybrid F2 = 9:3:3:1; Male sex chromosomes = XY.
  - Alleles = different forms of a gene; Punnett square = prediction grid.

  Question 2: "If TT × tt, what is the height of F1?" Options: All Tall / All

  Question 3: "The passing of traits from parents to offspring is called?"
  Options: Evolution / Heredity / Reproduction. Answer: B — Heredity.

  Question 4: "A plant has recessive 't' gene — it is short because?" Options:

  Answer: A — less growth hormone is produced. The 't' allele makes a faulty enzyme.

  Question 5: "The sex of a human child is determined by?" Options: Temperature /
  Mother's X / Father's sperm (X or Y). Answer: C — the father's sperm.

  Question 6: "F2 phenotypic ratio of a dihybrid cross?" Options: 9:3:3:1 / 3:1
  / 1:2:1. Answer: A — 9:3:3:1.

  Question 7: "Different forms of the same gene are called?" Options: Alleles /
  Genotypes / Chromosomes. Answer: A — Alleles.

  Question 8: "Which is an acquired trait?" Options: Blood group / A scar from an
  injury / Eye colour. Answer: B — a scar. It is caused by environment (injury),
  affects only somatic cells, and cannot be inherited.

  How did you do? If you got all 8 correct, you are fully prepared for MCQs from
  this chapter. If you missed any, revisit that specific concept before the exam.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Homozygous = TT or tt (not Tt).
  - TT × tt → F1 = All Tall (Tt genotype).
  - Sex determined by father's sperm, not mother.
  - Dihybrid F2 = 9:3:3:1; Monohybrid F2 = 3:1 (don't confuse them!).
  - Scar = acquired trait; Blood group and eye colour = inherited traits.

BOARD TIP:  MCQ traps in this chapter: (1) confusing monohybrid 3:1 with dihybrid 9:3:3:1; (2) saying mother determines sex. Both are very commonly wrong answers.
