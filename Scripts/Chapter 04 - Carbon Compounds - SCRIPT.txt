SLIDE 2: Key Vocabulary (ਮੁੱਖ ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table with three columns: English term | Punjabi term | Meaning
  - Six terms: Covalent Bond, Catenation, Saturated, Unsaturated,
    Isomers, Combustion

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we dive into the chemistry, let us lock down the key vocabulary
  for this chapter. Science becomes much easier once you know the language.

  The first term is Covalent Bond — ਸਹਿ-ਸੰਜੋਜਕ ਬੰਧਨ. A covalent bond is
  formed by the sharing of electrons between atoms. This is very different
  from ionic bonds, where electrons are transferred. Carbon almost always
  forms covalent bonds.

  Next, Catenation — ਲੜੀਬੰਧਨ. This is carbon's unique ability to bond
  with other carbon atoms to form long chains, branched chains, and rings.
  The word itself comes from the Latin "catena" meaning chain.

  Saturated — ਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ — means a carbon compound that has only single bonds
  between its carbon atoms. Think of it as "full" — every bond is a single
  bond, nothing more.

  Unsaturated — ਅਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ — means the compound has at least one double or
  triple bond between carbon atoms.

  Isomers — ਸਮਅੰਗ — are compounds that share the same molecular formula but
  have different structural arrangements. Same ingredients, different design.

  Finally, Combustion — ਬਲਣਾ — is the burning of a substance in oxygen to
  release carbon dioxide, water, heat, and light.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Covalent bond = sharing of electrons (ਸਹਿ-ਸੰਜੋਜਕ ਬੰਧਨ).
  - Catenation (ਲੜੀਬੰਧਨ) = carbon chaining with other carbons.
  - Saturated = only single bonds; Unsaturated = double or triple bonds.
  - Isomers = same formula, different structure.

BOARD TIP: These six terms appear frequently in 1-mark definition questions. Memorise the Punjabi equivalents too.

SLIDE 3: Vocabulary Continued (ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ ਜਾਰੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table: English | Punjabi | Meaning — four more terms
  - Etymology box: Catenation from Latin "Catena" (Chain / ਲੜੀ)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us continue building our vocabulary. Four more important terms today.

  Hydrocarbon — ਹਾਈਡ੍ਰੋਕਾਰਬਨ. A hydrocarbon is any compound made up of only
  two elements: Carbon and Hydrogen. Methane, ethane, propane — these are
  all hydrocarbons. They are the simplest family of carbon compounds.

  Heteroatom — ਹੈਟਰੋਪਰਮਾਣੂ. When an atom other than carbon or hydrogen
  appears in an organic molecule — for example, Chlorine, Oxygen, or
  Nitrogen — that atom is called a heteroatom. Heteroatoms are important
  because they create what we call functional groups, which completely
  change the chemical behaviour of the molecule.

  Micelle — ਮਿਸੈਲ. This is a spherical cluster of soap molecules that forms
  in water. The soap molecules arrange themselves so that their oil-loving
  (hydrophobic) tails are inside, trapping grease, while their water-loving
  (hydrophilic) heads face outward. We will explore this in detail later.

  Homologous Series — ਸਮਜਾਤੀ ਲੜੀ. This is a family of carbon compounds that
  share the same functional group and the same general formula, and whose
  successive members differ by just a -CH₂ unit.

  And here is an interesting word-origin tip: the word "Catenation" comes
  from the Latin root "Catena," meaning chain — ਲੜੀ in Punjabi. So when
  you hear catenation, picture a chain of carbon atoms growing longer and
  longer!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Hydrocarbon = only Carbon + Hydrogen.
  - Heteroatom = any atom other than C or H in an organic molecule.
  - Micelle = spherical soap cluster that traps grease.
  - Homologous Series = family of compounds with same functional group,
    differing by -CH₂ each time.

BOARD TIP: "Micelle" is frequently asked as a define/explain in 2-mark questions. Know both the word and the structure.

SLIDE 4: Why does Carbon Share? (Reasoning)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Carbon's electronic configuration: 2, 4 (4 valence electrons)
  - Two arguments: Why C⁴⁻ is impossible; Why C⁴⁺ is impossible
  - Conclusion: Carbon forms covalent bonds by sharing

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us answer one of the most important reasoning questions of this
  chapter: Why does carbon form covalent bonds instead of ionic bonds?

  To be stable, an atom needs 8 electrons in its outermost shell — the
  octet rule. Carbon has the electronic configuration 2, 4 — meaning it has
  4 valence electrons. To complete its octet, carbon needs 4 more electrons.
  It has two options.

  Option 1: Carbon could gain 4 electrons to form a C⁴⁻ anion. But think
  about what that means — the carbon nucleus has only 6 protons. It would
  now need to hold onto 10 electrons. The positive charge of 6 protons is
  simply not strong enough to hold 10 electrons in a stable way. So forming
  C⁴⁻ is not feasible.

  Option 2: Carbon could lose all 4 valence electrons to form a C⁴⁺ cation.
  But removing 4 electrons requires an enormous amount of energy — far too
  much for a stable compound to form under normal conditions. What you'd be
  left with is a tiny, intensely positively charged nucleus — extremely
  unstable.

  So carbon's only practical solution is to share its 4 valence electrons
  with other atoms, forming covalent bonds. This is elegant and efficient.
  This is precisely why nearly all carbon compounds are covalent — and why
  there are millions of them.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Carbon has 4 valence electrons (configuration: 2, 4).
  - C⁴⁻ not feasible: 6 protons cannot hold 10 electrons.
  - C⁴⁺ not feasible: removing 4 electrons requires too much energy.
  - Solution: Carbon shares electrons → forms covalent bonds.

BOARD TIP: This reasoning question ("Why does carbon form covalent bonds?") is a classic 3-mark question. Write both arguments clearly.

SLIDE 5: Electron Dot Structures (ਇਲੈਕਟ੍ਰਾਨ ਬਿੰਦੂ ਬਣਤਰ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: Electron dot structures show how valence electrons are shared
  - Four examples: H₂ (single bond), O₂ (double bond), N₂ (triple bond),
    NH₃ (lone pair)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Electron dot structures — ਇਲੈਕਟ੍ਰਾਨ ਬਿੰਦੂ ਬਣਤਰ — are diagrams that use
  dots to represent the valence electrons of each atom, showing exactly
  how those electrons are shared between atoms in a covalent bond.

  Let us go through the four examples on screen.

  Hydrogen gas, H₂: Each hydrogen atom has 1 valence electron. They each
  need just one more to complete a pair. So two hydrogen atoms share 1 pair
  of electrons — this is a single bond. Simple!

  Oxygen gas, O₂: Each oxygen atom has 6 valence electrons and needs 8.
  So each oxygen needs 2 more. They share 2 pairs of electrons — this is
  a double bond. You'll draw it as a double line between the two oxygen
  atoms.

  Nitrogen gas, N₂: Each nitrogen has 5 valence electrons and needs 3 more.
  So they share 3 pairs of electrons — a triple bond. This is an extremely
  strong bond, which is why nitrogen gas is so stable and unreactive.

  Ammonia, NH₃: Nitrogen shares one pair of electrons with each of its
  three hydrogen atoms. But nitrogen started with 5 valence electrons and
  used only 3 of them in bonding. The remaining 2 electrons form a "lone
  pair" — they belong to nitrogen alone and are not shared with anyone.
  This lone pair is important in reactions.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Single bond = 1 shared pair (e.g., H₂).
  - Double bond = 2 shared pairs (e.g., O₂).
  - Triple bond = 3 shared pairs (e.g., N₂).
  - Lone pair = unbonded electrons on an atom (e.g., N in NH₃).

BOARD TIP: Drawing electron dot structures is a common 2-mark question. Always show lone pairs on nitrogen and oxygen atoms.

SLIDE 6: The Versatile Nature of Carbon (ਕਾਰਬਨ ਦਾ ਬਹੁਮੁਖੀ ਸੁਭਾਅ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Why are there millions of carbon compounds?
  - Two reasons: Catenation and Tetravalency
  - Diagrams of chains, branched chains, and rings

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Why are there literally millions of carbon compounds, while most other
  elements form only a handful? The answer lies in two remarkable
  properties of carbon.

  The first is Catenation — ਲੜੀਬੰਧਨ. Carbon is the only element that can
  bond extensively with itself to form very long chains, branched chains,
  and even closed rings. A carbon chain can have just 1 carbon or it can
  have thousands of carbons linked together. No other element shows this
  ability to such a vast extent. Silicon can do it a little, but carbon is
  far superior. This one property alone accounts for the staggering variety
  of organic molecules — from simple methane to complex proteins.

  The second is Tetravalency — ਚੌ-ਸੰਜੋਜਕਤਾ. Because carbon has exactly 4
  valence electrons, it can form 4 covalent bonds simultaneously. This
  means carbon can bond with 4 different kinds of atoms at the same time —
  hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen, sulphur, chlorine, and so on. It can also
  form single, double, or triple bonds. This flexibility in bonding creates
  an almost unlimited range of molecular architectures.

  Together — catenation and tetravalency — these two properties explain why
  the chemistry of carbon is an entire branch of science in its own right,
  called Organic Chemistry.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Catenation (ਲੜੀਬੰਧਨ): carbon bonds with itself to form long chains,
    branches, and rings.
  - Tetravalency (ਚੌ-ਸੰਜੋਜਕਤਾ): carbon forms 4 bonds simultaneously.
  - These two properties together explain millions of carbon compounds.
  - Carbon can form single, double, or triple bonds.

BOARD TIP: A 2-mark question often asks: "Name the two special properties of carbon." Answer: Catenation and Tetravalency — always give both.

SLIDE 7: Reading Comprehension Story — "The Diamond and the Pencil"
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Story-mode slide with Read / Stop buttons
  - Title: "The Diamond and the Pencil"
  - Narrative passage about diamond vs. graphite

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is our story-mode reading slide. You can play the audio for your
  students, or read it aloud yourself. It is a wonderful analogy to
  introduce allotropes of carbon before the comparison table that follows.

  Here is the story: Carbon is the master of disguise. It is the only
  element that can form materials with completely opposite physical
  properties simply by changing how its atoms connect. Deep inside the
  Earth, under immense heat and crushing pressure, carbon atoms are forced
  into a rigid, 3D triangular grid where every atom holds tightly to four
  others — this creates a Diamond, the hardest natural substance known to
  humanity. However, if those exact same carbon atoms are arranged into flat,
  hexagonal sheets that slide over each other, they form Graphite. Graphite
  is so soft and slippery that we use it as the "lead" in our pencils to
  leave marks on paper.

  The key insight here — which you should make sure your students absorb —
  is that diamond and graphite are made of absolutely identical atoms. One
  hundred percent pure carbon in both cases. The only difference is the
  arrangement, the structure. And yet the properties could not be more
  different: one is the hardest known substance, the other is one of the
  softest. This is the power of structure in chemistry.

  After the story, ask students: "If I gave you a piece of diamond and a
  piece of graphite, could you tell which is which just from the formula?"
  The answer is no — you need to know the structure!

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Diamond and graphite are both 100% carbon — same atoms, different
    arrangement.
  - Structure determines properties.
  - Diamond: every C bonded to 4 others — very hard.
  - Graphite: C bonded to 3 others in flat sheets — soft, slippery.

SLIDE 8: Allotropes of Carbon (ਕਾਰਬਨ ਦੇ ਭਿੰਨ ਰੂਪ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: Allotropes = different physical forms of the same element
  - Comparison table: Diamond vs. Graphite
  - Bonus note: Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Allotropes — ਭਿੰਨ ਰੂਪ — are different physical forms of the same element.
  Carbon has three well-known allotropes: Diamond, Graphite, and
  Buckminsterfullerene. Let us compare the first two carefully, because
  they are certain to appear in your board exam.

  Diamond (ਹੀਰਾ): In diamond, each carbon atom is covalently bonded to
  four other carbon atoms in a tetrahedral arrangement. This creates a
  giant, rigid three-dimensional network. Because every electron is used
  in bonding, there are no free electrons — so diamond is an insulator and
  cannot conduct electricity. This rigid network also makes diamond the
  hardest natural substance on Earth. We use it to cut glass and in
  drilling equipment.

  Graphite (ਗ੍ਰੇਫਾਈਟ): In graphite, each carbon bonds to only three others
  in flat hexagonal layers. These layers are held together by weak forces,
  so they can slide over each other — that's why graphite is soft and
  greasy to touch and makes a good lubricant. Crucially, every carbon in
  graphite has one electron that is not used in the three bonds — this
  fourth electron is free to move between the layers. These delocalised
  electrons allow graphite to conduct electricity, making it useful in
  electrodes and battery components.

  As a bonus: Buckminsterfullerene is a C₆₀ allotrope shaped like a
  football — a hollow cage of 60 carbon atoms arranged in pentagons and
  hexagons. It is named after the architect Buckminster Fuller, who designed
  similar dome structures.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Diamond: each C bonded to 4 others → hard, insulator.
  - Graphite: each C bonded to 3 others → soft, conductor.
  - Free 4th electron in graphite allows electrical conduction.
  - Buckminsterfullerene (C₆₀): football-shaped allotrope.

BOARD TIP: A favourite 3-mark question: "Compare diamond and graphite as allotropes of carbon." Always mention bonding pattern, hardness, and conductivity.

SLIDE 9: Saturated vs. Unsaturated Hydrocarbons
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two-column comparison table
  - Saturated (ਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ): single bonds only, example Ethane
  - Unsaturated (ਅਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ): double or triple bonds, examples Ethene, Ethyne

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us understand one of the most fundamental classifications of
  carbon compounds: saturated versus unsaturated hydrocarbons.

  A Saturated hydrocarbon — ਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ ਹਾਈਡ੍ਰੋਕਾਰਬਨ — is one in which all the
  carbon-to-carbon bonds are single bonds. The word "saturated" means that
  the carbon atoms are already bonded to the maximum number of hydrogen
  atoms possible — they are "full up" or saturated with hydrogens. The most
  important family of saturated hydrocarbons is called Alkanes. Example:
  Ethane (C₂H₆), where the two carbon atoms share a single bond.

  An Unsaturated hydrocarbon — ਅਸੰਤ੍ਰਿਪਤ ਹਾਈਡ੍ਰੋਕਾਰਬਨ — is one that contains
  at least one double or triple bond between carbon atoms. Because some
  bonds are "double" or "triple," the carbons are not bonded to as many
  hydrogens as they could be — they are not fully saturated.
  - Alkenes contain double bonds (e.g., Ethene, C₂H₄).
  - Alkynes contain triple bonds (e.g., Ethyne, C₂H₂).

  This distinction matters because saturated and unsaturated hydrocarbons
  behave very differently in chemical reactions — saturated ones undergo
  substitution reactions, while unsaturated ones undergo addition reactions.
  And they burn very differently too, as we will see in the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Saturated = single bonds only (Alkanes, general formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂).
  - Unsaturated = at least one double or triple bond (Alkenes / Alkynes).
  - Alkenes: double bond, suffix -ene.
  - Alkynes: triple bond, suffix -yne.

BOARD TIP: A 1-mark question: "What type of bond is present in saturated hydrocarbons?" Answer: Single bond only.

SLIDE 10: Clean vs. Sooty Flames (ਸਾਫ਼ ਅਤੇ ਧੂੰਏਂ ਵਾਲੀ ਲਾਟ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Label: Classic 2-mark reasoning question on combustion
  - Saturated hydrocarbons → clean blue flame (complete combustion)
  - Unsaturated hydrocarbons → yellow, sooty flame (incomplete combustion)
  - Note about blocked gas stove air holes

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide covers a concept that comes up almost every year in board
  exams as a reasoning question: why do some hydrocarbons burn with a
  clean flame while others give a sooty, yellow flame?

  Saturated hydrocarbons, which are Alkanes, have a relatively lower ratio
  of carbon to hydrogen. When they burn in a sufficient supply of air, the
  combustion is complete — all the carbon is fully oxidised to CO₂ and all
  the hydrogen becomes water. This produces a clean, blue flame with no
  soot. Your gas cooking stove uses LPG, which is a mixture of butane and
  propane — both saturated hydrocarbons — and when working correctly, it
  burns with a nice clean blue flame.

  Unsaturated hydrocarbons — Alkenes and Alkynes — have a higher carbon-to-
  hydrogen ratio. In a normal air supply, there is not enough oxygen to
  completely combust all that carbon. As a result, some carbon particles are
  left unburnt and released into the air as soot. This gives a yellow or
  orange, sooty, smoky flame. If you have ever held a piece of acetylene
  (ethyne) torch near paper, you know how much soot it produces!

  Now here is the very important note: if you block the air holes of a gas
  stove — even though it uses a saturated hydrocarbon — the same LPG will
  now burn with a sooty yellow flame and blacken your cooking vessel! Why?
  Because the supply of oxygen is now restricted, so combustion becomes
  incomplete. The fuel is not the issue; the air supply is.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Saturated hydrocarbons → clean blue flame (complete combustion,
    lower C:H ratio).
  - Unsaturated hydrocarbons → yellow sooty flame (incomplete combustion,
    higher C:H ratio).
  - Blocked air holes → even saturated fuels give sooty flames.

BOARD TIP: Frequent 2-mark reasoning question: "Why does ethyne burn with a sooty flame while ethane gives a clean flame?" — always mention carbon content and oxygen supply.

SLIDE 11: Traits of a Homologous Series (PSEB 2020, 2022)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Label: Highly tested 3-mark question
  - 4 key features of a homologous series listed

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This slide is flagged as a PSEB board exam question from 2020 and 2022 —
  so pay very close attention! The question "State the characteristics of a
  homologous series" is a classic 3-mark or 4-mark question, and every
  student must know these four traits.

  A Homologous Series — ਸਮਜਾਤੀ ਲੜੀ — is a family of organic compounds. Here
  are its four defining traits:

  First: All members share the same general formula. For example, all
  Alkanes follow CₙH₂ₙ₊₂. If n=1, you get CH₄ (methane). If n=2, you get
  C₂H₆ (ethane). If n=3, you get C₃H₈ (propane). The formula is the same
  pattern for every member.

  Second: Successive members differ by exactly one -CH₂ group. So you go
  from methane to ethane by adding one carbon and two hydrogens, then
  ethane to propane by adding another -CH₂, and so on.

  Third: Because of this -CH₂ difference, each successive member has a
  molecular mass that is 14 atomic mass units higher than the previous one.
  (Carbon = 12, two Hydrogens = 2, total = 14 u.)

  Fourth: All members of the series have similar chemical properties,
  because they all contain the same functional group. However, their
  physical properties — like melting point, boiling point, and density —
  change gradually and predictably as the chain gets longer.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Same general formula for all members (e.g., CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ for alkanes).
  - Successive members differ by -CH₂.
  - Molecular mass increases by 14 u each step.
  - Similar chemical properties; gradually changing physical properties.

BOARD TIP: This is a guaranteed 3-mark question (appeared 2020, 2022). Write all four traits with examples to score full marks.

SLIDE 12: Naming Carbon Compounds — IUPAC Rules
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table: Number of Carbons | Prefix | Example (Alkane)
  - Meth- (1C, CH₄), Eth- (2C, C₂H₆), Prop- (3C, C₃H₈),
    But- (4C, C₄H₁₀), Pent- (5C, C₅H₁₂)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now let us learn the IUPAC system for naming carbon compounds. IUPAC
  stands for the International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry — it is
  the global standard for naming chemical compounds so that scientists
  everywhere can communicate clearly.

  The name of a carbon compound is built from two parts: a prefix that
  tells you how many carbon atoms are in the main chain, and a suffix or
  ending that tells you the family or functional group.

  Here are the five prefixes you must know for Class 10:

  One carbon → Meth- → Methane (CH₄). This is the simplest alkane.
  Natural gas is mostly methane.

  Two carbons → Eth- → Ethane (C₂H₆). The next alkane in the series.

  Three carbons → Prop- → Propane (C₃H₈). LPG contains propane.

  Four carbons → But- → Butane (C₄H₁₀). LPG also contains butane.

  Five carbons → Pent- → Pentane (C₅H₁₂). Five carbons in the chain.

  The suffix "-ane" tells you it is an alkane — a saturated hydrocarbon.
  If the suffix was "-anol," it would be an alcohol. If it was "-anoic acid,"
  it would be a carboxylic acid. We will cover suffixes in the next slide.
  But for now: memorise the five prefixes: Meth, Eth, Prop, But, Pent.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Prefix indicates number of carbons in the chain.
  - Meth=1, Eth=2, Prop=3, But=4, Pent=5.
  - Suffix indicates the functional group or type of compound.
  - Alkanes end in "-ane."

BOARD TIP: Mnemonic to remember prefixes in order: "My Enormous Purple Butterfly Pleases" — Meth, Eth, Prop, But, Pent.

SLIDE 14: Functional Group Suffixes (ਪਿਛੇਤਰ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - 6 functional group suffixes listed with examples:
    -ene (double bond), -yne (triple bond), -ol (alcohol, -OH),
    -al (aldehyde, -CHO), -one (ketone, -CO-), -oic acid
    (carboxylic acid, -COOH)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We have learned the prefixes. Now let us learn the suffixes — ਪਿਛੇਤਰ —
  which tell us what functional group is present in the compound.

  A functional group is the reactive part of an organic molecule. The
  heteroatom creates the functional group, and the functional group
  determines how the entire molecule behaves chemically. Knowing the suffix
  tells you the functional group instantly.

  Here are the six functional groups and their suffixes:

  Double bond between carbons: add the suffix -ene. Example: Propene —
  a 3-carbon chain with a double bond.

  Triple bond between carbons: add the suffix -yne. Example: Propyne —
  a 3-carbon chain with a triple bond.

  Alcohol group (-OH): add the suffix -ol. Example: Propanol — a 3-carbon
  chain with an -OH group. Ethanol (the alcohol in drinks) is a 2-carbon
  alcohol.

  Aldehyde group (-CHO): add the suffix -al. Example: Propanal — the
  -CHO group is at the end of the chain.

  Ketone group (-CO- in the middle of the chain): add the suffix -one.
  Example: Propanone — this is acetone, the nail-polish remover!

  Carboxylic Acid group (-COOH): add the suffix -oic acid. Example:
  Propanoic acid. The most famous example is Ethanoic acid — ਸਿਰਕਾ — better
  known as acetic acid, the acid in vinegar.

  Notice the pattern: the prefix gives you the carbon count, the suffix
  gives you the functional group.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - -ene = double bond; -yne = triple bond.
  - -ol = alcohol (-OH group).
  - -al = aldehyde (-CHO); -one = ketone (-CO-).
  - -oic acid = carboxylic acid (-COOH).

BOARD TIP: Always link suffix to functional group in your answer. "Ethanol contains the -OH functional group, which is why its name ends in -ol."

SLIDE 17: Structural Isomers of Pentane (ਪੈਂਟੇਨ ਦੇ ਸਮਅੰਗ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Statement: Structural isomerism possible for 4+ carbon atoms
  - 3 structural isomers of pentane (C₅H₁₂): n-pentane, isopentane,
    neopentane with structural descriptions
  - Board Exam Pitfall warning: every carbon must have exactly 4 bonds;
    draw all H atoms

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Structural isomers — ਬਣਤਰ ਸਮਅੰਗ — are compounds with the same molecular
  formula but different structural arrangements. And here is an important
  rule to note: structural isomerism is only possible for compounds with
  4 or more carbon atoms. You cannot draw two different structures for
  methane, ethane, or propane.

  Pentane, C₅H₁₂, has exactly three structural isomers — and the board
  exam may ask you to name all three or draw them.

  First isomer — n-pentane: A straight chain of 5 carbons in a row.
  "n" stands for "normal." Structure: C–C–C–C–C with hydrogens attached
  to all four bonds on each carbon.

  Second isomer — isopentane: A 4-carbon main chain with a methyl branch
  (-CH₃) attached to the second carbon. So the main chain has 4 carbons,
  and there is one carbon branch.

  Third isomer — neopentane: A 3-carbon main chain (or think of it as a
  central carbon bonded to 4 methyl groups). The middle carbon carries two
  methyl groups, creating a symmetrical cross-like shape.

  Now, the board exam pitfall — this is very important. When drawing
  structural isomers, never leave a carbon showing fewer than 4 bonds. If
  your carbon shows only 2 bonds, add 2 hydrogen atoms. Every carbon in
  every structure must have exactly 4 bonds total. A diagram with "naked
  carbons" showing fewer than 4 bonds will lose you marks.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Structural isomerism begins at 4+ carbons.
  - C₅H₁₂ has 3 isomers: n-pentane, isopentane, neopentane.
  - Every carbon must show exactly 4 bonds — draw all H atoms.
  - Same molecular formula, different structural arrangement.

BOARD TIP: Frequent 3-mark question — draw all three isomers of pentane. Also know that butane (C₄H₁₀) has 2 isomers: n-butane and isobutane.

SLIDE 18: The 4 Chemical Properties of Carbon
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four reactions listed: Combustion, Oxidation, Addition, Substitution
  - Key examples for each

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Carbon compounds show four main types of chemical reactions, and each
  one is important for your board exam.

  First — Combustion (ਬਲਣਾ): When carbon compounds burn in oxygen, they
  produce CO₂, water, heat, and light. This is the basis of all fossil
  fuels. Complete combustion gives CO₂; incomplete combustion gives CO
  (carbon monoxide), which is toxic.

  Second — Oxidation: Some carbon compounds, particularly alcohols, can be
  oxidised to become carboxylic acids. For example, Ethanol is oxidised to
  Ethanoic acid when treated with an oxidising agent like alkaline KMnO₄
  (potassium permanganate) or acidified K₂Cr₂O₇ (potassium dichromate).
  Think of KMnO₄ as an "electron-taking" agent — it takes hydrogen from
  the alcohol and converts it to an acid.

  Third — Addition Reaction: Unsaturated hydrocarbons (those with double
  or triple bonds) can add other atoms across the double or triple bond.
  The most important example is the addition of Hydrogen — called
  Hydrogenation. When vegetable oils (which are unsaturated) are reacted
  with hydrogen gas in the presence of a Nickel or Palladium catalyst, the
  double bonds are broken and hydrogen atoms are added. This converts the
  liquid oil into a solid fat — this is how vanaspati ghee and margarine
  are made!

  Fourth — Substitution Reaction: Saturated hydrocarbons can undergo
  substitution reactions. In the presence of sunlight, chlorine can replace
  hydrogen atoms in alkanes one by one. For example, in methane + chlorine
  under UV light, one H is replaced by Cl to give chloromethane, then
  another H is replaced to give dichloromethane, and so on.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Combustion: C compounds + O₂ → CO₂ + H₂O + energy.
  - Oxidation: Alcohols → Carboxylic Acids (with KMnO₄).
  - Addition: Unsaturated + H₂ (Ni/Pd catalyst) → Saturated
    (e.g., oil → solid fat).
  - Substitution: Alkane + Cl₂ (sunlight) → Halide.

BOARD TIP: Hydrogenation of vegetable oils is a very popular 2-mark question — always mention the Nickel catalyst and that double bonds are converted to single bonds.

SLIDE 19: Ethanol vs. Ethanoic Acid
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Properties of Ethanol (C₂H₅OH) and Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH)
  - Esterification reaction: Ethanol + Ethanoic Acid → Ester
  - Note on Saponification

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us study two of the most important carbon compounds in detail:
  Ethanol and Ethanoic Acid.

  Ethanol — C₂H₅OH — is the alcohol in alcoholic beverages. Industrially,
  it is also a very important solvent. Here are its key chemical behaviours:
  Ethanol reacts with Sodium metal to produce Hydrogen gas. When ethanol is
  heated with concentrated sulphuric acid (H₂SO₄), it undergoes
  dehydration — the water molecule is removed and ethene (an alkene) is
  produced. This is why concentrated H₂SO₄ acts as a dehydrating agent here.

  Ethanoic Acid — CH₃COOH — is the acid that gives vinegar its sharp smell
  and taste. A 5–8% solution of ethanoic acid in water is what we call
  vinegar — ਸਿਰਕਾ in Punjabi. One interesting physical fact about ethanoic
  acid: it has a relatively high melting point of 16.6°C. In cold winters,
  it can actually freeze into a solid. This solid, pure form is called
  Glacial Acetic Acid — because it looks like ice.

  Now the most important reaction connecting these two compounds:
  Esterification — ਐਸਟਰੀਕਰਨ. When Ethanol is reacted with Ethanoic Acid
  in the presence of a little concentrated H₂SO₄ as a catalyst, the two
  combine to form an Ester and water. Esters are sweet-smelling compounds
  used in perfumes, artificial flavours, and fruit essences. If you have
  ever smelled nail polish remover or fruit-flavoured candy, you know the
  smell of esters.

  The reverse reaction — when an ester is reacted with a base (like NaOH)
  to break it back into a carboxylic acid salt and alcohol — is called
  Saponification. This is exactly how soap is made. We will come back to
  this shortly.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Ethanol (C₂H₅OH): active ingredient of alcoholic drinks; reacts with Na
    to give H₂; dehydrates with H₂SO₄ to form ethene.
  - Ethanoic Acid (CH₃COOH): 5–8% solution in water = vinegar (ਸਿਰਕਾ);
    freezes in winter (glacial acetic acid).
  - Esterification: Ethanol + Ethanoic Acid → Ester (sweet-smelling).
  - Saponification: Ester + Base → Soap.

BOARD TIP: "Glacial acetic acid" and "saponification" are frequent 1-mark terms. Esterification equation is worth 2–3 marks.

  - Narrative on the molecular mechanism of soap cleaning

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Here is our third story-mode reading slide. This one explains the
  cleansing action of soap using a wonderful "tug-of-war" analogy. Play
  the audio or read it aloud to your class as a bridge into the next
  technical slide about micelle formation.

  The story goes: Washing a greasy shirt requires a fascinating molecular
  tug-of-war. Soap molecules are long chains with two very different ends.

  repels water but acts like a magnet for oils and dirt.

  into the greasy dirt, while the heads stick out into the surrounding water.
  This creates a spherical structure called a Micelle. As the washing
  machine agitates the water, the hydrophilic heads are pulled by the water
  currents, and the entire micelle — with the dirt trapped inside — is torn
  away from the fabric and washed down the drain.

  After the story, ask students: "What is the name of the spherical
  structure?" (Micelle.) "Which part of the soap attaches to grease?"
  (The hydrophobic tail.) This reading prepares them perfectly for the
  5-mark blueprint on the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Soap has two ends: hydrophilic head (loves water) and hydrophobic
    tail (loves oil/grease).
  - Tails burrowing into grease + heads facing water = Micelle.
  - Agitation pulls micelle + trapped dirt away from fabric.

SLIDE 21: Cleansing Action of Soap (PSEB 2019, 2021, 2023 — 5 Marks)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two ends of soap molecule: Hydrophilic Head, Hydrophobic Tail
  - 5-Mark Answer Blueprint: 4 structured steps
  - Note: Soap forms insoluble scum in hard water; detergents do not

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is possibly the highest-value slide in the entire chapter — the
  cleansing action of soap has appeared in PSEB board exams in 2019, 2021,
  and 2023 for 5 marks. Every student must know this answer perfectly.

  Let me teach you the four-step blueprint for a full 5-mark answer.

  Step 1 — Define the two ends of the soap molecule:
  A soap molecule is a sodium or potassium salt of a long-chain fatty acid.
  It has two distinct ends. The ionic end (the COO⁻ part) is called the
  hydrophilic end — ਪਾਣੀ-ਪ੍ਰੇਮੀ. It is attracted to water. The long
  hydrocarbon chain is the hydrophobic end — ਪਾਣੀ-ਵਿਰੋਧੀ. It is repelled
  by water but attracted to oils and grease.

  Step 2 — Explain micelle formation:
  When soap is dissolved in water and comes into contact with greasy dirt,
  the hydrophobic tails of soap molecules plunge into the grease droplet,
  while the hydrophilic heads remain pointing outward toward the water.
  This arrangement forms a spherical cluster called a Micelle — ਮਿਸੈਲ.
  The dirt is trapped inside the micelle.

  Step 3 — The cleaning step:
  Because the outside of the micelle is covered with hydrophilic heads,
  the entire micelle stays dispersed in water (it doesn't stick to the
  fabric anymore).

  Step 4 — Rinse and remove:
  When the water is agitated (by scrubbing or the washing machine) and
  rinsed away, the micelles — carrying the trapped dirt — are carried away
  with the water, leaving the fabric clean.

  Final important distinction: Soap does not work in hard water. Hard
  water contains dissolved salts of Calcium (Ca²⁺) and Magnesium (Mg²⁺).
  The soap reacts with these ions to form an insoluble white precipitate
  called scum — ਮੈਲ. This wastes soap and leaves a residue on fabric.
  Detergents, on the other hand, do not react with Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions, so
  they work effectively in hard water.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Soap has hydrophilic head (ionic end) and hydrophobic tail (hydrocarbon
    chain).
  - Tails enter grease; heads face water → forms a Micelle (ਮਿਸੈਲ).
  - Micelles are dispersed in water; rinsing removes dirt.
  - Soap + hard water → insoluble scum. Detergents work in hard water.

BOARD TIP: Guaranteed 5-mark question (PSEB 2019, 2021, 2023). Draw a micelle diagram — circle with tails pointing inward and heads outward. Label clearly.

  FALSE — Saturated hydrocarbons undergo substitution reactions.

  Q8: Micelles are formed when soap dissolves in water.

  Q13: The functional group of a ketone is -OH.
  FALSE — -OH is the functional group of an alcohol. Ketone's functional

  Q14: Methane is the simplest alkane.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Saturated = single bonds (Q1 False). Carbon valency = 4, not 6 (Q10 False).
  - Alkynes formula CₙH₂ₙ₋₂ (not CₙH₂ₙ) (Q12 False).
  - Ketone = -CO- group, not -OH (Q13 False).
  - Saturated hydrocarbons → substitution (not addition) (Q7 False).

  Q1: Carbon has how many valence electrons?
  Answer: 4 valence electrons (configuration 2, 4).

  Q2: Covalent bond means...?
  Answer: The sharing of electron pairs between two atoms.

  Q3: Saturated means...?
  Answer: A carbon compound in which all carbon-carbon bonds are single
  bonds.

  Q4: What is a 5–8% solution of ethanoic acid in water called?
  Answer: Vinegar — ਸਿਰਕਾ.

  Q5: Ester reacting with a base to form soap is called...?
  Answer: Saponification — ਸਾਬਣੀਕਰਨ.

  Q6: Soap forms scum in what type of water?
  Answer: Hard water (which contains dissolved Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ salts).

  Q7: What is the general formula for alkanes?
  Answer: CₙH₂ₙ₊₂.

  Q8: Which carbon allotrope is a good conductor of electricity?
  Answer: Graphite — because it has a free (delocalised) 4th electron.

  Q9: How many single bonds are in a methane (CH₄) molecule?
  Answer: 4 single bonds (one between carbon and each of the 4 hydrogens).

  Q10: What is the process of converting ethanol into ethanoic acid called?
  Answer: Oxidation.

  Q11: The prefix for a 3-carbon chain is...?
  Answer: Prop- (as in Propane, Propanol, Propanoic acid).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Carbon: 4 valence electrons, valency = 4.
  - Covalent bond = sharing of electrons.
  - Alkane general formula = CₙH₂ₙ₊₂.
  - Graphite = conductor; Diamond = insulator.
  - Saponification = ester + base → soap + glycerol.

BOARD TIP: Ensure every student can answer all 11 of these from memory before the board exam. These are the quick-mark questions that students should never drop.

  Q3: Successive members of a homologous series differ by —
  Answer: B (-CH₂).

  Q4: Converting vegetable oils to solid fats using hydrogen is an example

  Hydrogen is added across the double bonds of the unsaturated oil.

  Q5: Carbon's unique ability to bond with other carbons is called —
  Answer: C (Catenation).
  Tetravalency describes its 4-bond capacity; isomerism is about structure.
  Catenation specifically means self-bonding into chains.

  Q6: The formula of Butane is — Answer: B (C₄H₁₀).
  Using CₙH₂ₙ₊₂: n=4 → C₄H₁₀. (C₃H₈ is propane, C₄H₈ would be an alkene.)

  Q7: The functional group in a carboxylic acid is —
  Answer: C (-COOH).
  -OH is alcohol; -CHO is aldehyde; -COOH is carboxylic acid.

  Q8: Vegetable oils are hydrogenated using which catalyst —
  Answer: B (Nickel).
  Nickel (or Palladium) catalyst is used in the hydrogenation reaction.

  Q9: The by-product of saponification is —
  Answer: A (Glycerol).
  Fat + NaOH → Soap + Glycerol. Glycerol is always the by-product.

  Q10: Hard water contains salts of —
  Answer: B (Calcium and Magnesium).
  Ca²⁺ and Mg²⁺ ions in hard water react with soap to form scum.

  Q11: Buckminsterfullerene is an allotrope of —
  Answer: C (Carbon).
  C₆₀, football-shaped — the third allotrope of carbon after diamond and
  graphite.

  Q12: The molecular formula of Benzene is —
  Answer: B (C₆H₆).
  Benzene: 6 carbons, 6 hydrogens in a hexagonal ring. Not C₆H₁₂ (that
  would be cyclohexane) and not C₄H₁₀ (that is butane).
