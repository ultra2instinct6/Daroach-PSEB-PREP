SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let's continue with more important vocabulary. These terms will come up again and again

  Trophic Level — ਪੋਸ਼ੀ ਪੱਧਰ — means a feeding step in a food chain. Grass is at trophic level 1,

  chain. By the time they reach humans or eagles at the top, the concentration can be dangerously
  high.

  Ozone — ਓਜ਼ੋਨ — is O₃, a special molecule made of three oxygen atoms. It forms a layer in the

  microorganisms. Non-biodegradable — ਅਜੈਵ-ਵਿਘਟਨਸ਼ੀਲ — means waste that cannot be broken
  down — it persists in the environment for years or even centuries.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Trophic level = a step in the food chain (T1 = producers, T2 = herbivores, etc.).
  - Biological magnification = toxin concentration increases at every higher trophic level.
  - Ozone = O₃; it absorbs harmful UV radiation.
  - Biodegradable rots; non-biodegradable does not.

BOARD TIP:  "Biological magnification" and "trophic level" are frequently tested — know the definitions word-for-word.

SLIDE 5: Components of an Ecosystem — Definition
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: An ecosystem is a unit where living things interact with their non-living surroundings.
  - Two flip cards: Biotic Components (ਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼) and Abiotic Components (ਅਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let's now formally define an ecosystem. An ecosystem — ਪਾਰਿਸਥਿਤੀ ਪ੍ਰਬੰਧ — is a self-contained
  unit in which all living organisms interact with each other and with their non-living
  surroundings. Examples include a forest, a pond, a grassland, a desert, or even a small aquarium.

  Every ecosystem has two types of components. The first is Biotic Components — ਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼.
  These are all the living organisms in the ecosystem — plants, animals, bacteria, and fungi. They
  are further grouped into producers, consumers, and decomposers based on how they get their food.

  The second is Abiotic Components — ਅਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼. These are all the non-living physical and
  chemical factors — sunlight, temperature, rainfall, air, water, soil, and minerals. These factors
  determine which organisms can survive in a particular ecosystem. For example, cacti survive in
  a desert because they are adapted to the abiotic conditions there — high heat and low water.

  Remember: you cannot have a complete ecosystem without both biotic AND abiotic components
  working together.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Ecosystem = living organisms + non-living environment interacting together.
  - Biotic = all living things (plants, animals, microorganisms).
  - Abiotic = all non-living factors (sunlight, water, soil, temperature).
  - Both components are essential; neither alone makes an ecosystem.

BOARD TIP:  "Define ecosystem" or "What are the components of an ecosystem?" is a frequent 2–3 mark question.

SLIDE 6: Producers, Consumers & Decomposers
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Heading: Every organism has a trophic role based on how it gets food.
  - Table with three columns: Role | What they do | Examples
    - Producers ਉਤਪਾਦਕ: make food via photosynthesis (autotrophs) — green plants, algae
    - Consumers ਖਪਤਕਾਰ: eat plants or animals (heterotrophs) — deer, frog, lion, humans
    - Decomposers ਅਪਘਟਕ: break down dead bodies into simple nutrients — bacteria, fungi

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Every organism in an ecosystem plays a specific role based on how it obtains its food. Let's
  study these three roles carefully because they form the foundation of everything else in this
  chapter.

  Producers — ਉਤਪਾਦਕ — are green plants and algae. They are called autotrophs, meaning they make
  their own food. They capture sunlight and, through photosynthesis, convert carbon dioxide and
  water into glucose. They are always at the first trophic level — they are the starting point of
  every food chain.

  Consumers — ਖਪਤਕਾਰ — cannot make their own food. They are heterotrophs — they must eat other
  organisms. There are different levels of consumers. Primary consumers — like deer and grasshoppers
  — eat plants directly. Secondary consumers — like frogs — eat the primary consumers. Tertiary
  consumers — like snakes or eagles — eat the secondary consumers. Even we humans are consumers.

  Decomposers — ਅਪਘਟਕ — are the unsung heroes of the ecosystem. Bacteria and fungi break down
  dead plants and animals and the waste produced by living organisms into simple inorganic
  nutrients. These nutrients go back into the soil and are used again by producers. Without
  decomposers, dead matter would pile up everywhere and nutrients would be permanently locked
  away.

  So the cycle is: Producers make food → Consumers eat → Decomposers recycle. This keeps the
  ecosystem running continuously.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Producers = autotrophs; make food via photosynthesis; occupy T1.
  - Consumers = heterotrophs; eat other organisms; can be primary, secondary, tertiary.
  - Decomposers = bacteria and fungi; break down dead matter; return nutrients to soil.
  - Decomposers are essential — they complete the nutrient cycle.

BOARD TIP:  A common question: "What is the role of decomposers in an ecosystem?" — answer must mention nutrient recycling.

SLIDE 7: Food Chains (ਭੋਜਨ ਲੜੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition of a food chain
  - Example chain: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle
  - Note that each stage is a trophic level

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A food chain — ਭੋਜਨ ਲੜੀ — is a single, straight-line sequence that shows the feeding
  relationships between organisms. It shows us who eats whom and how energy flows from one
  organism to the next.

  Look at this example: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle.

  The grass is the producer — it uses sunlight to make food. The grasshopper eats the grass, so
  it is the primary consumer at trophic level 2. The frog eats the grasshopper — secondary
  consumer, trophic level 3. The snake eats the frog — tertiary consumer, trophic level 4. The
  eagle eats the snake — it is at trophic level 5.

  Two things are absolutely critical to remember about food chains. First: energy flows in ONE
  direction only — from the sun, to the producers, to the consumers. It never flows backwards.
  This is called unidirectional flow. Second: at each step, a large amount of energy is lost as
  heat, so by the time we reach the eagle, very little of the original energy captured by the
  grass remains.

  The arrow in a food chain always points from the organism being eaten to the organism doing
  the eating — it shows the direction of energy transfer.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - A food chain is a single feeding sequence — one straight line.
  - Energy flow is unidirectional — from Sun → Producer → Consumer (never backwards).
  - Each link in the chain is a trophic level (T1, T2, T3…).
  - The arrow shows direction of energy flow: eaten organism → eating organism.

BOARD TIP:  Drawing the arrow in the wrong direction (backwards) is a very common student mistake — always point from prey to predator.

SLIDE 8: Food Webs (ਭੋਜਨ ਜਾਲ) — Frequently Asked
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition of a food web
  - Explanation of why food webs are more stable than food chains
  - Diagram of interconnected food chains forming a web

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  In the real world, animals rarely eat just one type of food. A frog does not eat only
  grasshoppers — it might eat flies, worms, and other insects too. When many food chains are
  linked together because of these multiple feeding relationships, the result is a food web —
  ਭੋਜਨ ਜਾਲ.

  Think of a food web like a spider's web or a fishing net — many lines crossing each other,
  connecting many organisms in many different ways. Each intersection represents an organism that
  belongs to more than one food chain.

  Now here is a very important point that the board examiners love to ask: Why is a food web
  more stable than a single food chain? The answer is: because of alternative food sources. If
  one species in a food chain disappears — say, all the frogs die due to a disease — then in a
  simple food chain, the snake starves and the grasshoppers multiply out of control. The whole
  chain collapses. But in a food web, the snake might also eat rats or lizards, so it can
  survive. The system does not collapse because there are backup connections.

  This is why biodiversity — having many different species — is so important for ecosystem
  stability. A complex food web is more resilient to disturbances.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Food web = many interconnected food chains in a real ecosystem.
  - Animals typically have more than one food source — this creates the web.
  - Food webs are more stable than single food chains because of alternative food sources.
  - If one species disappears, the ecosystem can still function via other pathways.

BOARD TIP:  "Why is a food web more stable than a food chain?" is a very frequently asked 3-mark question — memorize the explanation about alternative food sources.

SLIDE 9: Trophic Levels & Ecological Pyramids
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Definition: number, biomass, or energy at each feeding step drawn as a pyramid
  - Table:
    T1 — Producer — Grass
    T2 — Primary Consumer (herbivore) — Grasshopper
    T3 — Secondary Consumer — Frog
    T4 — Tertiary Consumer — Snake
  - Note: chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels due to the 10% Law

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We have already seen that each step in a food chain is called a trophic level — ਪੋਸ਼ੀ ਪੱਧਰ.
  "Trophic" comes from the Greek word for food or nourishment. Let's look at the levels more
  carefully.

  Trophic Level 1, or T1, is always the Producers — green plants like grass. They are the most
  numerous and capture the most energy.

  T2 is the Primary Consumer — the herbivore, the plant-eater — such as a grasshopper.

  T3 is the Secondary Consumer — an organism that eats the herbivore — such as a frog.

  T4 is the Tertiary Consumer — an organism that eats the secondary consumer — such as a snake.

  Now, if we draw the number of organisms, or their total biomass, or the total energy at each
  level, we get a diagram shaped like a pyramid — the Ecological Pyramid — ਪਾਰਿਸਥਿਤਕ ਪਿਰਾਮਿਡ.
  The base is wide because there are many producers. As we go up, each level gets smaller —
  fewer individuals, less biomass, less energy — because energy is lost at every step.

  This is why food chains rarely go beyond 4 or 5 trophic levels. By the 5th level, so little
  energy remains that it cannot support another level of consumers. This is directly related to
  the 10% Law, which we will study in the next few slides.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - T1 = Producers, T2 = Primary consumers (herbivores), T3 = Secondary consumers, T4 = Tertiary consumers.
  - Ecological pyramid: base (producers) is broadest; top (top carnivores) is narrowest.
  - The pyramid narrows because energy, biomass, and organism numbers decrease going upward.
  - Food chains rarely exceed 4–5 trophic levels due to energy loss at each step.

BOARD TIP:  Be able to draw and label a 4-level ecological pyramid with correct organism names — frequently asked as a diagram question.

SLIDE 11: Energy Flow — The 10% Law
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Statement: Energy flow in ecosystems is unidirectional
  - Plants capture about 1% of the Sun's energy
  - Highlighted box: The 10% Law (10% ਦਾ ਨਿਯਮ)
  - Only 10% of energy transfers to the next trophic level; 90% is lost as heat
  - Food chains rarely go beyond 3–4 steps

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is one of the most important laws in this chapter — the 10% Law, or in Punjabi, 10% ਦਾ
  ਨਿਯਮ. Let me explain it very clearly.

  The Sun provides an enormous amount of energy. When plants carry out photosynthesis, they
  capture only about 1% of the total solar energy that falls on them — the rest is reflected or
  lost as heat.

  Now, when a grasshopper eats the grass, it does not get all the energy stored in the grass.
  The grasshopper uses most of that energy for its own life processes — moving, breathing,
  growing, staying warm. Only 10% of the energy stored in the grass is actually transferred to
  the grasshopper's body. The other 90% is lost, mainly as heat.

  The same happens at every step. When a frog eats the grasshopper, only 10% of the
  grasshopper's energy reaches the frog. When a snake eats the frog, only 10% of the frog's
  energy reaches the snake.

  So if grass has 10,000 joules of energy: the grasshopper gets 1,000 J, the frog gets 100 J,
  the snake gets 10 J, and an eagle would get only 1 J. You can see why after 4 or 5 steps,
  there is simply not enough energy left to support another organism. This is why food chains are
  short!

  This law was proposed by a scientist named Lindeman, so it is sometimes called Lindeman's 10%
  Law or the Law of Tenfold Energy Reduction.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Only 10% of energy at one trophic level is passed to the next level.
  - 90% is lost as heat at every step.
  - This is why food chains rarely have more than 4–5 trophic levels.
  - Energy flow is always unidirectional — from Sun → Producers → Consumers.

BOARD TIP:  Numerical questions based on the 10% Law are extremely common — if producers have 10,000 J, be able to calculate energy at T2, T3, T4 quickly.

SLIDE 12: Biological Magnification — Process Flow
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive sequencer: DDT accumulation through 5 trophic levels
  - Step 1: Water source — DDT at low concentration
  - Step 2: Producers (Plankton) — absorb DDT, cannot excrete it
  - Step 3: Primary Consumers (Small Fish) — eat hundreds of plankton; DDT accumulates in fat
  - Step 4: Secondary Consumers (Large Fish) — eat dozens of small fish; concentration spikes
  - Step 5: Top Carnivores (Humans/Birds) — maximum toxic dose; severe diseases possible

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we come to biological magnification — ਜੈਵਿਕ ਵਾਧਾ — one of the most alarming concepts in
  this chapter. This is also called biomagnification.

  Let's follow the story of DDT, a pesticide once widely used in farming. Farmers spray DDT on
  crops and fields to kill insects. Rainwater washes this DDT into nearby ponds and rivers. At
  first, the concentration of DDT in the water is extremely low — barely detectable.

  Step 1: The DDT is now in the water. The concentration is very low — let's say 0.003 parts per
  million (ppm).

  Step 2: Microscopic plants called plankton — the producers — absorb this DDT as they take in
  water. Here is the key problem: DDT dissolves in fat, not water. So plankton cannot excrete it.
  It builds up inside them. Now the concentration in plankton might be 0.04 ppm.

  Step 3: Small fish eat hundreds and thousands of plankton. All that DDT from thousands of
  plankton concentrates into one fish. The concentration might now be 0.5 ppm.

  Step 4: A large fish eats dozens of small fish. All of their accumulated DDT enters the large
  fish. Concentration might now be 2 ppm.

  Step 5: A human or a large bird like an eagle eats the large fish. All the DDT that was spread
  across many fish now concentrates in one body. The concentration might reach 25 ppm or more —
  thousands of times higher than the original level in the water!

  This enormous concentration in top carnivores can cause cancer, reproductive failure, immune
  system damage, and in birds, it caused egg shells to become so thin that they broke before
  hatching, wiping out entire populations.

  The lesson: even a tiny amount of non-biodegradable pollutant in the environment becomes
  extremely dangerous by the time it reaches the top of the food chain — which includes us.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Biological magnification = non-biodegradable toxins increase in concentration at each higher trophic level.
  - DDT cannot be excreted — it accumulates in fatty tissues.
  - Concentration is LOWEST in producers and HIGHEST in top carnivores (including humans).
  - This is why non-biodegradable pesticides are so dangerous.

BOARD TIP:  "Explain biological magnification with an example" is a guaranteed long-answer question — use DDT and include concentration increasing at each step.

SLIDE 13: Ozone Formation & Depletion
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Statement: Ozone (O₃) forms a protective shield against UV radiation in the upper atmosphere
  - Chemical equations:
    1. O₂ → (UV Light) → O + O
    2. O + O₂ → O₃ (Ozone)
  - Note: CFCs from old refrigerators destroy ozone and cause the "Ozone Hole"

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  High up in the Earth's atmosphere, in a layer called the stratosphere, there exists a thin but
  vital shield made of ozone — O₃ — ਓਜ਼ੋਨ. This ozone layer absorbs most of the Sun's harmful
  ultraviolet radiation before it can reach the Earth's surface. Without it, life as we know it
  could not exist.

  How does ozone form? It is a natural process. When ultraviolet rays from the Sun strike
  ordinary oxygen molecules — O₂ — they split them into two free oxygen atoms:
  O₂ → O + O (using UV light as energy).

  Each free oxygen atom is highly reactive. It quickly combines with another O₂ molecule to form
  ozone:
  O + O₂ → O₃.

  So ozone is essentially three oxygen atoms bonded together. It forms naturally in the
  stratosphere and is also continuously broken down and re-formed — it is a natural balance.

  The problem is that human beings have been releasing chemicals called CFCs —
  Chlorofluorocarbons — into the atmosphere. These were used in old refrigerators, air
  conditioners, aerosol spray cans, and fire extinguishers. CFCs rise into the stratosphere and
  react with ozone, destroying it. This has caused a thinning of the ozone layer — what we call
  the Ozone Hole — particularly over Antarctica.

  We will study the exact mechanism of CFC destruction in Slide 17. For now, remember: ozone
  forms from O₂ splitting under UV, and CFCs are its main destroyer.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Ozone (O₃) is in the stratosphere and absorbs harmful UV radiation.
  - Formation: UV splits O₂ → 2O; then O + O₂ → O₃.
  - CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) destroy the ozone layer, causing the Ozone Hole.
  - Sources of CFCs: old refrigerators, ACs, aerosol sprays.

BOARD TIP:  Write the two chemical equations for ozone formation correctly — they are often asked as a 2-mark question.

SLIDE 14: Biodegradable vs Non-biodegradable
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table comparing the two types of waste:
    - Biodegradable (ਜੈਵ-ਵਿਘਟਨਸ਼ੀਲ): broken down by microbes; examples: fruit peels, paper, cotton, cow dung, leaves; return to nature quickly
    - Non-biodegradable (ਅਜੈਵ-ਵਿਘਟਨਸ਼ੀਲ): cannot be broken down by microbes; examples: plastic, glass, DDT, metals, polythene; persist for years; cause pollution
  - Board Trap note: Non-biodegradable pesticides like DDT cause biological magnification

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  All waste that we produce can be classified into two categories based on whether
  microorganisms can break it down.

  Biodegradable waste — ਜੈਵ-ਵਿਘਟਨਸ਼ੀਲ — is waste that can be broken down by bacteria, fungi, and
  other decomposers. This includes natural things like fruit peels, vegetable scraps, fallen
  leaves, paper, cotton, and cow dung. When these are broken down, the nutrients return to the
  soil and are used again by plants. They cause minimal long-term environmental harm.

  Non-biodegradable waste — ਅਜੈਵ-ਵਿਘਟਨਸ਼ੀਲ — is waste that decomposers cannot break down. This
  includes plastic, polythene bags, glass, metals, and chemicals like DDT. Plastic takes
  anywhere from 100 to 1,000 years to degrade. These materials accumulate in soil and water,
  disrupt ecosystems, and harm or kill animals that ingest them.

  Here is the critical board trap to remember: when a non-biodegradable substance like DDT
  enters a food chain, it does not break down at any level. Instead, it accumulates and
  concentrates at each higher trophic level — which is exactly what biological magnification is.
  So the harm caused by non-biodegradable waste is not just pollution — it enters our bodies.

  The solution? Separate your waste. Biodegradable waste can be composted. Non-biodegradable
  waste must be carefully collected and either recycled or safely disposed of.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Biodegradable: broken down by microbes naturally; includes natural/organic materials.
  - Non-biodegradable: NOT broken down by microbes; persists for decades or centuries.
  - Non-biodegradable pesticides (DDT) cause biological magnification.
  - Examples must be memorized: plastic, glass, DDT = non-biodegradable; food waste, paper, cotton = biodegradable.

BOARD TIP:  Give at least two examples of each category in any answer — examiners award marks for specific examples.

SLIDE 17: How CFCs Destroy Ozone — Process Flow
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive sequencer with 5 steps of CFC-driven ozone destruction:
    Step 1: Release — CFCs from refrigerators/ACs drift to stratosphere
    Step 2: Breakdown — UV breaks CFC, releasing free chlorine (Cl) atom
    Step 3: Attack — Cl reacts with O₃, stealing an oxygen → ClO + O₂
    Step 4: Chain Reaction — Cl is freed again and destroys thousands more O₃ molecules
    Step 5: Result — Ozone layer thins; Ozone Hole forms; more UV reaches Earth

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We learned that CFCs destroy ozone. Now let's understand exactly HOW this happens, step by
  step, because this process flow is frequently asked in the board exam.

  Step 1 — Release: CFCs, or Chlorofluorocarbons, were widely used as coolants in refrigerators
  and air conditioners, as propellants in aerosol spray cans, and in fire extinguishers. When
  these devices leak or are improperly disposed of, the CFC gas escapes into the air. Being very
  stable and lightweight, CFCs drift upward all the way into the stratosphere — the layer of
  atmosphere where the ozone layer sits.

  Step 2 — Breakdown: In the stratosphere, CFCs are exposed to intense UV radiation. This UV
  energy is strong enough to break apart the CFC molecule, releasing a highly reactive free
  chlorine atom — Cl.

  Step 3 — Attack: This free chlorine atom is chemically very aggressive. It attacks an ozone
  molecule: Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂. In other words, it steals one oxygen atom from ozone, converting
  ozone (O₃) into ordinary oxygen (O₂). The ozone is destroyed.

  Step 4 — Chain Reaction: Here is what makes CFCs so devastatingly effective. After the
  chlorine reacts with ozone to form ClO, that ClO molecule is unstable. It quickly reacts with
  another free oxygen atom: ClO + O → Cl + O₂. The chlorine atom is released again — free to
  attack another ozone molecule! A single chlorine atom can destroy tens of thousands of ozone
  molecules before it is finally removed from the stratosphere.

  Step 5 — Result: Slowly, the ozone layer thins. Over Antarctica, this thinning became so
  severe that a massive hole — the Ozone Hole — formed. With less ozone to absorb UV radiation,
  more UV reaches the Earth's surface, causing the harmful effects we will see in the next slide.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - CFCs rise to the stratosphere where UV light breaks them apart, releasing free Cl atoms.
  - Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ (ozone is destroyed).
  - The Cl is regenerated and can destroy thousands of ozone molecules in a chain reaction.
  - A single CFC molecule can cause enormous cumulative damage.

BOARD TIP:  Write the reaction Cl + O₃ → ClO + O₂ correctly in your answer — it demonstrates understanding and earns full marks.

SLIDE 18: Effects of Ozone Depletion
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - A thinner ozone layer allows harmful UV rays to reach the surface
  - Effects listed with icons:
    - Skin cancer and skin ageing in humans
    - Cataracts (damage to the eyes)
    - Weakened immune system
    - Reduced crop yield and harm to phytoplankton
  - Solution: The Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFC production worldwide

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  When the ozone layer is depleted, more ultraviolet radiation reaches the Earth's surface.
  This has serious and wide-ranging consequences.

  For human health: UV radiation can cause skin cancer. It damages the DNA in skin cells,
  causing uncontrolled cell growth. UV also causes premature skin ageing — wrinkles and age
  spots. It damages the eyes, leading to cataracts, a condition where the lens of the eye
  becomes cloudy, causing blurred vision and, if untreated, blindness. UV radiation also
  suppresses the immune system, making the body less able to fight infections.

  other ozone-depleting substances. This is considered one of the most successful environmental
  treaties in history. Since the ban, the ozone layer has been slowly recovering.

  Remember: 1987, Montreal Protocol, banned CFCs — these three facts are important for the exam.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:

  This type of "spot the error" question is very common in board exams. Always check the
  direction of arrows whenever you draw or read a food chain.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:

  - The corrected chain: Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake.

SLIDE 22: The 10% Law — Energy Flow Numerical (Interactive)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Interactive sequencer tracing energy from 10,000 J at producers through 4 trophic levels
  - Step 1: Producers (Grass) = 10,000 J
  - Step 2: Primary Consumers (Deer) = 1,000 J (10% of 10,000)
  - Step 3: Secondary Consumers (Snake) = 100 J (10% of 1,000)
  - Step 4: Tertiary Consumers (Hawk) = 10 J (10% of 100)
  - Conclusion: Energy shrinks so fast, food chains rarely have more than 4–5 trophic levels
  - Footer: Lindeman's 10% Law

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is the slide that shows you how to solve the numerical questions based on the 10% Law —
  and these definitely come in the board exam. Let's work through it together.

  Imagine a grassland where grass (the producers) captures 10,000 joules of energy from
  the Sun. This is trophic level 1.

  Now, when deer eat the grass, only 10% of that energy is transferred. 10% of 10,000 J is
  1,000 J. So the deer have 1,000 J. The other 9,000 J was lost — used by the grass for its own
  life processes or released as heat.

  Next, a snake eats the deer. 10% of 1,000 J is 100 J. The snake has 100 J. 900 J was lost.

  Then a hawk eats the snake. 10% of 100 J is just 10 J. The hawk has only 10 J.

  Think about this — we started with 10,000 joules and after just four trophic levels, only 10
  joules remain. That is 99.9% of the original energy gone! If there were a fifth level — say a
  human eating the hawk — they would receive only 1 J.

  Now you can clearly see why food chains almost never go beyond 4 or 5 levels. There simply is
  not enough energy left to sustain another level of life.

  This law was proposed by the ecologist Raymond Lindeman and is called Lindeman's 10% Law or
  the Law of Tenfold Reduction.

  Practice the calculation: if a question says "producers have X joules, calculate energy at T3",
  multiply by 0.1 twice (or divide by 100).

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - 10,000 J → 1,000 J → 100 J → 10 J → 1 J (10% at each step).
  - To find energy at any trophic level: multiply previous level by 0.1 (or divide by 10).
  - Food chains stop at 4–5 levels because energy becomes insufficient beyond that.
  - This is Lindeman's 10% Law — know the scientist's name.

BOARD TIP:  A very common numerical: "Producers have 20,000 J — find energy at T4." Answer: 20,000 × 0.1 × 0.1 × 0.1 = 20 J. Practice this pattern.

  Question 7: The 3 R's of waste management are Reduce, Reuse and:
  A. Recycle  B. Reject  C. Return
  — Answer: A. Recycle. The three R's are Reduce, Reuse, Recycle.

  Question 8: Ozone (O₃) protects the Earth by absorbing which rays?
  A. Infrared rays  B. Ultraviolet rays  C. Radio waves
  — Answer: B. Ultraviolet rays. This is the primary function of the ozone layer.

  - Ozone depletion = CFCs (not CO₂, not methane).
  - T3 = carnivores (secondary consumers).
  - Biomagnification is maximum in top carnivores.
