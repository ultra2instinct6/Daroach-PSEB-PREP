SLIDE 2: Core Vocabulary (ਮੁੱਖ ਸ਼ਬਦਾਵਲੀ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Six key terms with Punjabi translations and pronunciation guides:
    Reflection → ਪਰਾਵਰਤਨ (Bouncing back)
    Refraction → ਅਪਵਰਤਨ (Bending of light)
    Concave → ਅਵਤਲ (Curved inwards)
    Convex → ਉੱਤਲ (Curved outwards)
    Virtual Image → ਅਭਾਸੀ ਪ੍ਰਤੀਬਿੰਬ (Not on screen)
    Refractive Index → ਅਪਵਰਤਨ ਅੰਕ (Measure of bending)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we go into the physics, let us build our vocabulary. These six terms are the building blocks of this entire chapter. If you know these words confidently, the concepts will fall into place automatically.

  First: Reflection — ਪਰਾਵਰਤਨ. This means bouncing back. When light hits a mirror and bounces back towards you — that is reflection. Think of how you see your face in a mirror every morning — that is ਪਰਾਵਰਤਨ.

  Second: Refraction — ਅਪਵਰਤਨ. This means bending of light. When light travels from one medium into another — say, from air into water — it changes its speed and bends. That bending is called ਅਪਵਰਤਨ.

  Third: Concave — ਅਵਤਲ. This means curved inward, like the inside of a bowl or a spoon. A concave mirror or concave lens curves inward. Remember it this way: a Cave goes inward — conCAVE, ਅਵਤਲ.

  Fourth: Convex — ਉੱਤਲ. This means curved outward, like the outside of a ball or the back of a spoon. Convex bulges out towards you.

  Fifth: Virtual Image — ਅਭਾਸੀ ਪ੍ਰਤੀਬਿੰਬ. This is an image that cannot be caught on a screen. When you look into a plane mirror, your image is inside the mirror — you cannot put a screen there and capture it. That is a virtual image, or ਅਭਾਸੀ ਪ੍ਰਤੀਬਿੰਬ.

  Sixth: Refractive Index — ਅਪਵਰਤਨ ਅੰਕ. This is a number that tells us how much a medium bends light. The higher this number, the more it bends light. We will study this in detail later.

  Make sure you can write each of these Punjabi terms correctly — they sometimes appear in 1-mark questions.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Reflection = light bouncing back (ਪਰਾਵਰਤਨ).
  - Refraction = light bending when changing medium (ਅਪਵਰਤਨ).
  - Concave = curves inward (ਅਵਤਲ); Convex = curves outward (ਉੱਤਲ).
  - Virtual image cannot be projected onto a screen.
  - Refractive index is a number measuring how much a medium bends light.

BOARD TIP: 1-mark questions often ask for the Punjabi term or the one-line meaning — learn all six here.

SLIDE 3: More Key Terms (ਹੋਰ ਸ਼ਬਦ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Six more terms with Punjabi translations:
    Normal → ਅਭਿਲੰਬ (Line ⊥ to surface)
    Centre of Curvature (C) → ਵਕ੍ਰਤਾ ਕੇਂਦਰ (Center of the sphere)
    Principal Axis → ਮੁੱਖ ਧੁਰਾ (Line through P and C)
    Focal Length (f) → ਫੋਕਸ ਦੂਰੀ (Distance P to F)
    Power of a Lens (P) → ਲੈਨਜ਼ ਦੀ ਸ਼ਕਤੀ (P = 1/f)
    Dioptre (D) → ਡਾਇਓਪਟਰ (Unit of lens power)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Excellent — now six more essential terms. These are the terms you will use constantly when drawing ray diagrams and applying formulas.

  The Normal — ਅਭਿਲੰਬ — is an imaginary line drawn perpendicular (at 90°) to the surface at the exact point where light hits. All angles in reflection and refraction are measured from this normal, never from the surface itself. This is a very common mistake students make — always measure from the normal, not the surface.

  Centre of Curvature — ਵਕ੍ਰਤਾ ਕੇਂਦਰ, written as C. A spherical mirror is a part of a hollow sphere. The centre of that complete sphere is called the Centre of Curvature. It is marked C on all ray diagrams.

  Principal Axis — ਮੁੱਖ ਧੁਰਾ. This is the straight line that passes through the Pole P and the Centre of Curvature C. Think of it as the central line of symmetry of your mirror or lens diagram.

  Focal Length — ਫੋਕਸ ਦੂਰੀ, denoted by f. This is the distance from the Pole P to the Principal Focus F. It is one of the most important quantities you will put into formulas.

  Power of a Lens — ਲੈਨਜ਼ ਦੀ ਸ਼ਕਤੀ, denoted by P. The formula is simply P = 1/f. A lens with a shorter focal length bends light more, so it has more power.

  Dioptre — ਡਾਇਓਪਟਰ, abbreviated D. This is the SI unit of lens power. When focal length is measured in metres, P comes out in dioptres. So a lens of focal length 0.5 m has a power of 1/0.5 = 2 D.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Normal is perpendicular to surface — angles are always measured from the normal.
  - C is the centre of the imaginary sphere; the mirror is a part of that sphere.
  - Principal Axis passes through P and C.
  - Focal length f = distance from Pole to Focus.
  - P = 1/f (in metres); unit is dioptre (D).

BOARD TIP: Drawing the normal correctly in ray diagrams earns marks — students who measure angles from the surface instead of the normal lose marks.

SLIDE 4: Reading — The Bending of Light
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Read-aloud passage about reflection and refraction
  - Examples: straw in water, magnifying glass, spectacle lens, mirrors, microscopes, telescopes
  - Play/Stop button for audio narration

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us now read a short passage together that connects this chapter to the real world. I will read it aloud, and you follow along.

  "Have you ever noticed that a straw in a glass of water looks broken at the surface?" — Yes! This is something you have all seen at home. The straw is perfectly straight, but it appears bent. Why? Because light coming from the part of the straw under water bends when it comes out into air. Your eye traces the bent ray backward in a straight line and gets the wrong position — so the straw looks broken. This is refraction, ਅਪਵਰਤਨ.

  "When light travels from air into water or glass, it slows down and changes direction." — This is the key mechanism of refraction. The slowing-down causes the bending. Just like a car wheel that hits soft ground on one side slows down on that side and causes the car to turn — light bends because one side of the wavefront slows down before the other.

  "A tiny lens in a magnifying glass can gather sunlight into a burning point." — You may have used a magnifying glass to focus sunlight on paper and burn it as a child. That focused point is the principal focus of the convex lens. The lens bends all parallel rays of sunlight to meet at one single point, generating enormous heat.

  "Mirrors work by reflection, bouncing light back so precisely that we see our own face." — The precision of reflection — ∠i = ∠r — is what creates a perfect mirror image.

  "Humans built microscopes to see germs and telescopes to see distant galaxies." — All of modern science — from biology to astronomy — depends on our ability to control the bending and bouncing of light. This chapter is the foundation of all optics.

  Take a moment to think about two examples from your own daily life where you see reflection, and two where you see refraction. You will be surprised how many there are.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - The straw-in-water illusion is caused by refraction at the water-air boundary.
  - Refraction occurs because light slows down when entering a denser medium.
  - Concave mirrors and convex lenses both converge light to a focus.
  - The laws of optics underpin microscopes, telescopes, cameras, and spectacles.

BOARD TIP: The straw-in-water example is a classic "give a real-life example of refraction" answer — remember it for 1- or 2-mark questions.

SLIDE 5: Laws of Reflection
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Statement of the two laws of reflection
  - Key terms: angle of incidence (∠i), angle of reflection (∠r), normal, plane of incidence

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us now state the Laws of Reflection formally. These two laws govern every reflection — whether from a plane mirror, a concave mirror, or a convex mirror. They are also the starting point for any ray diagram.

  Law 1: The angle of incidence equals the angle of reflection. In symbols: ∠i = ∠r. The angle of incidence is the angle between the incoming ray and the normal at the point of incidence. The angle of reflection is the angle between the reflected ray and the same normal. These two angles are always equal. If I shine a torch at a mirror at 30° to the normal, it bounces back at exactly 30° to the normal — no more, no less.

  Law 2: The incident ray, the reflected ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane. This means the reflection happens in a flat, two-dimensional plane — the ray does not "jump out" of the plane. Practically, this allows us to draw accurate 2D ray diagrams on paper.

  Let me make sure the diagram is clear in your mind: draw a mirror as a horizontal line. Draw the normal as a vertical dotted line at the point where the ray hits. The incoming ray makes angle i with the normal. The outgoing ray makes angle r with the normal. Both rays are on the same side of the normal, in the same plane. And i always equals r.

  These two laws apply equally to all types of mirrors — plane, concave, and convex.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - ∠i = ∠r (angle of incidence = angle of reflection) — this is the core law.
  - All three — incident ray, reflected ray, normal — lie in the same plane.
  - Angles are always measured from the normal, NOT from the mirror surface.
  - These laws apply to all types of mirrors without exception.

BOARD TIP: A very frequent 2-mark question asks you to "state the laws of reflection" — write both laws clearly and completely, not just ∠i = ∠r.

SLIDE 6: Real vs. Virtual Images
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Comparison table with four rows:
    Feature | Real Image | Virtual Image
    Formed by | Rays actually meeting | Rays only appearing to meet
    Can be on a screen? | Yes (Real can) | No (Virtual cannot)
    Orientation | Always inverted | Always erect
    Example | Cinema screen image | Plane-mirror image

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Before we go further into mirrors and lenses, we must clearly understand the difference between a real image and a virtual image. This distinction comes up in almost every question in this chapter.

  A Real Image is formed when the actual reflected or refracted light rays physically converge — they actually meet at a point. Because the rays genuinely cross at that point, you can place a screen there and you will see the image on the screen. Think of a projector in a cinema hall — it forms a real image on the screen at the back of the hall. A real image is always inverted, meaning it is upside down compared to the object.

  A Virtual Image is formed when the light rays do not actually meet — they only appear to meet when extended backward. Your eye traces the diverging rays backward and your brain perceives an image where the rays seem to come from, even though no actual light rays are there. Because no real rays meet there, you cannot capture it on a screen. The image you see in a plane mirror — it appears to be behind the mirror, but if you put a screen behind the mirror, nothing appears on it. That is a virtual image. Virtual images are always erect — right-side up.

  So the quick test: Can you catch it on a screen? Yes → Real. No → Virtual.

  Remember: Real images are always inverted; Virtual images are always erect. This rule alone will help you answer many questions.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Real image: actual rays meet; can be projected on a screen; always inverted.
  - Virtual image: rays only appear to meet (extended backward); cannot be on screen; always erect.
  - "Screen test": if you can project it → real; if you cannot → virtual.
  - Cinema projection = real image; plane mirror reflection = virtual image.

BOARD TIP: Frequent 1-mark question — "What type of image is formed by a plane mirror?" Answer: Virtual and erect. Write both words.

SLIDE 7: Plane Mirror — Image Characteristics
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four listed properties of the image in a plane mirror:
    1. Virtual and Erect
    2. Same Size (equal to object height)
    3. Same Distance (image distance = object distance)
    4. Laterally Inverted (AMBULANCE example)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  A plane mirror is the simplest mirror — perfectly flat. Before studying curved mirrors, you must know these four absolute properties of the image formed by a plane mirror. These four points come up in exams again and again.

  Property 1: The image is Virtual and Erect. The image forms behind the mirror — you cannot touch it or project it. And it is right-side up, not flipped upside down.

  Property 2: The image is the Same Size as the object. If you are 5 feet tall, your image in the mirror is exactly 5 feet tall. The magnification is exactly 1 — neither enlarged nor diminished.

  Property 3: The image is formed at the Same Distance behind the mirror as the object is in front of it. If you stand 2 metres in front of a plane mirror, your image appears to be 2 metres behind the mirror. If you move closer to the mirror, your image also moves closer at the same rate.

  Property 4: The image is Laterally Inverted. Left and right are swapped. Your right hand appears as the left hand in the mirror. This is why the word AMBULANCE is written in reverse on ambulance vehicles — when a driver sees it in their rear-view mirror, the laterally inverted reflection reads correctly as AMBULANCE, prompting them to give way.

  These four properties are specific to plane mirrors. Curved mirrors will give different results depending on where the object is placed.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Virtual and erect — cannot be projected on screen; right-side up.
  - Same size as object — magnification = 1.
  - Image distance = object distance from the mirror.
  - Laterally inverted — left-right swap (AMBULANCE example).

BOARD TIP: The "AMBULANCE" example for lateral inversion is a classic application question — worth 1 to 2 marks and very commonly asked.

SLIDE 8: Spherical Mirrors — Key Terms
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Introductory statement: concave reflects from inside; convex reflects from outside
  - Table of five terms:
    Pole (P) | Centre of mirror surface
    Centre of Curvature (C) | Centre of the sphere
    Radius of Curvature (R) | Distance P to C
    Principal Focus (F) | Where parallel rays converge (or appear to)
    Focal Length (f) | Distance P to F
  - Key relation: R = 2f

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we move to spherical mirrors. A spherical mirror is essentially a piece cut from the surface of a hollow sphere. If light reflects from the inner (concave) side, it is a Concave Mirror — ਅਵਤਲ ਦਰਪਣ. If light reflects from the outer (convex) side, it is a Convex Mirror — ਉੱਤਲ ਦਰਪਣ.

  Let us go through the key points on the mirror one by one.

  The Pole, P, is the geometric centre of the mirror's reflecting surface — the midpoint of the circular boundary of the mirror.

  The Centre of Curvature, C, is the centre of the complete sphere of which the mirror is a part. It lies in front of a concave mirror and behind a convex mirror. On ray diagrams, you always mark C.

  The Radius of Curvature, R, is the distance from the Pole P to the Centre of Curvature C. It is the radius of the original sphere.

  The Principal Focus, F, is the point where rays parallel to the principal axis converge after reflection (for a concave mirror) or appear to diverge from after reflection (for a convex mirror). For a concave mirror, F is in front of the mirror; for a convex mirror, F is behind the mirror.

  The Focal Length, f, is the distance from the Pole P to the Principal Focus F.

  Now, the most important relationship in this section: R = 2f. The focus is exactly halfway between the Pole and the Centre of Curvature. So if R = 20 cm, then f = 10 cm. If f = 15 cm, then R = 30 cm. This relationship is used very frequently in numericals — always verify both values when given one.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Concave mirror: reflecting surface is on the inside (cave side); convex is outside.
  - Pole P = centre of mirror; Centre of Curvature C = centre of parent sphere.
  - Principal Focus F = where parallel rays converge (concave) or appear to diverge from (convex).
  - f = distance from P to F; R = distance from P to C.
  - R = 2f — the focal length is always half the radius of curvature.

BOARD TIP: R = 2f is used in almost every mirror numerical — if the question gives R, halve it to get f before using the mirror formula.

SLIDE 9: Rules for Mirror Ray Diagrams
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four rules for constructing ray diagrams with spherical mirrors:
    Rule 1: Parallel ray → passes through F after reflection
    Rule 2: Ray through F → becomes parallel after reflection
    Rule 3: Ray through C → reflects back along the same path
    Rule 4: Ray at pole P → reflects making equal angles with the axis

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  To draw a ray diagram for a spherical mirror, you pick any TWO of these four standard rays and draw them from the top of the object. Where they meet after reflection gives the position of the image. Let me explain each rule carefully.

  Rule 1: A ray parallel to the principal axis — after reflecting from a concave mirror — passes through the Principal Focus F. After reflecting from a convex mirror, it appears to diverge from F (which is behind the mirror). This ray starts horizontal and bends toward F.

  Rule 2: A ray that is heading toward the Principal Focus F — before hitting a concave mirror — reflects back parallel to the principal axis. This is the reverse of Rule 1. For a convex mirror, a ray directed toward F (behind the mirror) reflects back parallel.

  Rule 3: A ray directed toward the Centre of Curvature C travels along the radius of the sphere — so it hits the mirror surface perpendicularly — and reflects back along exactly the same path. Remember, the normal at any point of a sphere passes through the centre, so ∠i = 0° and ∠r = 0°. The ray retraces its path.

  Rule 4: A ray hitting the Pole P of the mirror reflects just like it would off a plane mirror at that point — making equal angles with the principal axis on both sides. The principal axis itself acts as the normal at the pole.

  In your exam, always use Rules 1 and 2 together, or Rules 1 and 3. They are the cleanest pair. Draw both rays, and the point where their reflected rays intersect is the image location. The image is the intersection point.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Use any TWO rules to locate an image — they must intersect.
  - Rule 1: parallel ray → through F. Rule 2: through F → parallel. (Reverse of each other.)
  - Rule 3: through C → reflects back on itself (angle of incidence = 0°).
  - Rule 4: ray at pole → reflects at equal angle to the axis.
  - In board diagrams, clearly show arrowheads on all rays.

BOARD TIP: Board examiners look for: (1) two rays drawn, (2) correct labels C, F, P, (3) image clearly marked at intersection. Missing any of these loses marks.

SLIDE 10: Concave Mirror — Image for Each Position
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Table showing image formation for six object positions:
    At infinity → At F, point-sized, real, inverted
    Beyond C → Between F and C, diminished, real, inverted
    At C → At C, same size, real, inverted
    Between C and F → Beyond C, enlarged, real, inverted
    At F → At infinity, very large, real, inverted
    Between P and F → Behind mirror, enlarged, virtual, erect

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This table is one of the most important tables in the entire chapter. For the concave mirror, the nature, size, and position of the image change depending on where the object is placed. You must know all six rows for the board exam.

  Let me go through each case carefully.

  Case 1: Object at infinity. When parallel rays come in from a very distant object — like the sun or a star — they all converge at the Principal Focus F. The image is at F, it is point-sized, real, and inverted. This is the principle behind a solar cooker and a concave reflector in a torch.

  Case 2: Object beyond C. The image forms between F and C. It is diminished, real, and inverted. Think of a camera taking a photo of a distant building — the image on the film is smaller than the building.

  Case 3: Object at C. The image also forms at C. It is the same size, real, and inverted. Object and image are at the same point.

  Case 4: Object between C and F. Now the image jumps beyond C. It is enlarged, real, and inverted. This is used in reflecting telescopes — the object (e.g., moon) is between C and F, giving a magnified image.

  Case 5: Object at F. The reflected rays become parallel — they never meet in front of the mirror. We say the image is at infinity. In practice, this is used in searchlights and vehicle headlights — placing the bulb at F produces a parallel beam.

  Case 6: Object between P and F. THIS IS THE SPECIAL CASE. The image is behind the mirror — virtual, erect, and enlarged. This is how a shaving mirror and a make-up mirror work. You hold your face close (between P and F) and you get a magnified, upright reflection. This is the ONLY position where a concave mirror gives a virtual image.

  A quick memory trick for the size: as the object moves from infinity toward the mirror, the image starts at F (tiny), moves back toward C (smaller), reaches C (same size), then grows larger as the object goes between C and F, then goes to infinity when the object is at F, and finally the image becomes virtual when the object passes F toward P.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - There are SIX positions to know — learn them in order.
  - Image is ALWAYS real and inverted EXCEPT when object is between P and F.
  - Between P and F → virtual, erect, enlarged (shaving/make-up mirror use).
  - Object at F → image at infinity (searchlight/headlight principle).
  - Object at C → image at C, same size (standard reference case).

BOARD TIP: This entire table is a high-probability 3-mark or 5-mark question. Learn all 6 rows — object position, image position, size, and nature.

SLIDE 11: Convex Mirror — Uses
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Key property: Convex mirror always forms virtual, erect, and diminished image
  - Two main uses: rear-view mirrors; blind turns and shop security
  - Explanation of wider field of view

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The convex mirror — ਉੱਤਲ ਦਰਪਣ — has one unique and permanent property: no matter where you place the object, the image is always virtual, erect, and diminished. This never changes. You do not need to worry about six different cases here — it is always the same.

  Why? Because a convex mirror is a diverging mirror. The reflecting surface curves away from you, so reflected rays always diverge — they spread apart. Diverging reflected rays, when traced backward, appear to meet behind the mirror, giving a virtual, erect, diminished image behind the mirror.

  Now, why is this useful?

  Use 1: Rear-view mirrors in vehicles. You have seen the flat mirror on the left side of the road and the convex mirror on the right or inside the vehicle. Because the image is diminished, the mirror can capture a much wider area in a small mirror. You can see cars far behind you AND cars slightly to the side — all in one glance. The trade-off is that objects appear smaller and therefore farther than they actually are. This is why vehicles in India often write "Objects in mirror are closer than they appear."

  Use 2: Security mirrors at shop corners and blind turns. Convex mirrors placed at road bends and in shopping malls give a wide-angle view of oncoming traffic or activity in a wide area of the store.

  The key advantage is the wider field of view because reflected rays from a large area are diverged and packed into a small mirror surface.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Convex mirror: ALWAYS virtual, erect, diminished — no exceptions.
  - Diverging mirror → reflected rays spread out → image is always behind the mirror.
  - Wider field of view → main advantage → used in rear-view mirrors and security mirrors.
  - Convex mirrors make objects appear smaller and farther away.

BOARD TIP: "Why is a convex mirror used as a rear-view mirror?" is a very common 2-mark question. Answer must include: (1) always virtual, erect, diminished; (2) gives wider field of view.

  - Two target columns: Concave Mirror (ਅਵਤਲ ਦਰਪਣ — converging); Convex Mirror (ਉੱਤਲ ਦਰਪਣ — diverging)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  We now have a sorting activity — and this is excellent practice because a common exam question asks you to identify which type of mirror is used for which purpose.

  - Concave: torch reflector, shaving mirror, dentist's mirror — all need focusing or magnification.
  - Convex: rear-view mirror, security mirror, blind-turn mirror — all need wide field of view.
  - The distinguishing principle: concave converges (focuses); convex diverges (spreads).

BOARD TIP: "Name two uses of a concave mirror" and "Name two uses of a convex mirror" are frequent 2-mark questions — you need to remember 3 of each.

SLIDE 13: New Cartesian Sign Convention
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four rules of the New Cartesian Sign Convention:
    1. All distances measured from the Pole P
    2. Direction of incident light = positive; against it = negative
    3. Heights above axis = positive; below = negative
  - Result: concave f is negative (−); convex f is positive (+)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The New Cartesian Sign Convention is an agreement — a system — that tells us how to assign positive and negative signs to distances in mirror and lens problems. Without following this consistently, your numerical answers will be wrong. Let me explain it clearly.

  Rule 1: All distances are measured from the Pole P. P is your origin — the zero point. Every distance — object distance u, image distance v, focal length f — is measured from P.

  Rule 2: The incident light normally travels from left to right in our diagrams. Distances measured in the same direction as the incident light (to the right of P) are positive. Distances measured against the direction of incident light (to the left of P) are negative. Since objects are normally placed in front of the mirror (to the left of P in the standard diagram), the object distance u is always negative.

  Rule 3: Heights measured upward from the principal axis are positive. Heights measured downward are negative. An upright (erect) object has a positive height. An inverted image has a negative height.

  Now, applying these rules to focal length: For a concave mirror, the focus F is in front of the mirror — in the direction from which light comes — which means it is on the negative side. So the focal length of a concave mirror is negative (−f). For a convex mirror, the focus F is behind the mirror — on the positive side — so focal length is positive (+f).

  This might seem counterintuitive at first, but once you practice even two or three numericals, the signs become second nature. Always write down your given values with their correct signs before substituting into the formula.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - All distances measured from Pole P.
  - Against incident light direction → negative; with incident light → positive.
  - Object is always in front of mirror → u is always negative.
  - Concave mirror: f is negative. Convex mirror: f is positive.
  - Heights above axis = positive; inverted image height = negative.

BOARD TIP: Incorrect signs are the #1 cause of wrong answers in mirror numericals. Always list u, v, f with signs BEFORE substituting into the formula.

SLIDE 15: Numerical — Mirror Formula Step-by-Step
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Problem: Object 15 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm. Find v.
  - Step-by-step reveal (5 steps):
    Step 1: u = −15 cm, f = −10 cm, v = ?
    Step 2: Formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
    Step 3: Rearrange: 1/v = 1/f − 1/u
    Step 4: Substitute: 1/v = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −1/10 + 1/15 = (−3 + 2)/30 = −1/30
    Step 5: Answer: v = −30 cm (real, in front of mirror)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Let us solve a complete mirror numerical step by step. This is the exact format you should follow in your board exam — every step shown, every sign correct, units written.

  The Problem: An object is placed 15 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm. Find the image distance v.

  Step 1 — Write down the data with signs. The object is in front of the mirror, so u = −15 cm. The mirror is concave, so f = −10 cm. We need to find v.

  Step 2 — Write the formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f.

  Step 3 — Rearrange to find 1/v: subtract 1/u from both sides, giving 1/v = 1/f − 1/u.

  Step 4 — Substitute the values. 1/v = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15). This equals −1/10 + 1/15. To subtract these fractions, find a common denominator of 30: −3/30 + 2/30 = −1/30. So 1/v = −1/30.

  Step 5 — Find v: v = −30 cm. The negative sign tells us the image is in front of the mirror — on the same side as the object — which means it is a REAL image.

  Let me now also find the magnification for practice: m = −v/u = −(−30)/(−15) = −2. The magnitude is 2, so the image is twice as large as the object (enlarged). The negative sign confirms the image is real and inverted.

  Always finish a mirror numerical by stating the nature of the image: here, the image is real, inverted, enlarged, and 30 cm in front of the mirror.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Always assign signs first: u = −15 cm (in front), f = −10 cm (concave).
  - Rearrange: 1/v = 1/f − 1/u.
  - v = −30 cm → negative means real image (in front of mirror).
  - m = −v/u = −2 → negative means inverted; |m| = 2 means enlarged.
  - State the full nature of the image in your conclusion sentence.

BOARD TIP: In board exams, showing each arithmetic step earns step marks. Even if your final answer is slightly off, you still get marks for correct method and signs.

SLIDE 16: Rules for Lens Ray Diagrams
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Three rules for constructing ray diagrams for lenses:
    Rule 1 (Parallel): Ray parallel to axis → passes through F₂ on the other side
    Rule 2 (Focus): Ray through F₁ → emerges parallel to axis
    Rule 3 (Optical Center): Ray through optical center O → goes completely straight, no deviation
  - Emphasis on "Board Exception" for Rule 3

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Lenses work by refraction — light bends as it passes through the glass. For drawing lens ray diagrams, we have three standard rules. These are very similar to mirror rules, but with one crucial difference.

  Rule 1 — Parallel ray: A ray of light travelling parallel to the principal axis, after passing through a convex lens, converges at the second Principal Focus F₂ on the other side of the lens. For a concave lens, it appears to diverge from F₂ — trace it backward and it meets at F₂ on the same side as the object.

  Rule 2 — Focus ray: A ray that passes through the first Principal Focus F₁ (in front of the lens for a convex lens) emerges from the lens parallel to the principal axis. This is the reverse of Rule 1 — parallel and focus rays are mutual reverses of each other.

  Rule 3 — Optical Centre ray: THIS IS THE CRUCIAL BOARD EXCEPTION. A ray of light that passes through the exact Optical Centre O of the lens continues completely straight — it goes through the lens without any bending or deviation whatsoever. No refraction occurs at the optical centre. This is unique to lenses; there is no equivalent in mirror rules.

  This third rule is extremely important and also a very common source of mistakes. Students sometimes draw the optical centre ray as if it bends. No — it goes straight through. The optical centre ray gives you the direction of the image, while the parallel ray or focus ray gives you the height.

  For a ray diagram, draw: (1) the parallel ray → bends to F₂, and (2) the optical centre ray → goes straight. The intersection of these two gives the image position.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Rule 1: Parallel → through F₂ (convex) or appears from F₂ (concave).
  - Rule 2: Through F₁ → emerges parallel to axis.
  - Rule 3 (CRITICAL): Through optical centre O → NO BENDING — goes completely straight.
  - Use Rules 1 and 3 together for the clearest diagrams.
  - Lenses have two focal points: F₁ (object side) and F₂ (image side).

BOARD TIP: Drawing a ray through the optical centre and showing it bending is a common diagram error. It must go completely straight — examiners specifically check this.

SLIDE 17: The Image Formation Cheat Sheet
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Summary table of all four optical devices:
    Concave Mirror | Concave Mirror | Real & Inverted | Virtual & Erect only if object between P and F
    Convex Mirror | Always Virtual, Erect, Diminished | Used as rear-view mirror (wider field)
    Convex Lens | Real & Inverted | Virtual & Erect only if object between O and F₁
    Concave Lens | Always Virtual, Erect, Diminished | Similar to convex mirror

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This cheat sheet gives you the big picture for all four optical devices at once. Look at this table carefully — it is a brilliant study tool because it highlights both the general rule and the special exception for each device.

  Row 1 — Concave Mirror: Typically forms a real, inverted image — that is the general behaviour when the object is outside the focus. The special exception is when the object is very close, between P and F — then and only then does a concave mirror form a virtual, erect, and enlarged image.

  Row 2 — Convex Mirror: ALWAYS forms a virtual, erect, diminished image. There is no exception. The reason it is used as a rear-view mirror is this wide-angle, always-upright, always-safe image it provides.

  Row 3 — Convex Lens: Typically forms a real, inverted image — when the object is beyond the focus. The special exception is when the object is between the optical centre O and F₁ — then the convex lens acts like a magnifying glass and forms a virtual, erect, enlarged image. This is exactly the same situation as the concave mirror's special case.

  Row 4 — Concave Lens: ALWAYS forms a virtual, erect, diminished image. There is no exception. A concave lens is a diverging lens, just as a convex mirror is a diverging mirror — so their behaviour is analogous. Both always give the same type of image regardless of object position.

  Notice the beautiful symmetry here: Concave mirrors and convex lenses are both converging devices — they generally form real, inverted images (with one close-object exception). Convex mirrors and concave lenses are both diverging devices — they always form virtual, erect, diminished images, no exceptions.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Converging devices (concave mirror, convex lens): generally real+inverted; exception when object is very close.
  - Diverging devices (convex mirror, concave lens): ALWAYS virtual, erect, diminished — no exceptions.
  - The close-object exception for concave mirror = between P and F.
  - The close-object exception for convex lens = between O and F₁.
  - Convex mirror ↔ concave lens: same behavior (both always diverging).

BOARD TIP: This table is frequently tested as a "compare and contrast" or "fill in the table" question for 3–5 marks. Learn all four rows.

SLIDE 19: Decoding Magnification (ਵੱਡਦਰਸ਼ਣ)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Magnification formula: m = h'/h = −v/u
  - Rule 1 (Sign): m negative → real and inverted; m positive → virtual and erect
  - Rule 2 (Number): |m| > 1 → enlarged; |m| < 1 → diminished; |m| = 1 → same size

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Magnification — ਵੱਡਦਰਸ਼ਣ — is a formula that immediately tells you everything about the image. Once you calculate m, you know whether the image is real or virtual, and whether it is bigger, smaller, or the same size as the object.

  The formula is: m = h' divided by h = negative v divided by u.

  Here, h' is the height of the image and h is the height of the object. The ratio −v/u gives you the same result using the distances.

  Now let us decode what the value of m tells you — and this is the most useful part.

  Rule 1: The SIGN of m tells you whether the image is real or virtual.
  — If m is NEGATIVE: the image is REAL and INVERTED. Why? Because a real image forms below the principal axis when the object is above it — so the image height h' is negative while the object height h is positive. Negative divided by positive = negative m.
  — If m is POSITIVE: the image is VIRTUAL and ERECT. The image is on the same side as the object, upright. Positive h' over positive h = positive m.

  Rule 2: The MAGNITUDE (absolute value) of m tells you whether the image is larger or smaller.
  — If |m| > 1 (for example, m = 2 or m = −3): the image is ENLARGED — it is bigger than the object.
  — If |m| < 1 (for example, m = 0.5 or m = −0.7): the image is DIMINISHED — it is smaller than the object.
  — If |m| = 1 (for example, m = −1): the image is the SAME SIZE as the object.

  So a magnification of m = −2 tells you instantly: real, inverted (negative sign), and twice as large as the object (magnitude = 2).

  And m = +0.5 tells you: virtual, erect (positive sign), and half the size of the object (magnitude = 0.5).

  For mirrors: m = −v/u. For lenses: m = v/u (positive, no negative sign). Be careful with this difference.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - m = h'/h = −v/u (for mirrors); m = v/u (for lenses).
  - Negative m → real and inverted image.
  - Positive m → virtual and erect image.
  - |m| > 1 → enlarged; |m| < 1 → diminished; |m| = 1 → same size.
  - A single value of m tells you the nature, orientation, AND relative size of the image.

BOARD TIP: Numericals often ask "find m and state the nature of the image." Always interpret the sign AND magnitude — both are expected in your answer.

SLIDE 20: Laws of Refraction (Snell's Law)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Two laws of refraction:
    Law 1: Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal all lie in the same plane
    Law 2: Snell's Law — sin i / sin r = n₂₁ (constant for two given media)
  - Bending direction: denser medium → bends toward normal; rarer medium → bends away

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Just as reflection has two laws, refraction also has two laws. These govern how light bends when it moves from one medium to another.

  Law 1: The incident ray, the refracted ray, and the normal at the point of incidence all lie in the same plane. This is exactly analogous to the first law of reflection — the geometry is always flat and two-dimensional. We can represent it fully in a 2D ray diagram.

  Law 2: This is Snell's Law — the mathematical heart of refraction. The ratio of the sine of the angle of incidence (∠i) to the sine of the angle of refraction (∠r) is a constant for any two given media. This constant is written as n₂₁ and is called the relative refractive index of medium 2 with respect to medium 1.

  sin i / sin r = n₂₁ = a constant

  This constant depends only on the two media involved — not on the angle of incidence. Whether you shine light at 20° or 60°, the ratio sin i / sin r stays the same for those two media. This is a law of nature.

  Now, the direction of bending: this is something you must get right in diagrams.
  — When light goes from a rarer medium (like air) to a denser medium (like glass or water), it slows down and bends TOWARD the normal. The angle of refraction is smaller than the angle of incidence.
  — When light goes from a denser medium to a rarer medium (glass to air), it speeds up and bends AWAY from the normal. The angle of refraction is larger than the angle of incidence.

  A useful analogy: imagine a vehicle moving from a smooth road (fast) onto soft sand (slow). The side that hits the sand first slows down, causing the vehicle to turn toward the sand — toward the denser medium. Light behaves the same way.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Law 1: Incident ray, refracted ray, and normal are coplanar.
  - Law 2 (Snell's Law): sin i / sin r = n₂₁ = constant for two given media.
  - Denser medium → light bends toward normal (∠r < ∠i).
  - Rarer medium → light bends away from normal (∠r > ∠i).
  - The constant n₂₁ is the refractive index of medium 2 with respect to medium 1.

BOARD TIP: When drawing a refraction diagram, clearly show the normal and mark ∠i and ∠r from the normal — not from the surface. State which way the ray bends and why.

SLIDE 21: Refractive Index in Detail
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Formula: n = c / v (speed of light in vacuum / speed in medium)
  - Three properties:
    Higher n → denser medium → light travels slower
    Diamond n = 2.42 is the densest common material
    No unit (ratio of two speeds)

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  The refractive index — ਅਪਵਰਤਨ ਅੰਕ — is a number that quantifies how optically dense a medium is, or equivalently, how much slower light travels in that medium compared to its speed in a vacuum.

  The formula is: n = c / v, where c is the speed of light in vacuum (approximately 3 × 10⁸ m/s) and v is the speed of light in the medium.

  Since light always travels slower in a medium than in a vacuum (it cannot go faster than c), n is always equal to or greater than 1 for any real medium. For vacuum itself, n = 1 exactly. For air, n ≈ 1.0003 — essentially 1. For water, n ≈ 1.33. For glass, n ≈ 1.5. For diamond, n = 2.42.

  What does n = 2.42 mean for diamond? It means light travels in diamond at c/2.42 — about 41% of its speed in vacuum. This is very slow. And because light slows down so dramatically in diamond, it bends sharply — giving diamond its remarkable sparkle and brilliance. Every time a ray of light hits a facet of a cut diamond, it bends dramatically, creating rainbow-like dispersion and internal reflections.

  Important: The refractive index has NO unit. It is a ratio of two speeds (metres per second / metres per second), so the units cancel. Always state "no unit" or "dimensionless" if asked.

  Higher refractive index = more optically dense = light travels slower = more bending. Lower refractive index = less dense = light faster = less bending.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - n = c/v (speed of light in vacuum ÷ speed in medium).
  - n ≥ 1 always; for vacuum n = 1; for water ≈ 1.33; for glass ≈ 1.5; for diamond = 2.42.
  - Higher n → light travels slower → bends more toward the normal.
  - Diamond (n = 2.42) is the most optically dense common material.
  - Refractive index is dimensionless — no unit.

BOARD TIP: "Why does diamond sparkle?" — because of its very high refractive index (2.42), causing extreme bending and total internal reflection. A classic application answer.

SLIDE 22: Power of a Lens
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Formula: P = 1/f (f in metres)
  - SI unit: dioptre (D); 1 D = 1 m⁻¹
  - Sign rules: Convex (converging) → P is positive; Concave (diverging) → P is negative
  - Shorter focal length → greater power

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Power of a lens — ਲੈਨਜ਼ ਦੀ ਸ਼ਕਤੀ — measures how strongly the lens bends light. The more a lens bends light, the greater its power.

  The formula is beautifully simple: P = 1/f, where f is the focal length measured in metres. The result P comes out in dioptres.

  Let us understand this physically. A lens with a very short focal length — say 10 cm, which is 0.1 m — bends parallel rays so much that they converge within just 10 cm. That requires a lot of bending. Power = 1/0.1 = 10 D. Now compare a lens with focal length 50 cm = 0.5 m. It bends light much more gently. Power = 1/0.5 = 2 D. The shorter the focal length, the greater the bending, the higher the power.

  Signs of power: A convex (converging) lens has a positive focal length, so its power is positive. A concave (diverging) lens has a negative focal length, so its power is negative.

  The unit, dioptre — ਡਾਇਓਪਟਰ — is equivalent to m⁻¹ (per metre). So 1 D = 1 m⁻¹. This is the same unit used on your prescription glasses! When the optician writes "+2.5 D" on your spectacle prescription, they mean a convex lens of focal length 1/2.5 = 0.4 m = 40 cm.

  When two lenses are placed in contact (combined), their total power is simply the sum: P_total = P₁ + P₂. This is much easier than combining focal lengths! This property of simple addition of powers was the reason the dioptre unit was invented.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - P = 1/f (f must be in metres); unit is dioptre (D).
  - Convex lens: positive power (+). Concave lens: negative power (−).
  - Shorter focal length = higher power (more bending).
  - Combined lenses in contact: P_total = P₁ + P₂.
  - Real-world link: spectacle lens prescriptions are in dioptres.

BOARD TIP: A numerical asking for the power of a combined lens system (e.g., +3 D and −1 D together) = +2 D total — this is a PSEB favourite, seen in 2021 and other years.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (MINUS sign — this is the correct version).
  - Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f (PLUS sign).
  - NEVER use a plus sign in the lens formula — it gives a completely wrong answer.
  - Always write the full formula before substituting values.

BOARD TIP: Error-spotting activities like this translate directly into exam questions like "A student wrote... What is the error?" — answer by identifying the wrong sign and writing the correct formula.

SLIDE 27: Memorize This — Chapter Recap
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Six bullet-point summary of the entire chapter:
    Reflection: ∠i = ∠r; mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f
    R = 2f; concave f is −; convex f is +
    Convex mirror & concave lens: always virtual, erect, diminished
    Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f (note the minus!)
    m = h'/h = −v/u (mirror) = v/u (lens)
    P = 1/f; unit dioptre (D); convex +, concave −

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  This is your chapter summary — the six non-negotiable facts you must carry into the exam hall. Let me read each one aloud and add the key point.

  One: Reflection: ∠i = ∠r. Mirror formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f. PLUS sign. Applicable to concave and convex mirrors.

  Two: R = 2f. This relation connects radius of curvature to focal length. A concave mirror has f negative (−). A convex mirror has f positive (+). This affects the signs in every numerical.

  Three: Convex mirror and concave lens always form virtual, erect, diminished images — no exceptions. Both are diverging devices. Use them as rear-view mirrors and in spectacles for short-sightedness respectively.

  Four: Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f. MINUS sign. The single most important sign in the chapter. Never mix it up with the mirror formula.

  Five: Magnification m = h'/h = −v/u for mirrors, and m = v/u for lenses. Negative m = real and inverted. Positive m = virtual and erect. |m| > 1 = enlarged. |m| < 1 = diminished.

  Six: Power P = 1/f (f in metres); unit is dioptre (D). Convex lens: positive power. Concave lens: negative power. Combined lens power = P₁ + P₂.

  Write these six points in your revision notebook and read them aloud every day until you can reproduce them without looking. That is what it means to "memorize this."

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - These six points collectively cover 80% of all exam questions in this chapter.
  - The three sign traps: concave f is negative; mirror has + and lens has −; m negative = real.
  - R = 2f is used in nearly every mirror numerical.
  - m formula is different for mirrors and lenses — note both.
  - Power is always in dioptres when f is in metres.

BOARD TIP: Keep this slide as your last-minute revision. Read these six points the morning of the exam — they are all you need for theory questions.

  - Object at C → image at C, same size (standard reference case).

SLIDE 29: Numerical Practice — Mirrors & Lenses
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Four numericals with check-answer functionality:
    Q1: Object 15 cm from concave mirror (f = 10 cm) — find v
    Q2: Same mirror (u = −15 cm, v = −30 cm) — find m and state nature
    Q3: Object 30 cm from convex lens (f = 20 cm) — find v
    Q4: Focal length 25 cm — find power P

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Now we practise four numerical problems — two on mirrors and two on lenses. Solve each one on paper first, then check your answer. These four problems together cover all the key formula applications in the chapter.

  Numerical 1: Object 15 cm in front of a concave mirror of focal length 10 cm. Find v.
  Data: u = −15 cm, f = −10 cm (concave, so negative).
  Formula: 1/v + 1/u = 1/f → 1/v = 1/f − 1/u = 1/(−10) − 1/(−15) = −1/10 + 1/15.
  LCM of 10 and 15 is 30: −3/30 + 2/30 = −1/30.
  So v = −30 cm. The image is 30 cm in front of the mirror — real and inverted.

  Numerical 2: Same mirror, u = −15 cm, v = −30 cm. Find m.
  m = −v/u = −(−30)/(−15) = −30/15 × (−1/−1) — wait, let me be careful.
  m = −v/u = −(−30) / (−15) = 30 / (−15) = −2.
  m = −2. Magnitude 2 means the image is twice as large (enlarged). The negative sign confirms real and inverted.

  Numerical 3: Object 30 cm from a convex lens of focal length 20 cm. Find v.
  Data: u = −30 cm (object in front, so negative), f = +20 cm (convex lens).
  Lens formula: 1/v − 1/u = 1/f → 1/v = 1/f + 1/u = 1/20 + 1/(−30) = 1/20 − 1/30.
  LCM of 20 and 30 is 60: 3/60 − 2/60 = 1/60.
  So v = +60 cm. Positive v means the image is on the other side of the lens — real and inverted. It is 60 cm from the lens on the far side.

  Numerical 4: Focal length = 25 cm = 0.25 m. Find power.
  P = 1/f = 1/0.25 = +4 D.
  Power is positive because the focal length is positive (convex lens assumed). Power = +4 dioptres.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Always assign signs before substituting: u = negative; concave f = negative; convex f = positive.
  - Mirror formula has + sign; lens formula has − sign (but rearranges to + in Q3).
  - v negative = in front of mirror (real); v positive (for lens) = on far side (real).
  - Convert focal length to metres before calculating power.
  - Always state the complete nature of the image as your conclusion.

BOARD TIP: These four problem types cover every formula in the chapter. Master all four, and you will handle any numerical in the board exam with confidence.

SLIDE 30: True or False? (Interactive Quiz)
========================================================================

ON SCREEN:
  - Eight True/False questions with Punjabi translations:
    Q1: Ray through C reflects back along the same path → TRUE
    Q2: Focal length of a concave lens is negative → TRUE
    Q3: Ray through optical centre undergoes lateral displacement → FALSE
    Q4: Plane mirror forms a laterally inverted image → TRUE
    Q5: Negative magnification means real and inverted → TRUE
    Q6: Focal length of spherical mirror = half its radius → TRUE
    Q7: Convex lens is called a diverging lens → FALSE
    Q8: Power of a lens is measured in dioptres → TRUE

SCRIPT (say this aloud):
  Eight True or False questions — attempt each one before looking at the answer. These statements are designed to target common misconceptions.

  Q1: A ray through C reflects back along the same path. TRUE. A ray along the radius hits the mirror perpendicularly (∠i = 0°) and retraces its path. This is mirror Rule 3.

  Q2: Focal length of a concave lens is negative. TRUE. A concave lens is a diverging lens. Its focus is on the same side as the object, which is negative by the sign convention.

  Q3: A ray through the optical centre undergoes lateral displacement. FALSE — and this is very important. A ray through the optical centre goes completely straight, with absolutely NO bending or displacement. This is the key exception of lens Rule 3.

  Q4: A plane mirror forms a laterally inverted image. TRUE. Left and right are swapped — the AMBULANCE example.

  Q5: Negative magnification means the image is real and inverted. TRUE. m < 0 → real and inverted. This is the sign rule for magnification.

  Q6: The focal length of a spherical mirror is half its radius of curvature. TRUE. f = R/2, or R = 2f.

  Q7: A convex lens is called a diverging lens. FALSE. A convex lens is a CONVERGING lens — it brings parallel rays to a focus. A concave lens is the diverging lens.

  Q8: The power of a lens is measured in dioptres. TRUE. P = 1/f (f in metres); unit = dioptre (D).

  How many did you get right? If you missed Q3 or Q7, go back and revise those points — they are the trickiest.

KEY POINTS TO STRESS:
  - Ray through C → reflects back on itself (TRUE — ∠i = 0°).
  - Ray through optical centre → NO displacement, goes straight (Q3 is FALSE).
  - Convex lens = converging (NOT diverging) — Q7 is FALSE.
  - f of concave lens is negative; f of convex lens is positive.
  - Negative m = real and inverted — reliable rule, no exceptions.

BOARD TIP: Q3 and Q7 are classic trap questions. Examiners use "Does a ray through the optical centre bend?" and "Is a convex lens diverging?" specifically to catch unprepared students.
