Reading: The River of Charge

Voltage, Current and Resistance
Electricity flowing through a wire is a lot like water flowing through a river. The push that drives the charges forward is the voltage, the amount of charge flowing past each second is the current, and anything that slows the flow down is called resistance. A German teacher named Georg Ohm discovered that in many materials the current is simply proportional to the voltage, a neat relationship we still call Ohm's law today. When charges are forced through a high resistance, they collide and release energy as heat, which is exactly how the filament in a bulb or the coil in a heater glows. Understanding these simple rules lets engineers design everything from a tiny phone charger to the power grid that lights up a whole city.

Electric Current & Charge

Electric current (I) is the rate of flow of electric charge through a conductor.

I = Q / t
  • Q = charge (coulomb, C); t = time (seconds)
  • SI unit of current is the ampere (A); 1 A = 1 C/s.
  • Conventional current flows from + to − (opposite to electron flow).
  • Measured with an ammeter, connected in series.

Potential Difference (Voltage)

The potential difference (V) between two points is the work done to move a unit charge from one point to the other.

V = W / Q
  • SI unit is the volt (V); 1 V = 1 J/C.
  • Measured with a voltmeter, connected in parallel.
  • Voltage is the "push" that drives the current.

Ohm's Law & the V-I Graph

V = I × R

Ohm's law produces a linear graph when Voltage (V) is plotted against Current (I).

  • The Slope Formula: In math, slope = $Y / X$. Therefore, the slope of a V-I graph is $V / I$.
  • Conclusion: The slope of a V-I graph directly gives you the Resistance (R) of the conductor.
  • Exam Trick: A steeper line (higher slope) means the wire has a higher resistance.

Ohm's Law: Statement 🏆 Definition

Ohm's Law: At constant temperature, the current (I) through a conductor is directly proportional to the potential difference (V) across its ends.

V ∝ I  ⇒  V = IR
Condition: Temperature and physical state must stay constant. Conductors that obey Ohm's law (e.g. metals) are ohmic; those that don't (e.g. a diode or filament bulb) are non-ohmic.

Numerical: Using Ohm's Law 🏆 Step-by-Step

A 12 V battery drives a current of 3 A through a resistor. Find its resistance R.

1. List the data: V = 12 V, I = 3 A, R = ?
2. Choose the formula: Ohm's law → V = I × R
3. Rearrange: R = V ÷ I
4. Substitute: R = 12 ÷ 3
5. Answer: R = 4 Ω (ohm)

Factors Affecting Resistance

R = ρL / A
  • Length (L): Resistance is directly proportional. A longer wire = more resistance.
  • Area (A): Resistance is inversely proportional. A thicker wire = less resistance.
  • Material (ρ): Resistivity depends only on the material and temperature, NOT length.

Resistivity of Materials

Resistivity (ρ) is a property of the material only (unit: Ω·m). Lower ρ = better conductor.

TypeExamplesResistivity
ConductorsSilver, Copper, AluminiumVery low
AlloysNichrome, Constantan, ManganinHigh (used in heaters)
InsulatorsRubber, Glass, WoodVery high
Why alloys for heaters? High resistivity + high melting point + they do not oxidise (burn) easily at high temperature.

Series vs. Parallel Circuits

Series (ਲੜੀਬੱਧ)Parallel (ਸਮਾਨਾਂਤਰ)
Total $R = R_1 + R_2 + \dots$$1/R = 1/R_1 + 1/R_2 + \dots$
Current (I) is the same everywhere.Voltage (V) is the same everywhere.

Why are Homes wired in Parallel?

1. The "All or Nothing" Flaw: In series, if one appliance breaks, the whole house shuts off.
2. Shared Voltage: In series, voltage divides. In parallel, every appliance gets the full 220V it needs.

Resistors in Series

The same current I flows through every resistor; the total voltage is shared.

Rs = R1 + R2 + R3
  • Total resistance is larger than the largest single resistor.
  • Current is the same everywhere; voltage divides across each resistor.

Resistors in Parallel

The same voltage V is across every resistor; the total current is shared.

1/Rp = 1/R1 + 1/R2 + 1/R3
  • Total resistance is smaller than the smallest single resistor.
  • Voltage is the same everywhere; current divides between branches.

Joule's Law: Material Selection

When current passes through a high-resistance wire, electrical energy converts to heat ($H = I^2Rt$).

  1. Why Tungsten for bulbs? It is a very strong metal with an incredibly high melting point (approx. $3380^\circ C$). It can get white-hot and emit light without melting.
  2. Why filled with gas? If a tungsten filament got that hot in normal air, oxygen would instantly burn (oxidize) it, snapping the wire. Bulbs are filled with chemically inactive gases like Nitrogen or Argon to prolong filament life.

Electric Power

Power is the rate at which electrical energy is used up in a circuit.

P = VI = I2R = V2/R

SI unit is the watt (W); 1 W = 1 J/s. 1 kilowatt (kW) = 1000 W.

Target: P = VI

Electrical Energy & the Commercial Unit

E = P × t

The commercial unit of electrical energy is the kilowatt-hour (kWh), also called "1 unit".

1 kWh = 3.6 × 106 J
Example: A 1000 W (1 kW) heater used for 2 hours consumes 1 × 2 = 2 kWh = 2 units.

Heating Effect: Applications

DeviceHow it uses the heating effect
Electric bulbTungsten filament glows white-hot; filled with argon/nitrogen.
Heater / IronNichrome coil (high resistance) produces heat.
FuseThin, low-melting wire melts if the current is too high — a safety device.
Fuse rule: Always connected in the live wire and in series with the circuit.

Memorize This: Chapter Recap

Numerical: Equivalent Resistance 🏆 Step-by-Step

Interactive Sequencer: Find total resistance when R₁=2Ω and R₂=4Ω are in series, and this pair is in parallel with R₃=3Ω. (ਕੁੱਲ ਪ੍ਰਤੀਰੋਧ ਲੱਭੋ।)

1. Series part: R₁ and R₂ are end-to-end, so add them: Rs = 2 + 4 = 6 Ω.
2. Now a parallel pair: The 6 Ω group is in parallel with R₃ = 3 Ω.
3. Parallel formula: 1/Rp = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6.
4. Flip the fraction: Rp = 6/3 = 2 Ω.
Answer: Equivalent resistance = 2 Ω. Notice parallel resistance is always LESS than the smallest resistor!
⚠️ Board Trap Never add resistances directly when they are in parallel. Always use 1/Rp = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ first, then flip the fraction.
