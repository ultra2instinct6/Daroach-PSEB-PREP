Reading: The Web of Life

Energy Flow and Our Environment
Nothing in nature ever truly lives alone. A blade of grass captures sunlight, a grasshopper eats the grass, a frog eats the grasshopper, and a snake eats the frog, forming a chain in which energy passes from one living thing to the next. At each step, most of the energy is lost as heat, which is why there are always fewer snakes than there are blades of grass. When we dump plastic and other non-biodegradable waste into this system, it does not rot away but lingers for centuries, poisoning the creatures that swallow it. High above us, a fragile layer of ozone quietly shields all of this life from the Sun's harmful ultraviolet rays. Protecting these invisible connections is not just kindness to nature; it is how we protect ourselves.

Components of an Ecosystem 🏆 Definition

An ecosystem is a unit where living things interact with their non-living surroundings. It has two parts. Tap each card to reveal the meaning.

Biotic Components
(ਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼)
All living organisms — plants, animals, bacteria and fungi, grouped as producers, consumers and decomposers.
Abiotic Components
(ਅਜੈਵਿਕ ਅੰਸ਼)
All non-living factors — sunlight, temperature, air, water, soil and minerals. They decide which organisms can survive.

Producers, Consumers & Decomposers

Every organism has a trophic role based on how it gets food.

ਖਪਤਕਾਰ		Cannot make food; eat plants or animals — heterotrophs.Deer, frog, lion, humans

Food Chains (ਭੋਜਨ ਲੜੀ)

A food chain is a single pathway showing who eats whom. Energy flows in one direction only — from the sun to producers to consumers.

Grass → Grasshopper → Frog → Snake → Eagle

Each stage is a trophic level: producers are the 1st, herbivores the 2nd, and so on.

Food Webs 🏆 Frequently Asked

In nature animals eat more than one type of food. When many food chains interconnect, they form a food web.

Why is a food web more stable? If one species disappears, animals have alternative food sources, so the whole system does not collapse.

Trophic Levels & Ecological Pyramids

The number, biomass or energy at each feeding step can be drawn as an ecological pyramid. The base (producers) is always the largest.

Chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels because energy is lost at every step (the 10% Law).

Energy Flow: The 10% Law

Ecosystems rely on a unidirectional flow of energy. Plants (Producers) capture about 1% of the sun's energy to make food.

The 10% Law (10% ਦਾ ਨਿਯਮ)

Only 10% of energy is transferred from one trophic level to the next. The other 90% is lost as heat. Because so much energy is lost, food chains rarely go beyond 3 or 4 steps!

Biological Magnification 🏆 Process Flow

Interactive Sequencer: Build the DDT toxin accumulation chain. Notice how the concentration increases at every step.

1. Water Source: Farmers spray pesticides (DDT). It washes into a local pond at a very low, safe concentration.
2. Producers (Plankton): Microscopic plants absorb the DDT. They cannot excrete it.
3. Primary Consumers (Small Fish): Eat hundreds of plankton. The DDT accumulates heavily in their fat.
4. Secondary Consumers (Large Fish): Eat dozens of small fish. Concentration spikes dramatically.
5. Top Carnivores (Humans/Birds): We eat the large fish and receive the maximum toxic dose, leading to severe diseases.

Ozone Formation & Depletion

Ozone (O₃) forms a protective shield in the upper atmosphere against UV radiation.

1.   O2   → (UV Light) →   O + O

2.   O + O2   →   O3 (Ozone)

It is destroyed by CFCs (Chlorofluorocarbons) used in old refrigerators, causing the "Ozone Hole".

How CFCs Destroy Ozone 🏆 Process Flow

Interactive Sequencer: Follow how one CFC molecule can destroy thousands of ozone molecules.

1. Release: CFCs from old refrigerators, ACs and sprays drift up into the stratosphere.
2. Breakdown: Strong UV rays break the CFC, releasing a free chlorine (Cl) atom.
3. Attack: The Cl atom reacts with ozone (O₃), stealing an oxygen → ClO + O₂.
4. Chain Reaction: The Cl atom is freed again and destroys thousands more ozone molecules.
5. Result: The ozone layer thins, forming the "Ozone Hole" — more UV reaches Earth.

Effects of Ozone Depletion

A thinner ozone layer lets harmful UV rays reach the surface, which can cause:

  • 🧑 Skin cancer and skin ageing in humans
  • 👁️ Cataracts (damage to the eyes)
  • 🛡️ Weakened immune system
  • 🌾 Reduced crop yield and harm to phytoplankton
Solution: The Montreal Protocol (1987) banned CFC production worldwide to protect the ozone layer.

Reading: Our Responsibility

Human Impact on the Environment
Human activities have disturbed the delicate balance of nature. We cut forests, burn fossil fuels, and dump non-biodegradable plastics into rivers and oceans. Harmful chemicals move up food chains and concentrate in the bodies of top consumers, including us. The gases we release have even damaged the ozone layer that protects all life from the Sun's ultraviolet rays. But there is hope: by reducing waste, recycling materials, planting trees, and using eco-friendly products, every person can help repair these broken connections. Protecting the environment is not a choice — it is our shared responsibility.

  • Food chain = single line; food web = many chains (more stable).

  • Ozone (O₃) blocks UV; destroyed by CFCs. Montreal Protocol banned CFCs.

The 10% Law: Energy Flow 🏆 Numerical Favourite

Interactive Sequencer: If grass (producers) captures 10,000 J of the Sun's energy, trace how much reaches each level. (ਹਰ ਪੱਧਰ 'ਤੇ ਊਰਜਾ ਵੇਖੋ।)

1. Producers (Grass): Store 10,000 J from the Sun (1st trophic level).
2. Primary Consumers (Deer): Only 10% passes on → 1,000 J. The other 90% is lost as heat & life processes.
3. Secondary Consumers (Snake): 10% of 1,000 → 100 J.
4. Tertiary Consumers (Hawk): 10% of 100 → 10 J only!
Conclusion: Energy shrinks so fast that food chains rarely have more than 4–5 trophic levels.
Lindeman's 10% Law: only 10% of energy moves to the next trophic level; 90% is lost.
