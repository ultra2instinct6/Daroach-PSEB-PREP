Early Classification: Dobereiner's Triads

When elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic masses, groups of three elements (triads) with similar properties are found.

Rule: The atomic mass of the middle element is roughly the average of the atomic masses of the other two elements.

Example: The Alkali Metal Triad

  • Lithium (Li) = 7
  • Sodium (Na) = 23
  • Potassium (K) = 39

Average of Li and K = (7 + 39) / 2 = 23 (which is Sodium!)

🚨 Limitation

He could only identify three triads from the elements known at that time.

Newlands' Law of Octaves

He arranged the known elements in order of increasing atomic mass. He found that the properties of every eighth element were similar to that of the first (like musical notes).

🚨 Limitations

  1. It was applicable only up to Calcium. After that, the 8th element did not match.
  2. He assumed only 56 elements existed in nature and no more would be found.
  3. He placed dissimilar elements (like Cobalt and Nickel) in the same slot to fit the table.

Mendeleev's Periodic Table

Mendeleev arranged elements by Atomic Mass and similarity of chemical properties. He specifically looked at how elements reacted with Oxygen and Hydrogen (forming Oxides and Hydrides).

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

Mendeleev's Bold Gamble

Dmitri Mendeleev is remembered as a genius not just for what he put into his periodic table, but for what he left out. While arranging the known elements by their atomic masses, Mendeleev realized that the pattern occasionally broke. Instead of forcing elements into the wrong groups, he boldly left empty gaps in his table. He confidently predicted that these gaps belonged to elements that simply had not been discovered yet. For instance, he left a space under Aluminum and temporarily named the missing element Eka-Aluminum. Years later, scientists discovered the metal Gallium, and its physical and chemical properties matched Mendeleev's predictions almost perfectly, proving the profound power of his periodic classification.

Mendeleev's Anomalies (ਖਾਮੀਆਂ)

Mendeleev's table was great, but it had a few unsolvable problems:

  • Position of Hydrogen: It resembles both Alkali metals (Group 1) and Halogens (Group 17).
  • Position of Isotopes: Isotopes have the same chemical properties but different masses. They would need separate slots in a mass-based table, ruining the layout.
  • Wrong Mass Order: Sometimes he had to put slightly heavier elements before lighter ones (e.g., Cobalt before Nickel) to match chemical properties.

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

Moseley's X-Ray Revolution

In 1913, a brilliant 25-year-old physicist named Henry Moseley conducted a ground-breaking experiment. He fired a stream of electrons at various metal targets and measured the X-rays they emitted. He discovered that the frequency of the X-rays was mathematically tied to the amount of positive charge inside the atom's nucleus—a value we now call the Atomic Number. This proved that elements should be sorted by their atomic number, not their atomic mass! Moseley's genius instantly solved the flaws in Mendeleev's table. Tragically, just two years later, Moseley was killed in World War I, but his atomic number legacy forever shaped the modern periodic table.

Solving Mendeleev's Problems

The Modern Periodic Table (developed by Henry Moseley) solved Mendeleev's anomalies:

⚖️ Connecting the Law

The Modern Periodic Law states that properties depend on Atomic Number, not Mass. This instantly solved the Isotope problem because all isotopes of an element have the exact same atomic number, meaning they only take up one slot! It also fixed the Cobalt/Nickel order.

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

The Zig-Zag Line

If you look at the right side of the Modern Periodic Table, you will notice a strange, staircase-like line drawn between the elements. This zig-zag line acts as the border wall between two warring nations: the Metals on the left and the Non-Metals on the right. But the elements sitting exactly on this border, like Silicon, Germanium, and Arsenic, refuse to pick a side. They are called Metalloids. They look shiny like metals but are brittle like non-metals. Most importantly, they do not conduct electricity well enough to be metals, but they aren't insulators either. They are 'semiconductors'. This single property allows us to control the flow of electricity precisely, making metalloids the fundamental building blocks of all computer chips, smartphones, and modern technology.

Modern Periodic Table Organization

Based on Atomic Number (ਪਰਮਾਣੂ ਸੰਖਿਆ).

  • Groups (↓): 18 vertical columns. Elements in a group have the same number of valence electrons, hence similar chemical properties.
  • Periods (→): 7 horizontal rows. Elements in a period have the same number of electron shells.
  • The Zig-Zag Line: Separates Metals (left side) from Non-metals (right side). The elements on the line are Metalloids (e.g., Silicon, Boron, Germanium).

Valency vs. Valence Electrons (ਸੰਜੋਜਕਤਾ ਬਨਾਮ ਬਾਹਰੀ ਇਲੈਕਟ੍ਰਾਨ)

Do not confuse these two critical concepts!

Valence Electrons (ਬਾਹਰੀ ਇਲੈਕਟ੍ਰਾਨ) Valency (ਸੰਜੋਜਕਤਾ)
The total number of electrons in the outermost shell. The combining capacity (how many electrons needed to reach 8, or how many to lose).
Trend Across a Period: Constantly increases from 1 to 8. Trend Across a Period: First increases from 1 to 4, then decreases to 0.

Periodicity Vector Map (ਆਵਰਤੀ ਰੁਝਾਨ)

→ Across a Period →
  • Atomic Size Decreases (Increased nuclear charge pulls electrons closer).
  • Metallic Character Decreases (Tendency to lose electrons decreases).
↓ Down a Group ↓
  • Atomic Size Increases (New electron shells are added).
  • Metallic Character Increases (Electrons are further from nucleus, easier to lose).
