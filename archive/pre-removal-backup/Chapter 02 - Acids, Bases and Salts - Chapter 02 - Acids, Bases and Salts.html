📋 The Alkali Rule

"All alkalis are bases, but not all bases are alkalis!" Only bases that dissolve completely in water are given the special name 'Alkali' (like NaOH).

Acids vs. Bases (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਅਤੇ ਖਾਰ)

Acids (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ) Bases (ਖਾਰ)
Taste sour (ਖੱਟੇ) Taste bitter (ਕੌੜੇ)
Turn blue litmus to RED Turn red litmus to BLUE
Give H⁺ ions in water Give OH⁻ ions in water

🚨 Dry HCl Exception

Acids ONLY show acidic nature in water. Dry HCl gas does not change dry blue litmus paper because it has no water to produce the required H⁺ ions!

Strong vs. Weak (ਸ਼ਕਤੀਸ਼ਾਲੀ ਅਤੇ ਕਮਜ਼ੋਰ)

The strength of an acid or base depends on how many ions it produces in water.

Strong Acid (ਸ਼ਕਤੀਸ਼ਾਲੀ ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ) Weak Acid (ਕਮਜ਼ੋਰ ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ)
Completely ionizes in water to give many H⁺ ions. Partially ionizes in water to give fewer H⁺ ions.
Example: HCl, H₂SO₄ Example: Acetic Acid (CH₃COOH)

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

The Magic of Litmus

Long before digital pH meters existed, ancient chemists relied on nature to reveal the invisible world of acids and bases. They discovered that certain natural dyes changed color depending on the environment. The most famous of these is litmus, a purple dye extracted from lichens—a slow-growing plant-like organism found on rocks and trees. When placed in an acidic solution, this purple dye instantly flushes to a bright red. When dropped into a basic solution, it turns a deep, vibrant blue. This simple biological extract gave early scientists the power to 'see' the chemical nature of a liquid simply by watching its color shift.

Indicators & pH (ਸੂਚਕ ਅਤੇ pH)

Indicators test if a solution is acid or base.

  • Litmus: Red in acid, Blue in base.
  • Phenolphthalein: Colorless in acid, Pink in base.
  • Olfactory Indicators (ਸੁੰਘਣ ਵਾਲੇ ਸੂਚਕ): Odor changes (e.g., Onion, Clove lose smell in bases).

The pH Scale

  • Acidic: pH < 7 (Low pH)
  • Neutral: pH = 7 (Pure water)
  • Basic: pH > 7 (High pH)

pH in Digestion & Soil (ਪਾਚਨ ਅਤੇ ਮਿੱਟੀ ਵਿੱਚ pH)

  • Digestive System (ਪਾਚਨ ਪ੍ਰਣਾਲੀ): Our stomach produces Hydrochloric Acid (HCl) to digest food (pH ~1.2). If it produces too much, it causes pain (acidity). We use antacids to neutralize it.
  • Soil pH (ਮਿੱਟੀ ਦਾ pH): Plants grow best in neutral soil (pH close to 7). If soil is too acidic (common with fertilizer overuse), farmers add Quicklime (CaO) to neutralize it.

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

The Acid Rain Crisis

When fossil fuels like coal and petroleum are burned in factories and vehicles, they release large amounts of sulfur dioxide and nitrogen oxides into the atmosphere. These toxic gases float high into the sky and mix with atmospheric moisture. A chemical reaction occurs, transforming the water droplets into dilute sulfuric and nitric acids. When this moisture falls back to Earth, it is called acid rain. Normal rain is slightly acidic with a pH around 5.6, but acid rain drops significantly lower. As this highly acidic water flows into lakes and rivers, it lowers the pH of the entire body of water, making it nearly impossible for sensitive aquatic life, like fish and frogs, to survive.

Acid Reactions (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ ਦੀਆਂ ਕਿਰਿਆਵਾਂ)

  • Acid + Metal → Salt + Hydrogen Gas
    (ਤੇਜ਼ਾਬ + ਧਾਤ → ਲੂਣ + ਹਾਈਡ੍ਰੋਜਨ ਗੈਸ)
  • Acid + Base → Salt + Water
    (Neutralization / ਉਦਾਸੀਨੀਕਰਨ ਕਿਰਿਆ)
  • Acid + Carbonate → Salt + Water + CO₂

Reading Comprehension Story Mode

The Baking Soda Volcano

If you have ever mixed vinegar and baking soda together to make a fake science-fair volcano, you have witnessed an acid-carbonate reaction firsthand. Vinegar contains ethanoic acid, and baking soda is sodium hydrogencarbonate. When they meet, the acid breaks apart the carbonate molecule, instantly releasing water and a massive surge of carbon dioxide gas. This sudden release of gas causes the bubbling eruption. Bakers use this exact same chemical principle to make cakes. By mixing baking soda with a mild edible acid, carbon dioxide bubbles get trapped in the wet dough. When the cake bakes, the gas expands, forcing the dough to rise and become light and fluffy!

Acids with Carbonates (ਕਾਰਬੋਨੇਟਾਂ ਨਾਲ ਕਿਰਿਆ)

When an acid reacts with a metal carbonate or metal hydrogencarbonate, it produces salt, water, and Carbon Dioxide gas.

Na₂CO₃(s) + 2HCl(aq) → 2NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)
NaHCO₃(s) + HCl(aq) → NaCl(aq) + H₂O(l) + CO₂(g)

Metallic vs Non-Metallic Oxides

Metallic Oxides Non-Metallic Oxides
React with acids to form salt & water. React with bases to form salt & water.
Therefore, they are Basic in nature. Therefore, they are Acidic in nature.
Example: $CuO, MgO$ Example: $CO_2, SO_2$

Lab Guide: Identifying Gases (ਗੈਸਾਂ ਦੀ ਪਛਾਣ)

When acids react, gases are released. How do we test which gas it is?

🧪 The Pop Test (Hydrogen Gas)

Bring a burning matchstick near the test tube. If the gas burns with a 'pop' sound, it is Hydrogen ($H_2$).

🧪 The Milky Test (Carbon Dioxide)

Pass the gas through limewater (Calcium hydroxide). If the limewater turns milky/cloudy, it is Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$). If you pass excess $CO_2$, it turns clear again!

Safety: The Dilution Process (ਪਤਲਾ ਕਰਨਾ)

🚨 Rule: Add Acid to Water!

ALWAYS add acid to water slowly while stirring. NEVER add water to acid. Why? It is highly exothermic and the heat generated can cause the mixture to splash out and cause severe burns, or break the glass container.

What happens internally? Dilution decreases the concentration of ions ($H_3O^+$/$OH^-$) per unit volume.

Top "Give Reason" Questions PSEB 2017, 2019, 2023

  • Why should curd not be kept in brass or copper vessels?
    Curd contains acid (Lactic acid). It reacts with metal to form poisonous salts.
  • Why do acids not show acidic behavior without water?
    Acids only release H⁺ ions when dissolved in water.
  • Why does an aqueous solution of an acid conduct electricity?
    Because acids ionize in water to produce charged ions (H⁺) which carry the current.

The Chlor-Alkali Process PSEB 2020, 2022

When electricity is passed through an aqueous solution of sodium chloride (called brine), it decomposes to form sodium hydroxide.

2NaCl(aq) + 2H₂O(l) → 2NaOH(aq) + Cl₂(g) + H₂(g)
  • Chlorine gas (Anode): Used for water treatment and swimming pools.
  • Hydrogen gas (Cathode): Used for fuel and making margarine.
  • Sodium hydroxide (near Cathode): Used for degreasing metals and making soaps.

Manufacturing Salts: Bleaching Powder & Baking Soda

1. Bleaching Powder ($CaOCl_2$): Produced by the action of chlorine on dry slaked lime.

Ca(OH)₂ + Cl₂ → CaOCl₂ + H₂O

Uses: Disinfecting drinking water, bleaching cotton.

2. Baking Soda ($NaHCO_3$): Produced using sodium chloride, water, $CO_2$, and ammonia.

NaCl + H₂O + CO₂ + NH₃ → NH₄Cl + NaHCO₃

Uses: Making baking powder (which makes cakes fluffy), antacid.

Manufacturing Salts: Washing Soda & Plaster of Paris

3. Washing Soda ($Na_2CO_3 \cdot 10H_2O$): Recrystallization of sodium carbonate gives washing soda.

Na₂CO₃ + 10H₂O → Na₂CO₃·10H₂O

Uses: Glass/soap/paper industries, removing permanent hardness of water.

4. Plaster of Paris ($CaSO_4 \cdot rac{1}{2}H_2O$): Heating Gypsum at exactly 373K.

CaSO₄·2H₂O + Heat(373K) → CaSO₄·½H₂O + 1½H₂O

Uses: Doctors use it to support fractured bones.

Water of Crystallization Lab

Water of crystallization is the fixed number of water molecules present in one formula unit of a salt.

🧪 Practical Lab Guide: Heating Copper Sulphate

Copper sulphate crystals contain 5 molecules of water ($CuSO_4 \cdot 5H_2O$). They appear blue. When heated, they lose this water and turn into a white powder.

If you moisten the white powder with water again, the blue color is completely restored!

NCERT Exemplar: Unknown Metal Test

Question:

Metal compound A reacts with dilute HCl to produce effervescence. The gas evolved extinguishes a burning candle. Identify the gas.

Model Answer:

The gas is Carbon Dioxide ($CO_2$). The effervescence and extinguishing of the candle are characteristic tests for $CO_2$.

NCERT Exemplar: Dry HCl

Question:

Why does dry HCl not change the color of dry litmus paper?

Model Answer:

Acids require water to dissociate and produce $H^+$ ions. Without moisture, no ions are present to change the indicator's color.

Chapter 2 Quick Recap

  • Acids taste sour and yield $H^+$; Bases taste bitter and yield $OH^-$.
  • Indicators like Litmus and Phenolphthalein show color changes.
  • pH scale measures $H^+$ concentration (0 to 14).
  • Salts like Baking Soda and Washing Soda have major industrial uses.
  • Water of crystallization gives crystals their shape and color.
