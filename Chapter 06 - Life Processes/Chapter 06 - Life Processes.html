What Are Life Processes?

All living organisms constantly maintain their body even when they appear to be resting. These maintenance functions are called life processes.

ProcessMain job
NutritionObtains raw material and energy
RespirationReleases usable energy from food
TransportationCarries food, gases, water and wastes
ExcretionRemoves harmful metabolic wastes
Life processes keep the internal environment stable. (ਸਰੀਰ ਨੂੰ ਸੰਤੁਲਿਤ ਰੱਖਦੇ ਹਨ।)

Nutrition: Autotrophic vs Heterotrophic

Autotrophic NutritionHeterotrophic Nutrition
Organism makes its own food from simple substances.Organism obtains food from other organisms.
Needs chlorophyll, sunlight, CO₂ and water.Needs ready-made organic food.
Green plants, algae, some bacteria.Animals, fungi, most bacteria.
Trap: Plants are autotrophs, but they still respire day and night.

Photosynthesis 5-Mark Core

Green plants convert light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.

6CO₂ + 6H₂O --sunlight/chlorophyll--> C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Raw materials: CO₂ from air, water from soil.
  • Site: chloroplasts in green leaves.
  • Product: glucose; oxygen is released.
Photosynthesis stores energy; respiration releases it.

How Stomata Work

Stomata are tiny pores on leaves controlled by guard cells.

ConditionGuard cellsStomaResult
Plenty of waterSwell / become turgidOpenCO₂ enters, O₂ exits
Water shortageShrink / become flaccidCloseWater loss reduces
Stomata balance gas exchange with water conservation. (ਗੈਸਾਂ ਦੀ ਅਦਲਾ-ਬਦਲੀ ਅਤੇ ਪਾਣੀ ਦੀ ਬਚਤ।)

Nutrition in Amoeba & Paramecium

Amoeba captures food using pseudopodia. Paramecium uses cilia to sweep food into a fixed oral groove.

1. Food is surrounded by pseudopodia.
2. A food vacuole forms.
3. Enzymes digest the food.
4. Nutrients are absorbed into cytoplasm.
5. Undigested waste is egested.

Human Respiratory System

Air passes through a branching pathway before gases exchange in the alveoli.

Nostrils → Nasal cavity → Trachea → Bronchi → Bronchioles → Alveoli
  • Rings of cartilage keep the trachea open.
  • Diaphragm movement changes chest volume.
  • Alveoli are surrounded by blood capillaries.

Exchange of Gases at Alveoli

Alveoli are efficient exchange surfaces because they have:

  • Millions of tiny sacs for huge surface area.
  • Thin, moist walls for fast diffusion.
  • Dense capillary network to maintain concentration gradient.
DirectionGas
Alveoli → BloodOxygen
Blood → AlveoliCarbon dioxide

Breathing vs Respiration

BreathingRespiration
Physical process of inhalation and exhalation.Biochemical process that releases energy from food.
Occurs in lungs.Occurs inside cells, mainly mitochondria.
No energy is produced directly.ATP/energy is produced.
Do not write: breathing = respiration. They are related but not the same.

The Human Heart

The heart is a four-chambered pump: two atria receive blood and two ventricles push blood out.

SideBlood handledMain route
Right sideDeoxygenatedBody → heart → lungs
Left sideOxygenatedLungs → heart → body
Valves prevent backflow and keep blood moving one way.

Double Circulation

1. Body sends deoxygenated blood to right atrium.
2. Right ventricle pumps it to lungs.
3. Lungs oxygenate the blood.
4. Left atrium receives oxygenated blood.
5. Left ventricle pumps it to the whole body.
Double circulation keeps oxygenated and deoxygenated blood separate.

Lymph

Lymph is a pale fluid formed from tissue fluid. It returns excess fluid to the blood and transports absorbed fats from the intestine.

  • Contains fewer proteins than blood plasma.
  • Contains lymphocytes that help immunity.
  • Flows slowly through lymph vessels.

Transport in Plants: Xylem & Phloem

XylemPhloem
Transports water and minerals.Transports food/sugars.
Mainly upward movement.Both upward and downward movement.
Dead conducting cells.Living conducting cells.
Driven by root pressure and transpiration pull.Requires energy (ATP).

Transpiration Pull

Water evaporates from leaf surfaces through stomata. This creates a suction force that pulls water upward through xylem from roots to leaves.

  • Helps upward movement of water.
  • Cools the plant.
  • Maintains mineral transport.
More transpiration = stronger upward pull, if soil water is available.

Translocation

Translocation is the movement of soluble products of photosynthesis through phloem.

  • Food moves from source (leaf) to sink (root, fruit, growing bud).
  • It can move in both directions.
  • It requires energy from ATP.

Excretion in Humans

Human excretory system removes nitrogenous waste, mainly urea.

PartFunction
KidneyFilters blood and forms urine.
UreterCarries urine to bladder.
Urinary bladderStores urine.
UrethraPasses urine out.

The Nephron

1. Filtration: blood pressure filters water and small solutes into Bowman's capsule.
2. Reabsorption: useful glucose, salts and most water return to blood.
3. Secretion: extra wastes are added to filtrate.
4. Collection: urine passes to collecting duct, ureter and bladder.

Dialysis: Artificial Kidney

Dialysis removes urea and excess salts from blood when kidneys fail.

  • Blood flows through a semi-permeable membrane.
  • Dialysis fluid has no urea, so urea diffuses out.
  • Useful substances are kept at normal concentration to prevent loss.
Key word: semi-permeable membrane.

Excretion in Plants

Plants do not have a specialised excretory system, but they remove or store wastes in several ways.

  • Oxygen from photosynthesis exits through stomata.
  • Excess water leaves by transpiration.
  • Wastes may be stored in leaves, bark, resins or gums.
  • Some wastes are released into surrounding soil.

Chapter 6 Recap

  • Photosynthesis stores energy in glucose.
  • Respiration releases energy from glucose.
  • Alveoli and villi have large surface area.
  • Double circulation separates oxygenated and deoxygenated blood.
  • Xylem carries water; phloem carries food.
  • Nephron filters blood and forms urine.
