Reading: Why the Sky is Blue

Scattering and the Human Eye
Every evening the sky puts on a free show, glowing orange and red as the Sun sinks toward the horizon. The secret behind this beauty is the scattering of light by the tiny molecules of our atmosphere. Sunlight looks white, but it is actually a mixture of all the colours of the rainbow bundled together. Blue light is scattered the most by air molecules, which is why the daytime sky appears blue from every direction. At sunset, the light must travel through much more air, so the blue is scattered away and only the warm reds and oranges survive to reach our eyes. The human eye, with its adjustable lens and light-sensitive retina, then turns this flood of colour into the images we remember.

Structure of the Human Eye

The eye works like a camera, focusing light onto a light-sensitive screen (the retina).

Path of light: Cornea → Pupil → Eye lens → Retina → Optic nerve → Brain.

Persistence of Vision

An image continues to remain on the retina for about 1/16 of a second after the object is removed.

  • If still pictures are shown faster than 16 per second, the eye sees them as continuous motion.
  • This is the principle behind movies and animation.

Eye Accommodation (Focusing) (ਅਨੁਕੂਲਣ ਸਮਰੱਥਾ)

The Ciliary Muscles physically change the curvature of the eye lens so we can focus on objects at varying distances.

Looking at Distant Objects:

The ciliary muscles relax → The lens is pulled and becomes THIN → Focal length increases.

Looking at Nearby Objects:

The ciliary muscles contract → The ligaments loosen → The lens bulges and becomes THICK → Focal length decreases.

Near Point: Closest distance of distinct vision is 25 cm.
Far Point: Furthest distance is Infinity.

Presbyopia & Cataract

  • Presbyopia: with age the near point recedes and accommodation weakens. Corrected with bifocal lenses (concave upper part for distance, convex lower part for reading).
  • Cataract: the eye lens becomes cloudy/milky, blurring vision. Corrected by surgery (lens replacement), not by spectacles.

Defects of Vision Mechanics

Why do these lenses work? Here is the actual physics:

Myopia Correction Mechanics Hypermetropia Correction Mechanics
Image forms IN FRONT of the retina (eyeball too long). Image forms BEHIND the retina (eyeball too short).
Concave Lens: Diverges light rays before they hit the eye lens. This pushes the focal point backward onto the retina. Convex Lens: Converges light rays early. This accelerates focusing, pulling the focal point forward onto the retina.

Dispersion of Light through a Prism

A glass prism splits white light into its seven colours — VIBGYOR (Violet, Indigo, Blue, Green, Yellow, Orange, Red). This band of colours is a spectrum.

Remember: Red bends the LEAST (longest wavelength); Violet bends the MOST (shortest wavelength).

Recombination of White Light

Isaac Newton showed that the seven colours can be recombined back into white light using a second, inverted prism.

  • First prism: white light → spectrum (disperses).
  • Second (inverted) prism: spectrum → white light (recombines).
  • This proves that white light is a mixture of seven colours.

Formation of a Rainbow (ਸਤਰੰਗੀ ਪੀਂਘ)

Interactive Sequencer: Build the process of a rainbow forming inside a water droplet.

1. Refraction & Dispersion: Sunlight enters the water droplet, slows down, and splits into 7 colors.
2. Total Internal Reflection: The light hits the back wall of the water drop and bounces inside instead of passing through.
3. Refraction (Again): The colors exit the drop, reaching our eyes as a rainbow (always opposite to the sun).

Atmospheric Refraction

Light from the Sun and stars bends as it passes through layers of air of continuously changing density.

  • Advance sunrise & delayed sunset: we see the Sun about 2 minutes before actual sunrise and 2 minutes after actual sunset.
  • Oval Sun: the Sun appears flattened (oval) at sunrise and sunset.
  • The apparent position of the Sun is slightly higher than its true position.

Atmospheric Refraction: Why Planets Don't Twinkle

Stars twinkle due to Atmospheric Refraction. The atmosphere constantly shifts, bending starlight back and forth because stars are incredibly far away and act as tiny "point sources" of light.

So why don't planets twinkle?

Planets are much closer to Earth. Instead of being a single point of light, they act as an extended source (a collection of many point sources). While one point of light on the planet might dim due to the atmosphere, another point gets brighter. These changes cancel each other out, resulting in a steady, non-twinkling shine.

Scattering & The Tyndall Effect (ਟਿੰਡਲ ਪ੍ਰਭਾਵ)

Tyndall Effect: The scattering of a light beam by colloidal particles, making its path visible.

Atmospheric Phenomena

  • Blue Sky: Fine air particles strongly scatter short wavelengths (blue) in all directions.
  • Red Danger Signals: Red has the longest wavelength. It is scattered the least by fog, allowing it to reach our eyes directly over long distances.
  • Space is Dark: In outer space, there is no atmosphere and no particles. Therefore, no scattering occurs.

Why the Sky is Blue & Sunset is Red

  • Blue sky: air molecules scatter the shorter (blue) wavelengths most, so blue reaches us from all directions.
  • Red sunrise/sunset: light travels through more air, blue is scattered away, and only the longer red/orange wavelengths reach our eyes.
  • Dark space: with no atmosphere there is no scattering, so the sky looks black to astronauts.

Why Danger Signals Are Red

Red light has the longest wavelength, so it is scattered the least by fog, smoke and dust.

  • It can travel the greatest distance without being scattered away.
  • That is why STOP signals, brake lights and warning signs are red — they stay visible from far away.
